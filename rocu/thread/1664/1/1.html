The Word of the Day for August 09 is:


acolyte \AK-uh-lyte\ noun

1 : one who assists a member of the clergy in a liturgical service by performing minor duties
*2 : one who attends or assists : follower

Example sentence:
The lawyer arrived with one of her acolytes, an eager young attorney who looked at her with obvious admiration.

Did you know?
Follow the etymological path of &quot;acolyte&quot; back far enough and you&#39;ll arrive at &quot;keleuthos,&quot; a Greek noun that means &quot;path&quot; and that is itself the parent of &quot;akolouthos,&quot; an adjective that means &quot;following.&quot; &quot;Akolouthos&quot; traveled from Greek, leaving offspring in Medieval Latin and Anglo-French, and its descendant, &quot;acolyte,&quot; emerged in English in the 14th century. Originally, it was exclusively a term for a person who assisted a priest at Mass, but by the 19th century, &quot;acolyte&quot; had acquired additional meanings, among them &quot;attendant body, satellite&quot; (a meaning used in astronomy) and &quot;attendant insect&quot; (a zoological sense), as well as the general meaning &quot;assistant&quot; or &quot;sidekick.&quot;

The Word of the Day for August 10 is:

anodyne \\AN-uh-dyne\\ adjective

1 : serving to alleviate pain
*2 : not likely to offend or arouse tensions : innocuous

Example sentence:
Afraid of seeming overly critical, the new coach would only offer a few anodyne suggestions.

Did you know?
&quot;Anodyne&quot; came to English via Latin from Greek &quot;anōdynos&quot; (&quot;without pain&quot;), and it has been used as both an adjective and a noun (&quot;something that relieves pain&quot;) since the 16th century. It has sometimes been used of things that dull or lull the senses and render painful experiences less so. Edmund Burke used it this way, for example, in 1790 when he referred to flattery as an &quot;anodyne draft of oblivion&quot; that renders one (in this particular case, the deposed king Louis XVI) forgetful of the flatterer&#39;s true feelings. In the 1930s, a newer second sense began appearing in our vocabulary. Now, in addition to describing things that dull pain, &quot;anodyne&quot; can also refer to that which doesn&#39;t cause discomfort in the first place.

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

The Word of the Day for August 11 is:

aerodyne \\AIR-uh-dine\\ noun

: a heavier-than-air aircraft (as an airplane, helicopter, or glider)

Example sentence:
Every summer aerodyne fanciers gather at the Experimental Aircraft Association&#39;s air show in Oshkosh, Wisconsin, to show off home-built aircraft and get a close-up look at classic flying machines.

Did you know?
&quot;I regret the introduction of a new term in the nomenclature of aerodynamics, but ... &#39;flying-machine&#39; is too general and too suggestive of a wing-flapping machine; ... moreover, &#39;aeroplane&#39; is used to denote a soaring machine ...which is not power-driven.&quot; Thus a Canadian engineer named W.R. Turnbull, writing around 1906, acquainted readers of the Physical Review with &quot;aerodyne&quot; (a back-formation from &quot;aerodynamic&quot;). But the term never had adequate propulsion and it foundered. It received a temporary lift in the 1950s when aeronautics pioneer Alexander Lippisch insisted he preferred &quot;aerodyne&quot; for his experimental wingless aircraft, which was &quot;not an airplane but a new concept of flight.&quot; Today, &quot;aerodyne&quot; serves best in contrast to &quot;aerostat,&quot; the term for lighter-than-air aircraft such as balloons and blimps.

The Word of the Day for August 12 is:



Darby and Joan \\dar-bee-and-JOAN\\ noun

: a happily married usually elderly couple

Example sentence:
Mr. and Mrs. Goodman, the village&#39;s Darby and Joan, were often seen walking hand in hand along the beach.

Did you know?
&quot;Old Darby, with Joan by his side, / You&#39;ve often regarded with wonder: / He&#39;s dropsical, she is sore-eyed, / Yet they&#39;re never happy asunder.&quot; Thus ran the lines of a poem published in 1735 under the title &quot;The Joys of Love Never Forgot: A Song.&quot; By the mid-1700s, the elderly couple introduced in that poem had become symbolic of devoted couples. According to some sources, the verse&#39;s author was an amateur poet named Henry Woodfall and its inspiration was a real-life couple: a printer named John Darby (who was Woodfall&#39;s employer) and his wife Joan. Whoever they were originally, a more modern version of the two later appeared in a song titled simply &quot;Darby and Joan,&quot; a collaboration of Frederic Weatherly and James Molloy.

The Word of the Day for August 13 is:


distemper \\dis-TEM-per\\ verb

: to throw out of order

Example sentence:
&quot;Some people are adept at inflicting their moods on others. One sourpuss in a distempered state, with a skill for spraying it around, can bust up an entire happy dinner party.&quot; (Frank Ahrens, Washington Post, March 12, 2001)

Did you know?
If you &quot;temper&quot; something, you soften or dilute it by mixing in something else. You might, for example, temper wine with water or temper judgment with mercy. But what if you add the wrong thing and just end up with a big mess? That&#39;s the general idea behind &quot;distemper,&quot; which came to English in the 14th century from Late Latin &quot;distemperare&quot; (&quot;to mix badly&quot;). Nowadays, we often use the participial form &quot;distempered&quot; to refer to a mood that is affected by negative feelings. There&#39;s also the noun &quot;distemper,&quot; which can mean &quot;bad humor or temper&quot; or &quot;a serious virus disease of dogs.&quot; Another noun &quot;distemper&quot; refers to a painting process in which pigments are mixed with a glutinous substances, like egg yolks or whites.

The Word of the Day for August 14 is:


parvenu \\PAR-vuh-noo\\ noun

: one that has recently or suddenly risen to an unaccustomed position of wealth or power and has not yet gained the prestige, dignity, or manner associated with it

Example sentence:
Washington old-timers viewed the young senator as an upstart parvenu.

Did you know?
French has been generous in providing us with terms for obscure folks who suddenly strike it rich. In addition to &quot;parvenu,&quot; French has loaned us &quot;nouveau riche,&quot; &quot;arriviste,&quot; and &quot;roturier,&quot; all of which can describe a rich person of plebeian origins, especially one who is a bit snobby. Those colorful and slightly disparaging terms for the newly monied clearly show their French heritage, but it may be harder to see the French background of a term Massachusetts locals once used for coastal merchants made rich through the fishing trade: &quot;codfish aristocracy.&quot; &quot;Codfish&quot; comes from Middle English, but &quot;aristocracy&quot; passed into English via Middle French.

The Word of the Day for August 15 is:



captious \\KAP-shuss\\ adjective

1 : marked by an often ill-natured inclination to stress faults and raise objections
*2 : calculated to confuse, entrap, or entangle in argument

Example sentence:
The teacher often deliberately asked captious questions to get the students debating.

Did you know?
If you suspect that &quot;captious&quot; is a relative of &quot;capture&quot; and &quot;captivate,&quot; you&#39;re right. All of those words are related to the Latin verb &quot;capere,&quot; which means &quot;to take.&quot; &quot;Captious&quot; actually comes from &quot;captio,&quot; a Latin offspring of &quot;capere,&quot; which literally means &quot;a taking&quot; but which was also used to mean &quot;a deception&quot; or &quot;a sophistic argument.&quot; Arguments labeled &quot;captious&quot; are likely to capture you in a figurative sense; they often entrap through subtly deceptive reasoning or trifling points. A captious individual is one who you might also dub &quot;hypercritical,&quot; the sort of carping, censorious critic only too ready to point out minor faults or raise objections on trivial grounds.

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

The Word of the Day for August 16 is:

forswear \\for-SWAIR\\ verb

1 : to make a liar of (oneself) under or as if under oath
2 a : to reject or renounce under oath *b : to renounce earnestly
3 : to deny under oath

Example sentence:
Lizette had always enjoyed riding her bike to work, but after her third accident, she forswore bicycle riding in the city and purchased a subway pass.

Did you know?
&quot;Forswear&quot; (which is also sometimes spelled &quot;foreswear&quot;) is the modern English equivalent of the Old English &quot;forswerian.&quot; It can suggest denial (&quot;[Thou] would&#39;st forswear thy own hand and seal&quot; — John Arbuthnot, John Bull) or perjury (&quot;Is it the interest of any man . . . to lie, forswear himself, indulge hatred, seek desperate revenge, or do murder?&quot; — Charles Dickens, American Notes). But in current use, it most often has to do with giving something up, as in &quot;the warring parties agreed to forswear violence&quot; and &quot;she refused to forswear her principles.&quot; The word &quot;abjure&quot; is often used as a synonym of &quot;forswear,&quot; though with less emphasis on the suggestion of perjury or betrayal of the beliefs that one holds dear.

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

The Word of the Day for August 17 is:



fustian \\FUSS-chun\\ noun
1 : a strong cotton and linen fabric
*2 : highflown or affected writing or speech; broadly : anything highflown or affected in style

Example sentence:
The book is just more fustian from another author who is a pretentious bore writing to impress himself.

Did you know?
&quot;Fustian&quot; has been used in English for a kind of cloth since the 13th century, but it didn&#39;t acquire its highflown sense until at least three centuries later. One of the earliest known uses of the &quot;pretentious writing or speech&quot; sense occurs in Christopher Marlowe&#39;s play Doctor Faustus when Wagner says, &quot;Let thy left eye be diametarily [sic] fixed upon my right heel, with quasi vestigiis nostris insistere,&quot; and the clown replies, &quot;God forgive me, he speaks Dutch fustian.&quot; The precise origins of the word &quot;fustian&quot; aren&#39;t clear. English picked it up from Anglo-French, which adopted it from Medieval Latin, but its roots beyond that point are a subject of some dispute.

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.
