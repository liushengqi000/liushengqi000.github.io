anthophilous \\an-THAH-fuh-lus\\ adjective

: feeding upon or living among flowers

Example sentence:
A 1998 study in Tasmania showed that anthophilous parrots are among the major visitors to the flowers of the eucalyptus — and among this important tree&#39;s most effective cross-pollinators.

Did you know?
Parrots love eucalyptus flowers. That&#39;s because anthophilous birds are naturally attracted to &quot;ornithophilous&quot; flowers — which is to say, flowers that are pollinated by birds. The &quot;-philous&quot; in both of those terms is the combining form that means &quot;loving&quot; (from Greek &quot;-philos&quot;). &quot;Anthophilous&quot; uses the Greek word &quot;anthos,&quot; meaning &quot;flower,&quot; while &quot;ornithophilous&quot; traces back to Greek &quot;ornis,&quot; meaning &quot;bird.&quot; &quot;Ornithophilous&quot; is one of a whole swarm of specialized words that identify flowers in terms of the flower-loving creatures that pollinate them. &quot;Entomophilous&quot; flowers, for example, are pollinated by anthophilous insects, such as bees. There&#39;s even a word specifically for plants that are pollinated by bees: &quot;melittophilous&quot; (from the Greek word &quot;melitta,&quot; meaning &quot;bee&quot;).

ninja \\NIN-juh\\ noun

: a person trained in ancient Japanese martial arts and employed especially for espionage and assassinations

Example sentence:
Ninjas are thought to be able to run faster than ordinary men, scale impossible walls, and endure the severest of pain.

Did you know?
Ninjas may seem mysterious, but the origin of their name is not. The word &quot;ninja&quot; derives from the Japanese characters &quot;nin&quot; and &quot;ja.&quot; &quot;Nin&quot; initially meant &quot;persevere,&quot; but over time it developed the extended meanings &quot;conceal&quot; and &quot;move stealthily.&quot; In Japanese, &quot;ja&quot; means &quot;person.&quot; Ninjas originated in the mountains of Japan over 800 years ago as practitioners of ninjutsu, a martial art sometimes called &quot;the art of stealth&quot; or &quot;the art of invisibility.&quot; They often served as military spies and were trained in disguise, concealment, geography, meteorology, medicine, and also other martial arts. Popular legends still associate them with espionage and assassinations, but modern ninjas are most likely to study ninjutsu to improve their physical fitness and self-defense skills.

reciprocate \\rih-SIP-ruh-kayt\\ verb

1 : to move backward and forward alternately
2 : to give and take mutually
*3 : to make a return for something done or given

Example sentence:
We had a great time at our neighbors&#39; dinner party, and we hope to reciprocate with an invitation to a party of our own soon.

Did you know?
&quot;Reciprocate,&quot; &quot;retaliate,&quot; &quot;requite,&quot; and &quot;return&quot; all mean &quot;to give back,&quot; usually in kind or in quantity. &quot;Reciprocate&quot; implies a mutual or equivalent exchange or a paying back of what one has received (&quot;We reciprocated their hospitality by offering to let them stay for a week&quot;). &quot;Retaliate&quot; usually implies a paying back of an injury or offense in exact kind, often vengefully (&quot;Susan retaliated by spreading equally nasty rumors about them&quot;). &quot;Requite&quot; implies a paying back according to one&#39;s preference, and often not in an equivalent fashion (&quot;He requited her love with cold indifference&quot;). &quot;Return&quot; implies simply a paying or giving back (&quot;returned their call&quot; or &quot;return good for evil&quot;).

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

orphic \\OR-fik\\ adjective

1 capitalized : of or relating to Orpheus or the rites or doctrines ascribed to him
*2 : mystic, oracular
3 : fascinating, entrancing

Example sentence:
&quot;He liked to speak in orphic epigrams that one listened to respectfully but was not expected to dispute.&quot; (Donal Henahan, The New York Times, June 14, 1987)

Did you know?
Orpheus was a hero of Greek mythology who was supposed to possess superhuman musical skills. With his legendary lyre, he was said to be able to make even the rocks and trees dance around. In fact, when his wife Eurydice died, he used his lyre to convince Hades to allow her to return from the underworld (though he ultimately failed in this attempt because he broke his promise to not look back as they were leaving). Later on, according to legend, he was killed at the bidding of Dionysus, and an oracle of Orpheus was established that came to rival the oracle of Apollo at Delphi. Because of the oracle of Orpheus, &quot;orphic&quot; can mean &quot;oracular.&quot; Because of Orpheus&#39; musical powers, &quot;orphic&quot; can mean &quot;entrancing.&quot;

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

turophile \\TOOR-uh-fyle\\ noun

: a connoisseur of cheese : a cheese fancier

Example sentence:
The new store is a turophile&#39;s heaven, offering more than 2,000 varieties of cheese.

Did you know?
Are you stuck on Stilton or gaga for Gouda? Do you crave Camembert? If so, you just might be a turophile, the ultimate cheese lover. From an irregular formation of the Greek word for cheese, &quot;tyros,&quot; plus the English &quot;-phile,&quot; meaning &quot;lover&quot; (itself a descendant of the Greek &quot;-philos,&quot; meaning &quot;loving&quot;), &quot;turophile&quot; first named cheese aficionados as early as 1938. It was in the 1950s, however, that the term really caught the attention of the American public, when Clifton Fadiman (writer, editor, and former radio host) introduced &quot;turophile&quot; to readers of his eloquent musings on the subject of cheese.

fete \\FAYT\\ noun

1 : festival
2 a : a lavish often outdoor entertainment *b : a large elaborate party

Example sentence:
Nigel&#39;s 50th birthday was celebrated with an impressive fete, featuring an abundance of delicious food, an open bar, and endless music and dancing.

Did you know?
&quot;Fete&quot; is a word worth celebrating. It&#39;s been around since Middle English, when it was used in a manuscript to refer to &quot;fetes, spectacles and other worldly vanytees.&quot; Since the 19th century, it has been doing double duty, serving both as a noun (as we&#39;ve used it here) and as a verb meaning &quot;to honor or commemorate with a fete.&quot; You can honor &quot;fete&quot; by remembering that it entered English from Middle French, and that it derives ultimately from the Old French &quot;feste,&quot; meaning &quot;festival&quot; — a root that, not surprisingly, also gave English the word &quot;feast.&quot; (Because of its French ties, you will sometimes see &quot;fete&quot; spelled with a circumflex ( &#710; ) above the first &quot;e,&quot; as that&#39;s how it appears in that language.)

impolitic \\im-PAH-luh-tik\\ adjective

: not politic : contrary to or lacking in policy : unwise, inexpedient

Example sentence:
It was highly impolitic of Mayor Washburn to recommend a mayoral pay increase shortly before he ran for reelection.

Did you know?
&quot;Impolitic&quot; appeared over 400 years ago as an antonym of &quot;politic,&quot; a word that basically means &quot;shrewd,&quot; &quot;sagacious,&quot; or &quot;tactful.&quot; &quotolitic&quot; came to us via Middle French from Latin &quot;politicus.&quot; The Latin word, in turn, came from a Greek word based on &quot;politēs,&quot; meaning &quot;citizen.&quot; &quot;Impolitic&quot; has often been used to refer to action or policy on the part of public figures that is considered politically unwise — from British statesman Edmund Burke&#39;s judicious &quot;the most ... impolitick of all things, unequal taxation&quot; (1797) to a recent description (in U.S. News & World Report, June 20, 2005) of DNC Chairman Howard Dean&#39;s &quot;impolitic dissing of the other side.&quot;

tetchy \\TETCH-ee\\ adjective

: irritably or peevishly sensitive : touchy

Example sentence:
Jeannette knew how tetchy her old friend Doris could sometimes be, so she stifled the urge to tease her about her dirty car.

Did you know?
&quot;Tetchy&quot; is a word that may have been coined by Shakespeare — its first known use in English occurs in Romeo and Juliet (1592). Etymologists are not certain how the word came about, but some have suggested that it derives from &quot;tetch,&quot; an obsolete noun meaning &quot;habit.&quot; The similarity both in meaning and pronunciation to &quot;touchy&quot; might lead you to conclude that &quot;tetchy&quot; is related to it, but there is no conclusive evidence to suggest such a connection. The adjectives &quot;teched&quot; and &quot;tetched,&quot; meaning &quot;mentally unbalanced,&quot; are variations of &quot;touched,&quot; and are probably also unrelated to &quot;tetchy.&quot;

elucidate \\ih-LOO-suh-dayt\\ verb

1 : to make lucid especially by explanation or analysis
*2 : to give a clarifying explanation

Example sentence:
Mary was asked to elucidate a bit for those in the audience who weren&#39;t up-to-date on the latest research.

Did you know?
To &quot;elucidate&quot; is to make something clear that was formerly murky or confusing — and it is perfectly clear how the modern term got that meaning. &quot;Elucidate&quot; traces to the Latin term &quot;lucidus,&quot; which means &quot;lucid.&quot; &quot;Lucidus&quot; in turn descends from the verb &quot;lucēre,&quot; meaning &quot;to shine.&quot; So &quot;elucidating&quot; can be thought of as the figurative equivalent of shining a light on something to make it easier to see. &quot;Lucēre&quot; has also produced other shining offspring in English. Among its descendants are &quot;lucid&quot; itself (which can mean &quot;shining,&quot; &quot;clear-headed,&quot; or &quot;easily understood&quot;), &quot;lucent&quot; (meaning &quot;giving off light&quot; or &quot;easily seen through&quot;), and &quot;translucent&quot; (meaning &quot;partly transparent&quot; or &quot;clear enough for light to pass through&quot;).

rapier \\RAY-pee-er\\ adjective

: extremely sharp or keen

Example sentence:
In the June 1992 issue of Field & Stream magazine, Dave Hughes offered the following fly-fishing advice: &quot;In one fluid motion, lift the rod up and back, and drive it forward with a rapier thrust.&quot;

Did you know?
A rapier is a straight, two-edged sword with a narrow pointed blade, designed especially for thrusting. According to Encyclop&aelig;dia Britannica, &quot;the long rapier was beautifully balanced, excellent in attack, and superb for keeping an opponent at a distance.&quot; The word itself, which we borrowed in the 16th century, is from Middle French &quot;rapiere.&quot; (It has no connection to the word &quot;rape.&quot;) The first time that &quot;rapier&quot; was used as an adjective in its figurative &quot;cutting&quot; sense, it described a smile: &quot;Who can bear a rapier smile? A kiss that dooms the soul to death?&quot; (&quot;The Lover&#39;s Lament&quot; by Sumner Lincoln Fairfield, 1824). Wit was first described as &quot;rapier-like&quot; by an author in 1853, and, these days, the most common use of the adjective &quot;rapier&quot; is to describe wit.
