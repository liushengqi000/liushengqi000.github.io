The Word of the Day for February 20 is:

rictus &#8226; \\RIK-tus\\  &#8226; noun
1 : the gape of a bird&#39;s mouth
2 a : the mouth orifice *b : a gaping grin or grimace

Example sentence:
Randy&#39;s face contorted into a rictus that made his promise to keep Phoebe&#39;s secret seem insincere.

Did you know?
When &quot;rictus&quot; was first used in English in the early 19th century, it referred to the hole formed by the mouth of a bird. Later, it was applied to the mouths of other animals, including humans. In Latin &quot;rictus&quot; means &quot;an open mouth&quot;; it comes from verb &quot;ringi,&quot; which means &quot;to open the mouth.&quot; In English, &quot;rictus&quot; eventually acquired a sense referring to the expression of someone grinning widely, as in Lawrence Durrell&#39;s 1957 novel Justine: &quot;This ghastly rictus gouged out in his taut cheeks.&quot; Although &quot;rictus&quot; might be used to describe the mouth of a laughing or smiling person, it is not related to &quot;risible,&quot; a word associated with laughter. Rather, &quot;risible&quot; descends from Latin &quot;ridēre,&quot; which means &quot;to laugh.&quot;

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

The Word of the Day for February 21 is:

impervious &#8226; \\im-PER-vee-us\\  &#8226; adjective
1 *a : not allowing entrance or passage : impenetrable b : not capable of being damaged or harmed
2 : not capable of being affected or disturbed

Example sentence:
&quot;The church&#39;s thick stone walls seemed to be impervious to noise. . . .&quot; (Pittsburgh Post-Gazette, August 26, 2001)

Did you know?
The English language is far from impervious, and, of course, a great many Latinate terms have entered it throughout its history. &quot;Impervious&quot; is one of the many that broke through in the 17th century. It comes from the Latin &quot;impervius,&quot; which adds the prefix &quot;im-&quot; to &quot;pervius,&quot; meaning &quot;passable&quot; or &quot;penetrable.&quot; &quotervius,&quot; which is also the source of the relatively uncommon English word &quot;pervious,&quot; meaning &quot;accessible&quot; or &quot;permeable,&quot; comes from &quot;per-,&quot; meaning &quot;through,&quot; and &quot;via,&quot; meaning &quot;way.&quot;

The Word of the Day for February 22 is:

pestilence &#8226; \\PES-tuh-lunss\\  &#8226; noun
1 : a contagious or infectious epidemic disease that is virulent and devastating; especially : bubonic plague
*2 : something that is destructive or pernicious

Example sentence:
Bert insisted to his dying day that computers were a pestilence that would destroy human interaction.

Did you know?
In the 14th century, the bubonic and pneumonic plagues ravaged Europe, casting the population into terror and leaving a death toll in the millions. It is easy to see why people of that grim period began using &quot;pestilence,&quot; a derivative of &quot;pestis,&quot; the Latin word for &quot;plague,&quot; to refer to the horrifying diseases wracking the land. Plague and death became common literary themes of the era, and Geoffrey Chaucer used &quot;pestilence&quot; to vivid effect in &quot;The Pardoner&#39;s Tale&quot;:

Ther cam a privee theef men clepeth Deeth,
That in this contree al the peple sleeth,
And with his spere he smoot his herte atwo,
And wente his wey withouten wordes mo.
He hath a thousand slayn this pestilence.

The Word of the Day for February 23 is:

epistolary &#8226; \\ih-PIST-uh-lair-ee\\  &#8226; adjective
1 : of, relating to, or suitable to a letter
*2 : contained in or carried on by letters
3 : written in the form of a series of letters

Example sentence:
Pen pals Walter and Iris kept their epistolary relationship alive for 20 years before finally meeting in person.

Did you know?
&quot;Epistolary&quot; is formed from the noun &quot;epistle,&quot; which refers to a composition written in the form of a letter to a particular person or group. In its original sense, &quot;epistle&quot; refers to one of the 21 letters (such as those from the apostle Paul) found in the New Testament. Dating from the 13th century, &quot;epistle&quot; came to English via Anglo-French and Latin from the Greek noun &quot;epistolē,&quot; meaning &quot;message&quot; or &quot;letter.&quot; &quot;Epistolē,&quot; in turn, came from the verb &quot;epistellein,&quot; meaning &quot;to send to&quot; or &quot;to send from.&quot; &quot;Epistolary&quot; appeared in English four centuries after &quot;epistle&quot; and can be used to describe something that is contained in a letter (as in &quot;epistolary greetings&quot;) or composed of letters (as in &quot;an epistolary novel&quot;).

The Word of the Day for February 24 is:

snickersnee &#8226; \\SNIK-er-snee\\  &#8226; noun
: a large knife

Example sentence:
&quot;The Lord High Executioner in The Mikado is someone who couldn&#39;t bring himself to execute a fly with a newspaper let alone a fellow human being with a razor-sharp snickersnee.&quot; (Canberra Times, November 30, 2003)

Did you know?
Back when pirates were swashbuckling around the seven seas, someone who got into &quot;steake or snye&quot; was engaging in cut-and-thrust sword and dagger fighting. &quot;Steake or snye,&quot; which came from a Dutch term meaning &quot;to thrust or cut,&quot; was eventually modified into &quot;snick or snee,&quot; but the meaning of the phrase remained the same. By around 1775, the phrase had been compressed into the single word &quot;snickersnee,&quot; which was used both as a verb for the act of such fighting and as a noun naming the knife used in such clashes.

The Word of the Day for February 25 is:

lambent &#8226; \\LAM-bunt\\  &#8226; adjective
1 : playing lightly on or over a surface : flickering
*2 : softly bright or radiant
3 : marked by lightness or brilliance especially of expression

Example sentence:
On a warm, clear night, Roger and Theresa strolled through the park beneath the lambent glow of the moon.

Did you know?
Fire is frequently associated with lapping or licking imagery: flames are often described as &quot;tongues&quot; that &quot;lick.&quot; &quot;Lambent,&quot; which first appeared in English in the 17th century, is a part of this tradition, coming from &quot;lambens,&quot; the present participle of the Latin verb &quot;lambere,&quot; meaning &quot;to lick.&quot; In its earliest uses, &quot;lambent&quot; meant &quot;playing lightly over a surface,&quot; &quot;gliding over,&quot; or &quot;flickering.&quot; These uses were usually applied to flames or light, and by way of that association, the term eventually came to be used to describe things with a radiant or brilliant glow, as Alexander Pope used it in his 1717 poem &quot;Eloisa to Abelard&quot;: &quot;Those smiling eyes, attemp&#39;ring ev&#39;ry ray, Shone sweetly lambent with celestial day.&quot;

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence

The Word of the Day for February 26 is:

maunder &#8226; \\MAWN-der\\  &#8226; verb 语无伦次的说。。闲逛
1 : chiefly British : grumble
2 : to wander slowly and idly
*3 : to speak indistinctly or disconnectedly

Example sentence:
John had a tendency to maunder on about things of little interest, so we often tried to limit our conversations with him.

Did you know?
&quot;Maunder&quot; looks a lot like &quot;meander,&quot; and that&#39;s not all the two words have in common—both mean &quot;to wander aimlessly,&quot; either physically or in speech. Some critics have suggested that while &quot;meander&quot; can describe a person&#39;s verbal and physical rambling, in addition to the wanderings of things like paths and streams, &quot;maunder&quot; should be limited to wandering words. The problem with that reasoning is that &quot;maunder&quot; has been used of the physical movements of people since 1775, whereas &quot;meander&quot; didn&#39;t acquire that use until 1831. These days, &quot;meander&quot; tends to be the more common choice, although &quot;maunder&quot; does continue to turn up in both applications.

The Word of the Day for February 27 is:

septentrional &#8226; \\sep-TEN-tree-uh-nul\\  &#8226; adjective
: northern

Example sentence:
My septentrional cousins were impressed by the tall stately palm that grows in our suburban Florida front yard.

Did you know?
What does the word &quot;septentrional&quot; have in common with the month of September and the Big Dipper? The Latin word &quot;septem,&quot; meaning &quot;seven,&quot; provides the connection. &quot;Septem&quot; is an ancestor of both &quot;septentrional&quot; and &quot;September&quot; (the ninth month now, but in the earliest Roman calendar, the seventh month). We picked up &quot;septentrional&quot; from &quot;septentriones,&quot; a Latin word used to refer to the seven principal stars of either of two prominent constellations of the northern sky: Ursa Major (Latin for &quot;Greater Bear&quot;) or Ursa Minor (&quot;Lesser Bear&quot;)—also known as the Big Dipper and the Little Dipper, respectively. &quot;Septentriones&quot; in turn derives from &quot;septem&quot; plus &quot;triones,&quot; meaning &quot;plowing oxen.&quot;

The Word of the Day for February 28 is:

panoptic &#8226; \\pan-OP-tik\\  &#8226; adjective
: being or presenting a comprehensive or panoramic view

Example sentence:
When tourists reach the top of New York City&#39;s Empire State Building, they are dazzled by the panoptic view of the Big Apple.

Did you know?
The establishment of &quot;panoptic&quot; in the English language can be attributed to two inventions known as panopticons. The more well-known panopticon was conceived by the English philosopher Jeremy Bentham in 1787. Bentham&#39;s panopticon was a circular prison with cells arranged around a central tower from which guards could see the inmates at all times. The other panopticon, also created in the 18th century, was a device containing pictures of attractions, such as European capitals, that people viewed through an opening. Considering the views that both inventions gave, it is not hard to see why &quot;panoptic&quot; (a word derived from Greek &quot;panoptēs,&quot; meaning &quot;all-seeing&quot;) was being used by the early 19th century.

The Word of the Day for March 1 is:

quintessence &#8226; \\kwin-TESS-unss\\  &#8226; noun
1 : the fifth and highest element in ancient and medieval philosophy that permeates all nature and is the substance composing the celestial bodies
*2 : the essence of a thing in its purest and most concentrated form
3 : the most typical example or representative

Example sentence:
&quot;Do not shorten the morning by getting up late; look upon it as the quintessence of life, as to a certain extent sacred.&quot; (Arthur Schopenhauer, Counsels and Maxims)

Did you know?
Long ago, when people believed that the earth was made up of four elements—earth, air, fire, and water—they thought the stars and planets were made up of yet another element. In the Middle Ages, people called this element by its Medieval Latin name, &quot;quinta essentia,&quot; literally, &quot;fifth essence.&quot; Our forebears believed the quinta essentia was essential to all kinds of matter, and if they could somehow isolate it, it would cure all disease. We have since given up on that idea, but we kept &quot;quintessence,&quot; the offspring of &quot;quinta essentia,&quot; as a word for the purest essence of a thing. Modern physicists have given &quot;quintessence&quot; a new twist—they use it for a form of so-called &quot;dark energy,&quot; which is believed to make up 70 percent of the universe.
