Chronology: House Majority Leader DeLay

I selected some useful infomation from an article provided by THE ASSOCIATED PRESS
(The full passage is available at newyorktimes online)


Some key events in the career of House Majority Leader Tom DeLay.

1984: Elected to represent the 22nd District of Texas in the U.S. House of Representatives.

1994: Elected majority whip.

November 2002: Re-elected to the House.

2003: Elected majority leader without opposition.

September 2004: Grand jurors in Texas indict three DeLay associates Jim Ellis, John Colyandro, and Warren RoBold in an investigation of alleged illegal corporate contributions to a political action committee associated with him. The investigation involved the alleged use of corporate funds to aid Republican candidates for the Texas legislature in the 2002 elections.

September-October 2004: DeLay is admonished by the House ethics committee on three separate issues. The committee chastised DeLay for offering to support the House candidacy of Michigan Republican Rep. Nick Smith&#39;s son in return for the lawmaker&#39;s vote for a Medicare prescription drug benefit. The panel said DeLay created the appearance of linking political donations to a legislative favor, and that he had improperly sought the Federal Aviation Administration&#39;s intervention in a Texas political dispute.

March 2005: Media reports spur Democrats to question DeLay&#39;s relationship with lobbyist Jack Abramoff, who is under federal investigation. Delay has asked the House ethics committee to review allegations that Abramoff or his clients paid some of DeLay&#39;s overseas travel expenses. DeLay has denied knowing that the expenses were paid by Abramoff.

April 2005: House Republicans scrap controversial new ethics committee rules passed earlier in the year that would have made it harder to proceed with an ethics investigation. Democrats charged the rules were meant to protect DeLay. (Go to Article)

September 2005: Ellis and Colyandro are indicted on additional felony charges of violating Texas election law and criminal conspiracy to violate election law for their role in 2002 legislative races. (Go to Article)

Oct 3, 2005: A grand jury in Texas issued a second indictment against Representative Tom DeLay, accusing the Texas Republican and two aides of money laundering in a $190,000 transaction that prosecutors have described as a violation of the state&#39;s ban on the use of corporate money in local election campaigns. (Go to Article)

a successor

Roy Blunt, this is the new guy recently elected to assume the position of Delay, he is now the second most powerful man in the house.

some biography here, from new york times

Mr. Blunt, 55, a political protégé of John Ashcroft, the former attorney general and senator, grew up on a dairy farm in southwest Missouri in a political family; his father was a state representative. That tradition has continued; today, his son Matt is the governor of Missouri. Before coming to Congress, Mr. Blunt was the secretary of state in Missouri, and also spent four years as president of his alma mater, Southwest Baptist University in Bolivar, Mo.

He came to Washington in 1997. Two years later, as he was entering his second term, Mr. DeLay, then the whip, appointed him chief deputy whip. He took over the whip&#39;s job in January 2003, when Mr. DeLay became the House majority leader. Though Mr. Blunt has been a staunch defender of Mr. DeLay, Republicans say that the two are not especially close, and that relations were strained after Mr. Blunt installed his own pick - Representative Eric Cantor, Republican of Virginia - as chief deputy whip, over several close allies of Mr. DeLay.

Mr. Delay&#39;s defence

At a news conference in his office, Mr. DeLay described the veteran Democratic prosecutor who brought the indictment - Ronnie Earle, the Travis County district attorney - as a &quot;partisan fanatic&quot; leading a &quot;coordinated, premeditated campaign of political retribution.&quot; Mr. DeLay said he had done nothing wrong.

&quot;I have violated no law, no regulation, no rule of the House,&quot; Mr. DeLay, who is in his 11th term, told reporters, adding: &quot;My defense in this case will not be technical or legalistic; it will be categorical and absolute. I am innocent.&quot;

&quot;It may be a witch hunt, but it is a huge problem,&quot; Representative Zach Wamp, Republican of Tennessee, said of the conspiracy charge. &quot;He will probably be exonerated in the long term, but that is a long time.&quot;

For years, first as the House majority whip and then the majority leader, Mr. DeLay has been an aggressive partisan as well as a proponent of socially conservative ideas. When others were urging that Republicans drop the idea of impeaching President Bill Clinton in 1998, Mr. DeLay almost single-handedly kept up the pressure that led to the House impeachment vote.

Mr. DeLay has been a linchpin of Republican success over the past decade, since playing a role in the Republican takeover of the House in 1994. He is often called the Hammer in print for his hard-nosed approach, though it is not a nickname anyone uses with Mr. DeLay himself.
