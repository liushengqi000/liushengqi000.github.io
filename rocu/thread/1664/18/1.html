The Word of the Day for February 01 is:
abulia \\ay-BOO-lee-uh\\ noun

: abnormal lack of ability to act or to make decisions

Example sentence:
&quot;Since his college graduation, my son seems to be suffering from abulia &#151; he just can&#39;t decide what he wants to do next,&quot; sighed Philip.

Did you know?
&quot;I must have a prodigious quantity of mind,&quot; Mark Twain once wrote. &quot;It takes me as much as a week, sometimes, to make it up.&quot; The indecision Twain laments is fairly common; only when inability to make decisions reaches an abnormal level does it have an uncommon name: &quot;abulia.&quot; The English term we use today comes from a New Latin word that combines the prefix &quot;a-,&quot; meaning &quot;without,&quot; with the Greek word &quot;boulē,&quot; meaning &quot;will.&quot; &quot;Abulia&quot; can refer to the kind of generalized indecision that makes it impossible to choose what flavor ice cream you want, though it was created to name a severe medical disorder that can render a person nearly inert.

The Word of the Day for February 04 is:

querulous \\KWAIR-yuh-luss\\ adjective

1 : habitually complaining
*2 : fretful, whining

Example sentence:
&quot;Georgia, I&#39;m tired of waiting,&quot; complained Grandfather in a querulous voice, his peevish expression revealing unmistakable irritation at the girl&#39;s dawdling.

Did you know?
English speakers have tagged fearful whiners &quot;querulous&quot; since late medieval times. The Middle English form of the word, &quot;querelose,&quot; was an adaptation of the Latin adjective, &quot;querulus,&quot; which in turn evolved from the Latin verb &quot;queri,&quot; meaning &quot;to complain.&quot; &quot;Queri&quot; is also an ancestor of the English words &quot;quarrel&quot; and &quot;quarrelsome,&quot; but it isn&#39;t an ancestor of the noun &quot;query&quot; (meaning &quot;question&quot;). No need to complain that we&#39;re being coy; we&#39;re happy to let you know that &quot;query&quot; descends from the Latin verb &quot;quaerere,&quot; meaning &quot;to ask.&quot;

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

The Word of the Day for February 05 is:

deference \\DEF-uh-runss\\ noun

: respect and esteem due a superior or an elder; also : affected or ingratiating regard for another&#39;s wishes

Example sentence:
&quot;In deference to our visitors from Brazil,&quot; the host said, &quot;the ceremony will be conducted in both English and Portuguese.&quot;

Did you know?
We need to be very specific when we tell you that &quot;deference&quot; and &quot;defer&quot; both derive from the Latin &quot;deferre,&quot; which means &quot;to bring down&quot; or &quot;to carry away.&quot; You might also have heard that &quot;defer&quot; traces to the Latin &quot;differre,&quot; which means &quot;to postpone&quot; or &quot;to differ.&quot; So which root is right? Both are. That&#39;s because English has two verbs, or homographs, spelled &quot;defer.&quot; One means &quot;to submit or delegate to another&quot; (as in &quot;I defer to your greater expertise&quot;). That&#39;s the one that is closely related to &quot;deference&quot; and that comes from &quot;deferre.&quot; The other means &quot;put off or delay&quot; (as in &quot;we decided to defer the decision until next month&quot;); that second &quot;defer&quot; derives from &quot;differre.&quot;

The Word of the Day for February 6 is:

velar \\VEE-ler\\  adjective
*1 : formed with the back of the tongue touching or near the soft palate
2 : of, forming, or relating to a velum and especially the soft palate

Example sentence:
Anglo-Norman scribes used &quot;k&quot; instead of the Old English &quot;c&quot; to represent the velar /k/ sound, and thus today we have &quot;king&quot; and &quot;kind&quot; instead of &quot;cing&quot; and &quot;cind.&quot;

Did you know?
&quot;Velar&quot; is ultimately derived from Latin &quot;velum&quot; (meaning &quot;curtain&quot; or &quot;veil&quot;), which was itself adopted into English by way of New Latin as a word for the soft palate (the fold at the back of the hard palate that partially separates the mouth from the pharynx). &quot;Velar&quot; is used by phonologists to refer to the position of the tongue in relation to the soft palate when making certain sounds. Other terms for what phonologists refer to as &quot;places of articulation&quot; are &quot;palatal&quot; (tongue against the roof of the mouth), &quot;dental&quot; (tongue against the upper teeth), and &quot;alveolar&quot; (tongue against the inner surface of the gums of the upper front teeth).

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

Despite its archaic, literary ring, &quot;oftentimes&quot; is quite alive today. In fact, it seems to be more popular even now than it was thirty years or so ago. Nor is &quot;oftentimes&quot; confined to writing—it appears frequently in quoted speech. &quot;Oftentimes&quot; was first used in the 14th century (the same century that gave us &quot;often&quot;), and its meaning hasn&#39;t changed—as meanings oftentimes will—in all that time. It was formed as an extension of its slightly older synonym &quot;ofttimes.&quot; Today &quot;ofttimes&quot; is less common, but &quot;oft&quot; (which comes from Old English and also means &quot;often&quot; or &quot;frequently&quot;) is popular in combination with past participles, as in &quot;oft-praised.&quot;

The Word of the Day for February 8 is:

fissile \\FISS-ul\\  adjective
1 : capable of being split or divided in the direction of the grain or along natural planes of cleavage
*2 : capable of undergoing fission

Example sentence:
The only fissile material that occurs in usable amounts in nature is uranium-235.

Did you know?
When scientists first used &quot;fissile&quot; back in the 1600s, the notion of splitting the nucleus of an atom would have seemed far-fetched indeed. In those days, people thought that atoms were the smallest particles of matter that existed and therefore could not be split. &quot;Fissile&quot; (which can be traced back to Latin &quot;findere,&quot; meaning &quot;to split&quot;) was used in reference to things like rocks. When we hear about &quot;fissile materials&quot; today, the reference is usually to nuclear fission: the splitting of an atomic nucleus that releases a huge amount of energy. But there is still a place in our language for the original sense of &quot;fissile&quot; (and for the noun &quot;fissility,&quot; meaning &quot;the quality of being fissile&quot;). A geologist, for example, might refer to slate as &quot;fissile.&quot;

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

The Word of the Day for February 9 is:

kanban \\KAHN-bahn\\  noun
: a manufacturing strategy wherein parts are produced or delivered only as needed : just-in-time

Example sentence:
&quot;To stay competitive,&quot; Rob said, &quot;we need to reduce our manufacturing costs by switching to a kanban system.&quot;

Did you know?
Toyota Motor Company is credited with developing the kanban system of manufacturing, which takes its name from the Japanese word for &quot;sign&quot; or &quot;placard.&quot; In the kanban system, each shipment of parts used in making a product comes with a &quot;kanban,&quot; or sign. When the parts are nearly exhausted, the sign is sent to suppliers, who ship new ones to the assembly line. In the early 1980s, &quot;kanban&quot; became a buzzword in the American business community—offering a perfect example of how languages often reflect larger societal trends . . . and how trading partners often trade more than durable goods!
