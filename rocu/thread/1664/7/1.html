The Word of the Day for October 09 is:

piebald \\ PYE-bawld \\ adjective

*1 : composed of incongruous parts
2 : of different colors; especially : spotted or blotched with black and white

Example sentence:
Lee&#39;s self-proclaimed &quot;experimental novel&quot; was a piebald accumulation of random stories woven together with the thinnest of narrative threads.

Did you know?
To many people, the species Pica pica is nothing but a pest — and a pest those noisy black-and-white birds, better known as magpies, may be. But the Latin root &quot;pica&quot; that was adopted for their name isn&#39;t a linguistic nuisance; it played an important role in the development of &quot;piebald.&quot; The &quot;pie&quot; of &quot;piebald&quot; (&quot;pie&quot; is another name for a magpie) derives from &quot;pica,&quot; Latin for &quot;magpie,&quot; and &quot;bald&quot; has the meaning &quot;marked with white.&quot; Knowing those two facts surely makes the origin of &quot;piebald&quot; black-and-white (so to speak).

The Word of the Day for October 10 is:

diatribe \\DYE-uh-trybe\\ noun

*1 : a bitter and abusive speech or writing
2 : ironical or satirical criticism

Example sentence:
The columnist wrote a ruthless diatribe condemning people who talk on cell phones while driving.

Did you know?
Ancient Greek philosophers liked to while away the hours in rational contemplation and intellectual discussion. Their fondness for waxing philosophical is reflected in the Greek noun &quot;diatribē,&quot; meaning either &quot;pastime&quot; or &quot;discourse.&quot; That noun passed into Latin as &quot;diatriba,&quot; which was in turn adapted to &quot;diatribe&quot; by 16th-century English speakers. In its earliest English use, &quot;diatribe&quot; meant simply &quot;a prolonged discourse,&quot; but that sense has become obsolete. &quot;Diatribe&quot; has also seen use as the name of a specific type of philosophical discourse — satirical sermons directed against an object of disapproval — that was introduced in the 3rd century B.C. by Greek philosopher Bion of Borysthenes. Today, however, the term is usually applied broadly to any biting or abusive denunciation.

The Word of the Day for October 11 is:

harangue \\huh-RANG\\ noun

1 : a speech addressed to a public assembly
*2 : a ranting speech or writing
3 : lecture

Example sentence:
The comedian&#39;s stand-up act consisted mostly of sharp harangues against celebrities and the media.

Did you know?
In Old Italian, the noun &quot;aringo&quot; referred to a public assembly, the verb &quot;aringare&quot; meant &quot;to speak in public,&quot; and the noun &quot;aringa&quot; referred to a public speech. &quot;Aringa&quot; was borrowed into Middle French as &quot;arenge,&quot; and it is from this form that we get our noun &quot;harangue,&quot; which made its first appearance in English in the 16th century. Perhaps due to the bombastic or exasperated nature of some public speeches, the term quickly developed an added sense referring to a speech or writing in the style of a rant (though the word &quot;rant&quot; is not etymologically related). There is also a verb &quot;harangue,&quot; which refers to the act of making such a speech.

The Word of the Day for October 13 is:

terra firma \\tair-uh-FER-muh\\ noun

: dry land : solid ground

Example sentence:
The passengers on the ocean liner looked forward to setting foot on terra firma at the end of the long voyage.

Did you know?
The etymology of &quot;terra firma&quot; hasn&#39;t been watered down a bit. The phrase comes directly from New Latin, where it literally means &quot;solid land.&quot; When &quot;terra firma&quot; first set foot in English prose in the 17th century, it referred specifically to the dry land of continents or mainlands (as distinct from smaller, more water-bound landforms, such as islands), or even more specifically, to certain Italian mainland territories controlled by Venice. By the end of the 17th century, the broader sense of &quot;terra firma&quot; (any dry land) had also established footing. That sense remains firmly established to this day. Figurative use (&quot;a theory built on the terra firma of facts,&quot; for example) is also common. The older senses, however, have since crumbled away.

The Word of the Day for October 14 is:

tintinnabulation \\tin-tuh-nab-yuh-LAY-shun\\ noun

1 : the ringing or sounding of bells
*2 : a jingling or tinkling sound as if of bells

Example sentence:
The best man&#39;s toast to the bride and groom ended with the customary tintinnabulation of a hundred clinking champagne glasses.

Did you know?
If &quot;tintinnabulation&quot; rings a bell, that may be because it traces to a Latin interpretation of the sound a ringing bell makes. Our English word derives from &quot;tintinnabulum,&quot; the Latin word for &quot;bell.&quot; That Latin word, in turn, comes from the verb &quot;tintinnare,&quot; which means &quot;to ring, clang, or jingle.&quot; Like the English terms &quot;ting&quot; and &quot;tinkle,&quot; &quot;tintinnare&quot; originated with a vocal imitation of the sound associated with it — that is, it is onomatopoeic. Edgar Allan Poe celebrates the sonic overtones of &quot;tintinnabulation&quot; in his poem &quot;The Bells,&quot; which includes lines about &quot;the tintinnabulation that so musically wells / From the bells, bells, bells, bells, / Bells, bells, bells — / From the jingling and the tinkling of the bells.&quot;

The Word of the Day for October 16 is:

lexicographer \\lek-suh-KAH-gruh-fer\\ noun

: an author or editor of a dictionary

Example sentence:
The great lexicographer Noah Webster, who wrote the first authoritative dictionary of American English, was born on October 16, 1758.

Did you know?
Happy Dictionary Day! We&#39;re celebrating with a look at a word that is dear to our hearts: &quot;lexicographer.&quot; The ancient Greeks were some of the earliest makers of dictionaries; they used them mainly to catalog obsolete terms from their rich literary past. To create a word for writers of dictionaries, the Greeks sensibly attached the suffix &quot;-graphos,&quot; meaning &quot;writer,&quot; to &quot;lexikon,&quot; meaning &quot;dictionary,&quot; to form &quot;lexikographos,&quot; the direct ancestor of the English &quot;lexicographer.&quot; &quot;Lexikon,&quot; which itself descends from the Greek &quot;lexis&quot; (meaning &quot;word&quot; or &quot;speech&quot;), also gave us &quot;lexicon,&quot; which can mean either &quot;dictionary&quot; or &quot;the vocabulary of a language, speaker, or subject.&quot;

The Word of the Day for October 17 is:

pomaceous \\poh-MAY-shuss\\ adjective

1 : of or relating to apples
*2 : resembling a pome

Example sentence:
&quot;Her face was looking papery and translucent... but she still had nice legs, with the pomaceous calves of a Pittsburgh girl.&quot; (Michael Chabon, The New Yorker, April 1990)

Did you know?
&quotomaceous&quot; was first planted in the English language by physician Edward Baynard when, in 1706, he advised, &quot;Apples and pomaceous Juices, are the greatest Pectorals.&quot; (&quotectoral&quot; is now a rarely used word for a food that helps digestion.) Since then, &quot;pomaceous&quot; has mainly been sown by botanists and poets. The word, which is ultimately derived from Late Latin &quot;pomum&quot; (meaning &quot;apple&quot;), was originally used of apples and things relating to apples, but later it was also applied to things that look like pears. (Pears, like apples, belong to the pome family.)

The Word of the Day for October 18 is:

expropriate \\ek-SPROH-pree-ayt\\ verb

1 : to deprive of possession or proprietary rights
*2 : to transfer (the property of another) to one&#39;s own possession

Example sentence:
When Maria went home, we expropriated her pens and extra paper to finish the group project.

Did you know?
If you guessed that &quot;expropriate&quot; has something in common with the verb &quot;appropriate,&quot; you&#39;re right. Both words ultimately derive from the Latin adjective &quot;proprius,&quot; meaning &quot;own.&quot; &quot;Expropriate&quot; came to us by way of the Medieval Latin verb &quot;expropriare,&quot; itself from Latin &quot;ex-&quot; (&quot;out of&quot; or &quot;from&quot;) and &quot;proprius.&quot; &quot;Appropriate&quot; descends from Late Latin &quot;appropriare,&quot; which joins &quot;proprius&quot; and Latin &quot;ad-&quot; (&quot;to&quot; or &quot;toward&quot;). Both the verb &quot;appropriate&quot; (&quot;to take possession of&quot; or &quot;to set aside for a particular use&quot;) and the adjective &quot;appropriate&quot; (&quot;fitting&quot; or &quot;suitable&quot;) have been with us since the 15th century, and &quot;expropriate&quot; has been a part of the language since at least 1611. Other &quot;proprius&quot; descendants in English include &quot;proper&quot; and &quot;property.&quot;

The Word of the Day for October 20 is:

bouleversement \\bool-vair-suh-MAHNG (the &quot;NG&quot; is not pronounced, but the vowel is nasalized)\\ noun

*1 : reversal
2 : a violent disturbance : disorder

Example sentence:
The economist pointed out that, in a bouleversement of housing trends, more people are purchasing homes now, whereas rentals have dropped off.

Did you know?
English picked up &quot;bouleversement&quot; from French in the latter part of the 18th century (it ultimately traces to Middle French &quot;boule,&quot; meaning &quot;ball,&quot; and &quot;verser,&quot; meaning &quot;to overturn&quot;), and while not very common, it has steadily remained in use since that time. F. Scott Fitzgerald, for one, used it in his 1920 novel This Side of Paradise: &quot;For the second time in his life Amory had had a complete bouleversement and was hurrying into line with his generation.&quot; Both Fitzgerald&#39;s use and our own example sentence suggest the idea of turning something around, but some usage of &quot;bouleversement&quot; dispenses with this notion and instead implies a general kind of upheaval or dramatic change, as in a revolution.

The Word of the Day for October 21 is:

provenience \\pruh-VEE-nee-unss\\ noun

: origin, source

Example sentence:
Its exact provenience is shrouded in mystery, but some people think the Hope diamond was cut from a gem stolen from the French crown jewels in 1792.

Did you know?
Did you suspect that &quot;provenience&quot; and &quot;provenance&quot; originate from the same source? You&#39;re right; they&#39;re parent and child. &quotrovenance&quot; is the older of the two. It has been used to mean &quot;origin&quot; in English since at least the 1780s, when it was borrowed from French. Its linguistic grandfather is the French verb &quot;provenir,&quot; meaning &quot;to come forth, originate.&quot; The French verb, in turn, derives from Latin &quot;provenire,&quot; a composite of &quot;pro-&quot; (meaning &quot;forth&quot;) and &quot;venire&quot; (meaning &quot;come&quot;). &quotrovenience&quot; is a chip off the old block, originating as a modification of &quot;provenance&quot; about 100 years after its parent debuted in English texts. The source of the extra syllable in &quot;provenience&quot; is most likely &quot;proveniens,&quot; a participle of &quot;provenire.&quot; (The similar Latin participle &quot;conveniens&quot; gave us &quot;convenience,&quot; another &quot;venire&quot; derivative.)
