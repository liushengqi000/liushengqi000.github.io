The Word of the Day for September 08 is:

gormandize \\GOR-mun-dyze\\ verb

: to eat gluttonously or ravenously

Example sentence:
Lady Baleforth watched in horror as Lord Hoggwood gormandized the hors d&#39;oeuvres, polishing off the entire lot before any of the other guests even arrived.

Did you know?
&quot;Gormandize&quot; entered English in the mid-1500s as a modification of &quot;gourmand,&quot; a term borrowed from the French that served as a synonym for &quot;glutton.&quot; The meanings of both &quot;gourmand&quot; and &quot;gormandize&quot; were clearly disparaging until the 19th century, when &quot;gourmet&quot; came into use to refer to a connoisseur of food and drink. Since then, the meaning of &quot;gourmand&quot; has softened, so that it now suggests someone who likes good food in large quantities but not in a way that is disgusting or gluttonous. &quot;Gormandize&quot; still carries such negative connotations, but it does also imply that a big eater has a discriminating palate as well as a generous appetite.

The Word of the Day for September 09 is:

thaumaturgy \\THAW-muh-ter-jee\\ noun

: the performance of miracles; specifically : magic

Example sentence:
In J.K. Rowling&#39;s debut novel, Harry Potter and the Sorcerer&#39;s Stone, Harry learns to perform feats of thaumaturgy at Hogwarts School of Witchcraft and Wizardry.

Did you know?
The magic of &quot;thaumaturgy&quot; is miraculous. The word, from a Greek word meaning &quot;miracle working,&quot; is applicable to any performance of miracles, especially by incantation. It can also be used of things that merely seem miraculous and unexplainable, like the thaumaturgy of a motion picture&#39;s illusions (aka &quot;movie magic&quot;), or the thaumaturgy at work in an athletic team&#39;s &quot;miracle&quot; comeback. In addition to &quot;thaumaturgy,&quot; we also have &quot;thaumaturge&quot; and &quot;thaumaturgist,&quot; both of which mean &quot;a performer of miracles&quot; or &quot;a magician,&quot; and the adjective &quot;thaumaturgic,&quot; meaning &quot;performing miracles&quot; or &quot;of, relating to, or dependent on thaumaturgy.&quot;

The Word of the Day for September 10 is:

odious \\OH-dee-us\\ adjective

: arousing or deserving hatred or repugnance : hateful

Example sentence:
Martin was an odious person: cruel, violent, and deceitful, willing to do anything to anyone to gain the wealth and power he craved.

Did you know?
&quot;Odious&quot; has been with us since the days of Middle English. We borrowed it from Anglo-French, which in turn had taken it from Latin &quot;odiosus.&quot; The Latin adjective came from the noun &quot;odium,&quot; meaning &quot;hatred.&quot; &quot;Odium&quot; is also an ancestor of the English verb &quot;annoy&quot; (another word that came to Middle English via Anglo-French). And, at the beginning of the 17th century, &quot;odium&quot; entered English in its unaltered form, giving us a noun meaning &quot;hatred&quot; or &quot;disgrace&quot; (as in &quot;ideas that have incurred much odium&quot;).

The Word of the Day for September 11 is:

panjandrum \\pan-JAN-drum\\ noun

: a powerful personage or pretentious official

Example sentence:
After years of expanding his vocabulary and perfecting his word play strategies, Uncle Ira considers himself to be a panjandrum in the world of SCRABBLE&reg; players.

Did you know?
&quotanjandrum&quot; looks like it might be a combination of Latin and Greek roots, but in fact it is a nonsense word coined by British actor and playwright Samuel Foote around 1755. According to the Oxford English Dictionary, Foote made up a line of gibberish to &quot;test the memory of his fellow actor Charles Macklin, who had asserted that he could repeat anything after hearing it once.&quot; Foote&#39;s made-up line was, &quot;And there were present the Picninnies, and the Joblillies, and the Garyulies and the Grand Panjandrum himself, with the little round button at the top.&quot; Some 75 years after this, Foote&#39;s passage appeared in a book of stories for children by the Anglo-Irish writer Maria Edgeworth. It took another quarter century before English speakers actually incorporated &quot;panjandrum&quot; into their general vocabulary.

The Word of the Day for September 12 is:

apophasis \\uh-PAH-fuh-sis\\ noun

: the raising of an issue by claiming not to mention it

Example sentence:
&quot;And I won&#39;t even mention my opponent&#39;s dismal record on environmental issues,&quot; said the candidate, using apophasis to take a jab at her rival.

Did you know?
Apophasis is a sly debater&#39;s trick, a way of sneaking an issue into the discussion while maintaining plausible deniability. It should come as no surprise, then, that the roots of &quot;apophasis&quot; lie in the concept of denial — the word was adopted into English from Late Latin, where it means &quot;repudiation,&quot; and derives from the Greek &quot;apophanai,&quot; meaning &quot;to deny.&quot; (&quot;Apophanai,&quot; in turn, comes from &quot;apo-,&quot; meaning &quot;away from&quot; or &quot;off,&quot; and &quot;phanai,&quot; meaning &quot;to say.&quot;) This particular rhetorical stunt is also known by the labels &quot;preterition&quot; and &quot;paraleipsis&quot; (which is a Greek word for &quot;omission&quot;), but those words are rarer than &quot;apophasis.&quot; Incidentally, don&#39;t confuse &quot;apophasis&quot; with &quot;apophysis&quot;; the latter is a scientific word for an expanded or projecting part of an organism.

The Word of the Day for September 13 is:

assay \\a-SAY\\ verb

1 : try, attempt
2 *a : to analyze (as an ore) for one or more specific components b : to judge the worth of : estimate

Example sentence:
A certified laboratory will assay the samples of gold and silver to determine their levels of purity.

Did you know?
Usage experts warn against confusing the verbs &quot;assay&quot; and &quot;essay.&quot; Some confusion shouldn&#39;t be surprising, since the two words look alike and derive from the same root, the Middle French &quot;essai,&quot; meaning &quot;test&quot; or &quot;effort&quot; (a root that in turn comes from the Late Latin &quot;exagium,&quot; meaning &quot;act of weighing&quot;). At one time, the two terms were synonyms, sharing the meaning &quot;try&quot; or &quot;attempt,&quot; but many modern usage commentators recommend that you differentiate the two words, using &quot;essay&quot; when you mean &quot;to try or attempt&quot; (as in &quot;he will essay a dramatic role for the first time&quot;) and &quot;assay&quot; to mean &quot;to test or evaluate&quot; (as in &quot;the blood was assayed to detect the presence of the antibody&quot;).

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

The Word of the Day for September 14 is:

prodigious \\pruh-DIJ-us\\ adjective

1 : exciting amazement or wonder
*2 : extraordinary in bulk, quantity, or degree : enormous

Example sentence:
Artisans began the prodigious task of restoring the beautiful mosaics just days after they were destroyed in the earthquake.

Did you know?
&quotrodigious,&quot; &quot;monstrous,&quot; &quot;tremendous,&quot; and &quot;stupendous&quot; all mean extremely impressive. &quotrodigious&quot; suggests something marvelous or extraordinary, whereas &quot;monstrous&quot; implies that something is not only large but also ugly or deformed. &quot;Tremendous&quot; and &quot;stupendous&quot; both imply a power, the former to terrify or awe, the latter to stun or astound. &quotrodigious&quot; and the related noun &quot;prodigy&quot; derive from the Latin &quot;prodigium,&quot; meaning &quot;omen&quot; or &quot;monster&quot;; at one time, both words were used in English to refer to portents, or omens, but these senses are now considered obsolete.

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

The Word of the Day for September 15 is:

cannibalize \\KAN-uh-buh-lyze\\ verb

1 : to take parts from a machine for use in building or repairing another machine
*2 : to take sales away from an existing product by selling or being sold as a similar but new product usually from the same manufacturer
3 : to practice cannibalism

Example sentence:
The band&#39;s concert CD did not cannibalize sales of the full-length album as some people expected.

Did you know?
During World War II, military personnel often used salvageable parts from disabled vehicles and aircraft to repair other vehicles and aircraft. This sacrifice of one thing for the sake of another of its kind must have reminded some folks of cannibalism by humans and animals, because the process came to be known as &quot;cannibalizing.&quot; The armed forces of this time were also known to cannibalize — that is, to take away personnel from — units to build up other units. It didn&#39;t take long for this military slang to become civilianized; and since its demobilization, the term has become a popular item served up in business writing and at company meetings to describe the practice of taking sales away from other businesses or even one&#39;s own products.

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

The Word of the Day for September 16 is:

simon-pure \\sye-mun-PYUR\\ adjective

: of untainted purity or integrity; also : pretentiously or hypocritically pure

Example sentence:
Alfred is a simon-pure Republican, rocked in his cradle to the stirring rhythms of G.O.P. speeches, grown to a man sure to vote the party line.

Did you know?
British dramatist and actress Susannah Centlivre (1669-1723) introduced the character of Simon Pure in her 1718 comedy A Bold Stroke for a Wife. In that play, Colonel Fainall wants to marry Anne Lovely, but to do so he must win the consent of Anne&#39;s guardian, a Quaker gentleman named Obadiah Prim. Fainall tries to gain the needed approval by impersonating a Quaker preacher named Simon Pure. Unfortunately for the scheme, the real Simon Pure appears and proves himself to be the genuine article. People adopted the phrase &quot;the real Simon Pure&quot; (which in turn gave rise to the adjective &quot;simon-pure&quot;) from the play to refer to things true or genuine.

The Word of the Day for September 17 is:

execrable \\EK-si-kruh-bul\\ adjective

1 : deserving to be execrated : detestable
*2 : very bad : wretched

Example sentence:
&quot;The room is dirty, the mattress is lumpy, and the heater doesn&#39;t work, and I refuse to spend the night in such execrable conditions!&quot; Elaine informed the motel&#39;s manager.

Did you know?
He or she who is cursed faces execrable conditions. Keep this in mind to remember that &quot;execrable&quot; is a descendant of the Latin verb &quot;exsecrari,&quot; meaning &quot;to put under a curse.&quot; Since its earliest uses in English, beginning in the 14th century, &quot;execrable&quot; has meant &quot;deserving or fit to be execrated,&quot; the reference being to things so abominable as to be worthy of formal denouncement (such as &quot;execrable crimes&quot;). But in the 19th century we lightened it up a bit, and our &quot;indescribably bad&quot; sense has since been applied to everything from roads (&quot;execrable London pavement&quot; — Sir Walter Scott) to food (&quot;The coffee in the station house was... execrable.&quot; — Clarence Day) to, inevitably, the weather (&quot;the execrable weather of the past fortnight&quot; — The [London] Evening Standard).
