The Word of the Day for August 28 is:

morpheme \\MOR-feem\\ noun

: a word or part of a word that contains no smaller unit of meaning

Example sentence:
The word &quot;unloader&quot; includes the morphemes &quot;un-,&quot; &quot;load,&quot; and &quot;-er.&quot;

Did you know?
Morphemes are the indivisible basic units of language, much like the atoms which physicists once assumed were the indivisible units of matter. English speakers borrowed &quot;morpheme&quot; from French &quot;morphème,&quot; which was itself created from the Greek root &quot;morphē,&quot; meaning &quot;form.&quot; The French borrowed &quot;-ème&quot; from their word &quot;phonème,&quot; which, like English &quot;phoneme,&quot; means &quot;a basic unit of speech that distinguishes one utterance from another.&quot; The French suffix and its English equivalent &quot;-eme&quot; are used to create words that refer to distinctive units of language structure. Words formed from &quot;-eme&quot; include &quot;lexeme&quot; (&quot;a meaningful linguistic unit in the vocabulary of a language&quot;), &quot;grapheme&quot; (&quot;a unit of a writing system&quot;), and &quot;toneme&quot; (&quot;a unit of intonation in a language in which variations in tone distinguish meaning&quot;).

The Word of the Day for August 29 is:



canker \\KANG-ker\\ verb

1 : to become infested with erosive or spreading sores
*2 : to corrupt the spirit of
3 : to become corrupted

Example sentence:
&quot;It was evident that their hearts were cankered with discontent.&quot; (Samuel Johnson, Rasselas)

Did you know?
&quot;Canker&quot; is commonly known as the name for a type of spreading sore that eats into the tissue — a use that obviously furnished the verb with both its medical and figurative senses. The word ultimately traces back to Latin &quot;cancer,&quot; which could refer to a crab or a malignant tumor. The Greeks had a similar word, &quot;karkinos,&quot; and according to the Ancient Greek physician Galen the tumor got its name from the way the swollen veins surrounding the affected part resembled a crab&#39;s limbs. &quot;Cancer&quot; was adopted into Old English, becoming &quot;canker&quot; in Middle English and eventually shifting in meaning to become a general term for ulcerations. &quot;Cancer&quot; itself was reintroduced to English later, first as a zodiacal word and then as a medical term.

exiguous \\ig-ZIG-yuh-wus\\ adjective

: excessively scanty : inadequate

Example sentence:
The current evidence supporting her hypothesis is exiguous, but Carla is sure she&#39;ll get convincing results from the next round of experiments.

&quot;Exiguous&quot; is so expansive sounding that you might expect it to mean &quot;extensive&quot; instead of &quot;meager.&quot; Even a scanty glimpse at the word&#39;s etymology will disabuse you of that notion, however. &quot;Exiguous&quot; derives from the Latin &quot;exiguus,&quot; which has the same basic meaning as the modern English term. &quot;Exiguus,&quot; in turn, derives from the Latin verb &quot;exigere,&quot; which is variously translated as &quot;to demand,&quot; &quot;to drive out,&quot; or &quot;to weigh or measure.&quot; The idea of weighing or measuring so precisely as to be parsimonious or petty gave &quot;exiguous&quot; its present sense of inadequacy. Just so we aren&#39;t accused of being skimpy with the details, we should also mention that &quot;exigere&quot; is the parent term underlying other English words including &quot;exact&quot; and &quot;exigent.&quot;

The Word of the Day for September 01 is:



rubric \\ROO-brik\\ noun

*1 : heading, title; also: class, category
2 : a rule especially for the conduct of a liturgical service

Example sentence:
The new magazine includes a section of odd news items that fall under the rubric &quot;Odds & Ends.&quot;

Did you know?
Centuries ago, whenever manuscript writers inserted special instructions or explanations into a book, they put them in red ink to set them off from the black used in the main text. (They used the same practice to highlight saints&#39; names and holy days in calendars, a practice which gave us the term &quot;red-letter day.&quot;) Ultimately, such special headings or comments came to be called &quot;rubrics,&quot; a term that traces back to &quot;ruber,&quot; the Latin word for &quot;red.&quot; While the printing sense remains in use today, &quot;rubric&quot; also has an extended sense referring to any class or category under which something is organized.

The Word of the Day for September 02 is:

copacetic \\koh-puh-SET-ik\\ adjective

: very satisfactory

Example sentence:
The star receiver had openly criticized his coach for benching him last week, but today he told reporters that everything is now copacetic between them.

Did you know?
Theories about the origin of &quot;copacetic&quot; abound. The tap dancer Bill &quot;Bojangles&quot; Robinson believed he had coined the word as a boy in Richmond, Virginia. When patrons of his shoeshine stand would ask, &quot;How&#39;s everything this morning?&quot; he would reply, &quot;Oh jes&#39; copasetic, boss; jes&#39; copasetic.&quot; But the word was current in Southern Black English perhaps as early as 1880, so it seems unlikely that Robinson (born in 1878) could have invented the term. Another explanation is that the word is from the Hebrew phrase &quot;kol be sedher,&quot; meaning &quot;everything is in order.&quot; Possibly it was coined by Harlem blacks working in Jewish businesses. This explanation does not account for the word&#39;s popularity among Southern blacks, however. Ultimately, the origin of &quot;copacetic&quot; remains a mystery.

panacea \\pan-uh-SEE-uh\\ noun

: a remedy for all ills or difficulties : cure-all

Example sentence:
Education reform is sometimes viewed as a panacea for all of society&#39;s problems.

Most likely that&#39;s what the author is doing in a 1625 anatomical treatise, describing &quot;a certaine medicine made of saffron, quick silver, vermilion, antimonie, and certaine sea shels made up in fashion of triangular lozenges,&quot; and calling it a panacea.

The Word of the Day for September 04 is:


shrive \\SHRYVE\\ verb

1 : to administer the sacrament of reconciliation to
*2 : to free from guilt

Example sentence:
Only the knowledge that her son had forgiven her could shrive her of the guilt she felt for leaving him behind when she fled the country all those years ago.

Did you know?
We wouldn&#39;t want to give the history of &quot;shrive&quot; short shrift, so here&#39;s the whole story. It began when the Latin verb &quot;scribere&quot; (meaning &quot;to write&quot;) found its way onto the tongues of certain Germanic peoples, who brought it to Britain in the early Middle Ages. Because it was often used for laying down directions or rules in writing, 8th-century Old English speakers used their form of the term, &quot;scrīfan,&quot; to mean &quot;to prescribe or impose.&quot; The Church adopted &quot;scrīfan&quot; to refer to the act of assigning penance to sinners and, later, to hearing confession and administering absolution. Today, the noun form of &quot;shrive,&quot; &quot;shrift,&quot; makes up half of &quot;short shrift,&quot; a phrase meaning &quot;little or no consideration.&quot; Originally, &quot;short shrift&quot; was the barely adequate time for confession before an execution.

The Word of the Day for September 05 is:

timorous \\TIM-uh-russ\\ adjective

*1 : of a timid disposition : fearful
2 : expressing or suggesting timidity

Example sentence:
Matthew was too timorous to stand up to the no-good electronics salesman who ripped him off.

Did you know?
&quot;Timid&quot; and &quot;timorous&quot; don&#39;t just have similar spellings and meanings; they are etymologically related as well. Both words ultimately derive from the Latin verb &quot;timēre,&quot; meaning &quot;to fear.&quot; The immediate ancestor of &quot;timid&quot; is Latin &quot;timidus&quot; (same meaning as &quot;timid), whereas &quot;timorous&quot; traveled to Middle English by way of the Latin noun &quot;timor&quot; (&quot;fear&quot;) and the Medieval Latin adjective &quot;timorosus.&quot; &quot;Timid&quot; may be the more common of the two words, but &quot;timorous&quot; is older. It first appeared in English in the mid-15th century; &quot;timid&quot; came on the scene a century later. Both words can mean &quot;easily frightened&quot; (as in &quot;a timid mouse&quot; or &quot;a timorous child&quot;) as well as &quot;indicating or characterized by fear&quot; (as in &quot;gave a timid smile&quot; or &quot;took a timorous step forward&quot;).

The Word of the Day for September 07 is:

politesse \\pah-lih-TESS\\ noun

formal politeness : decorousness

Example sentence:
&quot;We rely more and more on technologies such as cell phones and the Internet, where the rules for politesse are still evolving.&quot; (Glamour, February 2001)

Did you know?
Nowadays, no one refers to a &quot;polite looking glass&quot; or &quot;houses polite and in good repair,&quot; but &quot;polite&quot; (or &quot;polit&quot; or &quot;polyt,&quot; as it was spelled in Middle English) originally meant simply &quot;polished&quot; or &quot;clean.&quot; By the early 1700s, &quot;polite&quot; was being used of polished and refined people, and &quot;politeness&quot; had been penned to name the shining quality of such people. &quotolitesse&quot; (a French borrowing) debuted in 1713 in the lines of an English comedy discussing how a man of high society loses all his &quot;politesse with his liberty&quot; when he marries. All three words stem from Latin &quot;polire,&quot; meaning &quot;to polish.&quot; Today we tend to use &quot;politeness&quot; for everyday good manners and reserve &quot;politesse&quot; for more formal courtesies.
