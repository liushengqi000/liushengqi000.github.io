Essay Writing

Because it is a test meant to give colleges an indication of how prepared a student is for college level work, the SAT now includes an essay section. Both the makers of the SAT and the colleges to which scores are sent know how important it is that you have control over standard written English, and the essay section gives them a chance to determine the level of your control. It also gives you an opportunity to show them that you are prepared for your life as a college student, which will involve quite a bit of time spent writing essays!
The task of writing is not as daunting as it may seem. It is a skill, and, like any other skill, it can be practiced. This tutorial is designed to help you isolate and concentrate on the specific skills involved in writing a college essay. These skills include planning your argument, developing your point of view, supporting your position, and doing so in a clear, coherent, and focused discussion that utilizes a varied style. Don&#39;t be overwhelmed. We will guide you through the process, and formulating a clear, coherent argument will become second nature for you!
On the test, you will be presented with a single issue and will be asked to write a short essay in which you develop a point of view on that issue. Your essay will be scored on a scale of 1 to 6, 6 being the highest. An essay given the score of 6 will exhibit a mastery of compositional skills. Skills that the scorers will be paying particular attention to are those that involve grammar, usage, and mechanics; sentence structure; use of language; organization; and development.
This may sound like a lot to ask for, especially in the 25 minutes in which you will be expected to complete your essay, but a crucial point to remember as you begin writing is that what you will write in 25 minutes is a first draft. Don&#39;t spend too much time constructing a precise outline or perfecting each rhetorical nuance, or you will run out of time to finish writing.
Make An Outline

As we have said, on the actual test you will be presented with a single issue. The first thing you will want to do is read through the prompt (the description of the issue and the instructions for writing) and think about the position you would like to develop through the course of your essay. These thoughts are the beginning of your outline.
Again, remember that your outline will be rough, not precise and not terribly thorough. You will probably want to spend about 5 minutes working on it. The purpose of the outline is to give shape to your essay before you begin writing, to give you a map to follow through the writing process. It will be far easier for you to move efficiently and effectively through the essay if, before you begin, you have a sense of your position on the problem or situation (the issue) you are presented with, the buildup of your position, and its resolution or conclusion.
The issue you will be expected to take a position on will not include any specific literary or technical references: it will be something to which you will be able to respond without specialized knowledge. It will be an issue, though, to which there will be at least two clear possible responses. (You can think of your answer as fitting roughly into a &quot;for&quot; or &quot;against&quot; category, as being roughly a &quot;yes&quot; or &quot;no&quot; answer. For any such question, or issue, there is always also the possibility of the balanced, &quot;a little from column A, a little from column B&quot; answer. Knowing that your time is limited, though, you may not want to embroil yourself in too complicated a perspective, lest your essay appear to take no clear position at all.)
When you have decided upon your position, you will want to think about how you will develop it, and how you will make it clear and compelling to your reader. Keep in mind that the development of any position generally involves some acknowledgement of possible counterarguments. Acknowledging the position opposite to your own will help you to clarify and strengthen your position, and will also exhibit your critical thinking skills.
To show that yours is the most persuasive position, you will need to use evidence. This evidence can take the form of examples from your experiences, your observations, or things you have read. What is important is that the examples you use to support your argument are clear to your reader and appropriate to your essay. Make sure that they do, in fact, prove your point, that they are not simply things that the prompt reminded you of and that you thought were interesting.
Finally, you will want to have some sense, before you begin, of how your essay will end, of how you will resolve or conclude your discussion. It helps here to reflect on the examples you have chosen to support your position. Consider any tensions you have introduced into your discussion; acknowledge any potentially problematic or controversial aspects of your perspective. Then, reaffirm your position in light of these tensions. You can think of this as having the structure of an &quot;on the one hand…on the other hand&quot; statement, or as an &quot;although&quot; or &quot;while&quot; claim.
You may have the instinct, once you have decided upon your position, to charge forward with that position full-speed-ahead, looking neither right nor left and heading straight for the final line. While this instinct is natural, the most persuasive prose will be balanced. Relevant, appropriate supporting evidence and an acknowledgement of possible opposition will only strengthen what may already be the passionate intensity of your perspective.
So, the first 5 minutes or so of your time with the essay section should leave you with an understanding of the issue that you have been presented with, a position on that issue, one or two pieces of evidence to support that position, a sense of the possible counterargument(s), and an idea of how you will conclude your essay. This is all to say that you should at this point know the general shape of your essay.
Shaping an Essay

Here we should give a note on what we mean by shape. You may have heard of, practiced, or even been regularly assigned exercises in what is called the five-paragraph essay. This is a standard, fairly formulaic system championed by many as a way to clearly convey a single argument. Its strengths are its reliability and its simplicity: almost any argument can be coherently constructed according to the five-paragraph essay outline.
In case you haven&#39;t yet seen it, or have seen but are not familiar with it, here is the standard five-paragraph essay outline:
I.    Introduction
A.    Introductory Discussion
B.    Thesis Statement
II.    Body Paragraph One
A.    Topic Sentence
B.    Evidence
C.    Commentary
III.    Body Paragraph Two
IV.    Body Paragraph Three
V.    Conclusion
A.    Restatement of Thesis
Each body paragraph follows the same pattern, and the claim of each topic sentence is included within the thesis statement.
The benefits of this system are its reliability and its simplicity. These are also, as you might imagine, its detriments. Depending on this outline is dangerous: your writing can very easily become formulaic. While clarity is always important, the best essays will clearly articulate their positions with more sophistication than this outline generally allows. Remember that what you say should have some impact on how you say it, and so the shape of your essay will, to some degree, be determined by the prompt to which you are asked to respond.
This is not to say that the five-paragraph essay outline is inherently bad, or that its logic is somehow flawed. It is a cautionary reminder that fitting your argument into this system is only one of many ways to present what you have to say. You should also be aware that many graders (for this test, but more importantly for college) will consider the five-paragraph essay to be unoriginal, and that an ability to pull it off only demonstrates that you are a competent writer, not a really good one.
Responding to a Prompt

Now, let&#39;s look at an example of a prompt, and think about how a successful response might take shape. Here is a prompt:
Children have an innate sense of what is right for them. Before the complications of adulthood set in, what one should do is remarkably close to what one would like to do, and parents need to acknowledge and encourage this. Harry Truman appreciated this perspective, having once said, &quot;I have found the best way to give advice to your children is to find out what they want and then advise them to do it.&quot;
Assignment: What is your view on the idea that children are able and should be allowed to choose what to do with themselves and their time? Plan and write an essay in which you develop your point of view on this issue. Support your position with evidence taken from your experiences, observations, or readings.
Now, the first thing you will want to do after reading through the prompt is to gauge your reaction to the question: what position do you naturally tend toward? What answer would you immediately give? It is usually the case that the position you are more strongly in favor of will be easier for you to argue, and that will help you to move quickly through a convincing essay. Remember that you are not being scored according to which answer you give — there is no right answer, as they say — but rather according to how well you argue whichever position you choose.
Take a moment to choose a position, and think about what evidence you might use to support it.
Once you&#39;ve chosen a position, the first step is to construct an outline. Here is an example of a possible outline:
Outline:
I.    My position: Harry Truman is wrong. Children need strict guidance and cannot generally choose the proper course of action.
II.    Support: After having watched a number of cowboy movies, my younger brother was stubbornly determined to be a gunfighter. He did not understand the danger of this lifestyle and did not understand that it was impossible.
III.    Counterclaim: Some may say that since it was impossible, his dream was harmless.
IV.    Response: But he didn&#39;t know it was impossible, and so may have tried to fulfill it by looking for a gun and, perhaps, hurting himself or someone else. Even if he couldn&#39;t find a gun, it would only crush his spirits to chase after what he could never have, and that chase would take time away from the realization of a productive, and realistic, dream.
V.    Conclusion: Children need guidance to help them to develop safe and realistic dreams.
You needn&#39;t by any means take the time to write out full sentences; here, we have given full sentences only for clarity. Remember that the outline is for your own use and benefit — it will not be scored.
This sample outline takes one position, but the opposite position is, of course, just as arguable. What is important is not so much which position you take, but how you support it.
A well-designed prompt will lead to superbly well-written essays arguing equally persuasively from each side of a debate. In our example prompt, those siding in favor of the position might argue that children should be permitted to follow their dreams before having to confront the rude realities of the adult world. Similarly, someone might argue that children are inherently good and it is adults who corrupt them.
But our outline sets out an opposite view. Our outline will lead to an essay that says that adult limits protect children from harm they might do themselves, and perhaps that it is the role of adults to teach social behaviors and values that do not come naturally to a child.
Which answer is the correct one? For the present purposes, it does not matter.
What matters is that you know how to put together a decent argument and express it eloquently. And what matters is that you convey to the reader (grader) that you are aware of the larger themes that are in play.
Does the adult world corrupt the innocence of children? Or do children need to learn from adults? There is truth to both sides. No grader expects your 25 minute essay to wrap up a question that scholars have spent lifetimes studying. But a grader will punish an essay where the writer does not give any sign of getting &quot;the big picture&quot;.
Nothing is more tedious for college professors to grade than narrow, one-dimensional essays that completely miss the point of the exercise. For example, suppose someone responded to the prompt in the following manner:
Kids don&#39;t know what&#39;s good for them. A kid might want to play with a knife, and a knife is dangerous. Harry Truman doesn&#39;t know what he is talking about because it is the adult&#39;s job to keep sharp things away from kids. And kids should not run with scissors either, even though they sometimes want to.
Granted this is an extreme example of a bad response. But notice that it is actually a similar position to the one adopted in our outline (and in the essay below). Its weakness is that the author gives no indication of any sophistication beyond the given example. To the writer, this is an open and shut case that you keep kids away from sharp objects. Period.
It is safe to say that the graders would not consider the essay writer sharp. The author&#39;s position is clear enough, but it is an unsophisticated and poorly expressed position that does not address the broader issues suggested by the prompt.
Again, what is important is not which position you take, but that you take a position and support it. While this outline includes one primary supporting point, your outline may include two supporting points. It never hurts to begin with more evidence than you may need, as long as you do not spend too much time deciding on evidence and do not feel compelled to use all of your evidence if you find that, in writing, you are running short on time.
You might have noticed that this outline includes a counterclaim and a response to that counterclaim. These two things together, sections III and IV in our outline, can function as a single second piece of supporting evidence. While two directly supporting points (section II is one such point) can be just as persuasive, there are argumentative as well as stylistic benefits to giving a counterclaim and response in lieu of a second directly supporting point.
Because the credibility of your argument depends entirely upon your own evidence and your ability to clearly convey that evidence, you, as a writer, face the danger of seeming totally biased or unbalanced. You can avoid this danger by making clear to your reader that you are aware of, and have considered, contrary evidence. However, it is wise to enter the test with a certain amount of flexibility in this regard — the prompt you are given may not be best suited to an argument that includes a counterclaim. Please do not consider our advice here to be a formula, but rather an effort to encourage a working knowledge of the skills needed to write well, and an ability to apply those skills to any situation.
While constructing your outline, remember that, ideally, the following key words will be applied to your argument: clear, coherent, focused, and supported.
1.    Clear: your position is stated directly, is not ambiguous.
2.    Coherent: the paragraphs hold together; no paragraph seems unnecessary or out of place.
3.    Focused: throughout the essay&#39;s development, each paragraph addresses your position.
4.    Supported: body paragraphs avoid being repetitive by including evidence in support of your position.
Writing the Actual Essay

Once the prewriting stage is completed, you will be ready to begin the essay itself. There are three skills that you can practice, which, together, will help you to lead your reader through a smoothly developed point of view. These are the skills of writing an introduction, writing a conclusion, and transitioning from one thought to the next.
The introduction and conclusion are both paragraphs that are particularly emphatic simply due to their position. The introduction is the first thing that your reader will encounter, and so you want to make sure that it functions to comfortably introduce your point of view. As its name betrays, the introduction to an essay is not unlike the introduction of a person. If called upon to introduce a friend to a group of people, you might highlight some of your friend&#39;s more interesting qualities or attributes; you might indicate the context in which you introduce him or her, giving a sense of what about your friend in particular is relevant to this gathering; and, finally, you might make an extra effort to grab the attention of your listeners. This is just what you want to do when introducing an essay.
After your introduction is written, the next thing you will encounter is the need for a smooth transition. Transitions should not generally be particularly emphatic. Between every two thoughts, every two sentences, and especially every two paragraphs, there is a seam. Transitions function to smoothly carry your reader across those seams. As you begin to articulate a new thought, turn back to the preceding thought and make sure that the connection will be clear to your reader, that the progression of your thoughts is coherent.
In the conclusion of your essay, it is helpful to your reader for you to briefly restate your position, but if all you do is restate what you have already said, your conclusion may seem to fall flat. You will also want to attend to the separate though related activity of concluding. Here it often helps to reflect: have you learned anything, recognized anything new through the course of your discussion? How has your discussion been relevant to a larger context in which it exists? There is a balance to maintain here, and it&#39;s important to be careful. You don&#39;t want to shock your reader with an entirely new claim or distract your reader with as yet unmentioned lines of inquiry, but you do want to reflect on the development of your point of view after you have restated it.
Sample Essay

Okay, let&#39;s look at an example essay much better than the paragraph above about sharp objects and running with scissors. Click here for a pop-up with our sample. This essay is long, about 700 words and more polished than a first draft. Nevertheless, we give this example because we feel it has several good qualities which we will point out in the subsequent discussion. The goal you want to accomplish is that even though you address an audience with a wide variety of opinions, you hope that everyone agrees you have accomplished your task of providing a clear, coherent, focused and supported essay.
Take a moment to read through the essay, and try to identify what you think might be some of its strengths in terms of content and style.
Introduction
What has the writer done with the introduction? By the time we have finished reading the paragraph, we know exactly what her position is, clearly and unambiguously. But the opening lines seem at first to paint a different picture, to evoke a sense of the wonder and optimism that Truman&#39;s quote reflects. The author has made it clear that she recognizes the worth and the origin of the claim which she will ultimately argue against, and does so through a statement general enough for her reader to associate with but specific enough to be relevant to the particular position she will develop. In the third sentence, we see the structure &quot;while A is true, B is in some respect more important or relevant here.&quot; This structure allows the writer to acknowledge an opposing claim and to use that opposing claim to make her own argument more compelling.
The Middle Section
Where did the author take the reader after the introduction? The first thing you might notice is that while the writer was expecting to produce three paragraphs, what we have here is four. The second paragraph of the essay, beginning &quot;Children left to their own desires,&quot; was not written into the outline. It was not planned. This is no reason, though, to erase it. The writer might have realized while writing that in order to move smoothly from the introduction to the supporting evidence of her younger brother she would need more than a single sentence: she would need an entire paragraph. This is perfectly fine. Remember that your outline is for your own use, is not scored, and is meant only to help you. There is no way you can perfectly foresee your entire essay before it is written, and you will need to have some flexibility when writing.
Notice how each paragraph begins by acknowledging what the preceding paragraph ended with. The reader is never left to wonder how the writer got from one thought to the next. The transitions are clear, but they are also smooth. The writer doesn&#39;t depend on transitional adverbs, such as also, moreover, or furthermore to make her point. Transitional adverbs can be helpful, but they carry their own danger. They can seem forced or repetitive, and are often used when there is no true, clear transition, as a means to hide the lack of a transition.
Through the end of the fifth paragraph, the writer&#39;s argument has been reasonably compelling. This does not necessarily mean that every reader will agree with the position argued, but even those who do not agree will see that this position is defensible. Now, all that is left is for the writer to close, or conclude, her essay.
Our writer has concluded by taking what may seem to be the opposing point of view into her own. She begins her conclusion by seemingly arguing for the other side. This garners for her a certain amount of argumentative persuasiveness and authorial integrity, since we, as readers, can believe that she has thoroughly and fairly considered the contrary evidence. We may still disagree with her position, but at the same time we recognize that she has a clear and coherent point.
Throughout this essay the writer has played off the opposing view. One caution regarding this strategy is in order: Be sure you don&#39;t present the opposing view in too persuasive a manner. It does you no good to cast your invisible opponent in such a good light that the reader moves over to the other side!
Perhaps then you are saying, &quot;Well, why present any counterevidence at all?&quot; Presenting counterevidence is a good technique when used to anticipate objections that might arise in the reader&#39;s mind as he or she reads. Consider the opening of this paragraph. We anticipated a question that might be in your mind, and by addressing it right now in the text, we hopefully have reassured you that there is a purpose to introducing, and refuting, counterclaims.
Let&#39;s consider a few other successful techniques used in the example essay
A simple example that serves a larger purpose. It is hard to imagine a simpler example than the one chosen by the author of this essay. Nevertheless, the cowboy games of her younger brother serve the author&#39;s purpose well. After the second paragraph it seemed like she might be in trouble. Children can be dangerous and scary? No, not possible, thinks the reader. And then, with the one simple example, the author makes her point. You cannot always get away with an example this simple, but it can be very effective on a &quot;take a position and defend it&quot; task. Notice that the author did not try to impress or intimidate the reader with some obscure and technical example designed to show off substantive knowledge in some domain. That is not what the essay task is about.
Varied syntax The author&#39;s style is not repetitive. She has expressed herself with a healthy variety of sentence structures. She is consistent in her tone and with her voice, but still has a fluid style. Without exceeding her abilities, or challenging the comprehension of the reader, she has demonstrated an ability to construct a variety of complex sentence structures that depart from the basic Subject-Verb-Object construction. (See below for more on sentence structures.)
Echoes of the prompt. The author echoes the prompt throughout the essay while making her points. She does not begin by restating the question from the prompt directly. That is a very predictable way to begin or end the essay, and a way that makes it appear that the writer is just trying to fill lines on the page. Instead, phrases from the entire prompt appear in the body of the essay. In the second paragraph, &quot;complication of the adult world&quot; is directly quoted. In paragraph three, a reference is made to Harry Truman. In the fifth paragraph, a distinction is drawn between what the child wants to do, and what the child should do, and this refers to the initial claim in the prompt. The effect of these echoes from the prompt — and we stress that there are not straight restatements used to open and close the essay — is to reassure the reader that the argument is on point and consistent with the task.
Good vocabulary. The author articulates her position with the use of a broad and advanced vocabulary. Nevertheless, her word choice is always precise and accurate. She does not arbitrarily throw in large words where smaller ones will do, nor does she misuse words. As a writer you should use a vocabulary consistent with your ability to express yourself, and consistent with the purpose of your prose. A &quot;highfalutin&quot; word thrown in here and there will not impress a judge.
Summary

Remember, you are striving to make each of your essays clear, coherent, focused, and supported. For practice, try writing an essay on the sample prompt given above. Take the position that our writer did not take, construct an outline, and compose an essay. Time yourself to make it more like the test situation for which you are practicing.
All that we have said here may seem like an awful lot to think about while you&#39;re writing your essay. Much of it, though, can be so thoroughly practiced before your test date comes that, during your test, it will come naturally to you.
Actually, in this tutorial we have only emphasized structure, style and the process of writing the essay. You should also be aware of technical matters such as grammar, usage, and mechanics. A successful essay will avoid errors with such things as subject/verb agreement (grammar), homonym confusion (usage), and punctuation (mechanics). We have left those details to our grammar tutorial, which we strongly recommend you look at as well.
Take advantage of the Grammar tutorial and the Vocabulary Builder to practice your technical skills, and read often to enhance your vocabulary further and to introduce yourself to various rhetorical styles. Also, test yourself. Practice developing points of view in written form, using supporting evidence. Limit yourself to 25 minutes to ready yourself for the timed situation of the SAT. You will find that practice will help you to hone your critical thinking skills and will strengthen your ability to coherently articulate your own individual perspective on issues that confront you every day!

Any one of us who has ever watched a child at play knows the innocent assurance that children can exhibit. Young enough not to have been faced with the need to have a job or take care of a family, children tend not to consider consequences before they act. Although this can be endearing to watch, it is only endearing when those children are acting under the supervision of a person old enough to keep them out of harm&#39;s way. The thought of a child doing exactly what he or she wants to do, innocent and confident but unsupervised, is not at all endearing: it is scary.
Children left to their own desires might not be so frightening if we could depend on them to curb those potentially dangerous desires that they sometimes have, and if we could only be sure that they would think and think twice before trying to realize every dream. But if children were really this responsible, would they not lose a small part of their charm? Wouldn&#39;t many of the &quot;complications of adulthood&quot; then already be mastered? Some of the most charming dreams and desires of children are unrealistic and potentially destructive. These dreams and desires can be charming because the children who have them are protected by adults who ensure that potentially threatening consequences of their foolishness are never realized.
Such scenes of potential destruction are fairly common is my house, where my younger brother, eight years old, enacts regular gunfights with cowboys on the television screen, with the bodies of myself and the rest of my family, or simply with combatants brought to life from thin air by his imagination. He has seen such gunfights in movies and on old television reruns so many times that they are now definitely part of his reality, and he wants nothing more passionately than to grow up to be a gun fighting cowboy. What is not part of his reality, of course, is the blood that any similar gunfight would draw in real life. He doesn&#39;t associate his games with real injury or death. His games are endearing and he is charming because his guns are not real. He doesn&#39;t yet understand that the lifestyle he desires is dangerous and that, since most of the movies he knows are set in a time and place far removed from his modern Seattle suburb, it is also impossible.
Of course he will grow out of this cowboy fantasy. And since we all know that he will grow out of it, many will say, along with Harry Truman, that the best thing for now is to encourage him to do what he wants to do. Encourage him to be a cowboy for as long as he wants to be a cowboy. His dream is impossible and so it is harmless, these people might say, and he may even develop some skills that will be useful to him later in life while seeking this impossible dream.
This permissive point of view is attractive, but ultimately dangerous and an abdication of adult responsibility. While my parents may indulge my brother&#39;s imaginative cowboy play, they must also help him to understand the difference between imagination and reality. As adults, it is their role to ensure that his games do not go too far, that he does not ever wield a real gun as he wields his imaginary ones, and that he learns to accept that his world of school and piano lessons does not function like the Wild West he so adores. I agree that my brother has an innate sense of what he wants to do, but I do not agree that he can be trusted to know what he should do.
Dreams are important for a child, maybe as important as anything else, including homework and music lessons. They help a child to develop a sense of hope, of possibility, and of confidence. I would be the last to discourage dreams. I am the first, though, to encourage safe and realistic dreams. In a child&#39;s mind, anything is possible. Each child deserves the guidance, though, that will teach him or her that anything is possible even within the real world, and even in a safe manner.
