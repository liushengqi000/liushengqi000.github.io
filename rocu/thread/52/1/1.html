Although the number of applications from prospective students decreased slightly this year, overall admission statistics were similar to those of previous years.

As of Wednesday, 7,880 students had applied to Rice, and 1,908, or 24 percent, had been offered admission. Applications will increase to about 7,900 by the time varsity coaches finish recruiting, Dean of Undergraduate Enrollment Julie Browning said. Like last year, the enrollment goal for the class is 720 freshmen. The Admission Office also plans to enroll about 60 transfer students.

Last year, a record 8,110 students applied for admission. Of those, 1,806, or 22 percent, were accepted. About 40 percent of accepted students matriculated at Rice.

This year, Rice extended 831 regular decision admission offers, compared to 616 last year. Regular decision notifications were mailed March 29 to meet the official notification date of April 1.

Between 1,500 and 2,000 applicants were offered spots on the waiting list this year. About 600 students will likely accept the offer, Browning said. The waiting list is used to exactly meet the enrollment goal, and the number of students admitted from the list varies widely from year to year, Browning said. Based on previous yield rates — a measure of how many students accept the offer of admission— it is expected that 710 places in the freshman class will be filled before students are admitted from the waiting list, Browning said.

The 25th-percentile SAT score for admitted students was 1400, and the 75th-percentile score was 1540.

The number of applicants increased in each racial subcategory except white, Browning said. The acceptance rates for black, Hispanic, Native American and foreign national applicants decreased slightly from last year’s, Browning said. The acceptance rate for Asians was similar to last year’s.

The most dramatic decrease in acceptance rate occurred in the multi-racial subcategory. Compared to last year, 79 more multi-racial students applied, but the acceptance rate for these students decreased from 45 percent to 27 percent.

Browning said the increase in minority applicants was partially the result of a fall calling campaign designed to solicit applications. In addition, the Rice Native American Student Association promoted Rice at a science and engineering conference in Alaska targeted at Native Americans.

Rice used race as a factor in its admission decisions for the second consecutive year, Browning said.

“The notion … is [to] have enough representation of various minority groups on your campus so that no one student feels they’re suddenly the voice for their whole race,” Browning said. A 2003 Supreme Court decision in Grutter v. Bollinger superseded the 5th U.S. Circuit Court of Appeals ruling that banned universities from using race in admissions, which had applied to Rice since March 1996. Former President Malcolm Gillis then formed a committee to review whether race should be used as a factor in admissions.

Ensuring no student feels like the sole representative of his racial group is especially difficult because the residential college system spreads students out, Browning said. But one of Rice’s educational goals is for every student to be exposed to others of different races, cultures and perspectives, she said. Willingness to share life experiences is more important than the racial identification box an applicant checks, Browning said.

About 300 fewer Texans applied to Rice this year, while the number of out-of-state applicants stayed about the same. Only 19 percent of Texas applicants were offered admission, while nearly 41 percent of out-of-state applicants were accepted. Besides Texas, the states with the largest numbers of applicants were California, Florida, Maryland and New York. The number of Texas applicants and admitted students will increase when the recruiting season is complete because many varsity student-athletes at Rice are from Texas, Browning said.

The numbers of males and females applying this year were similar, although slightly more males than females were offered admission. Almost 25 percent of the male applicants were offered admission, while 23 percent of female applicants were accepted. The figures are consistent with Rice’s typical gender breakdown — females comprised 48 percent of Rice students in 2003-’04.

Admission rates for each school were similar to past years’ rates. The schools of engineering and natural sciences received the highest numbers of applicants.

                              ------By the Rice Thresher                                                                 
                            (The student newspaper of Rice University)[p:1] [free][/free]
