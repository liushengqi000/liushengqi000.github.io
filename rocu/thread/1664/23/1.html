The Word of the Day for March 21 is:

pernicious \\per-NISH-us\\ adjective

: highly injurious or destructive : deadly

Example sentence:
Farmers stopped using the pesticide after discovering that it had a pernicious effect on some wheat crops.

Did you know?
When the wind blows ill and wicked, and you need a word for the evil that such deadly currents can do, consider &quot;pernicious&quot; or &quot;noxious&quot;; both describe something exceedingly harmful. Of the two, &quot;pernicious&quot; more strongly implies irreparable harm done through evil or insidious corrupting or undermining. &quot;Noxious&quot; is more often used for that which is offensive or injurious to the health of body or mind. The sense of wickedness in those two words is entwined deep in their histories. &quoternicious&quot; comes from the Latin root &quot;nex&quot; (meaning &quot;violent death&quot;), which is related to Latin &quot;noxa&quot; (meaning &quot;harm&quot;), the root of English &quot;noxious.&quot;

The Word of the Day for March 22 is:

chouse \\CHOWSS (&quot;OW&quot; as in &quot;cow&quot;)\\ verb

: cheat, trick

Example sentence:
In Dickens&#39;s A Tale of Two Cities, Mr. Cruncher says, &quot;If I ain&#39;t, what with piety and one blowed thing and another, been choused this last week into as bad luck as ever a poor devil of a honest tradesman met with!&quot;

Did you know?
&quot;You shall chouse him of Horses, Cloaths, and Mony,&quot; wrote John Dryden in his 1662 play Wild Gallant. Dryden was one of the first English writers to use &quot;chouse,&quot; but he wasn&#39;t the last. That term, which may derive from a Turkish word meaning &quot;doorkeeper&quot; or &quot;messenger,&quot; has a rich literary past, appearing in works by Samuel Pepys, Henry Fielding, Sir Walter Scott, and Charles Dickens, among others, but its use dropped off in the 20th century. In fact, English speakers of today may be more familiar with another &quot;chouse,&quot; a verb used in the American West to mean &quot;to drive or herd roughly.&quot; In spite of their identical spellings, though, the two &quot;chouse&quot; homographs are not related (and the origin of the latter is a source of some speculation).

The Word of the Day for March 23 is:

esculent \\ESS-kyuh-lunt\\ adjective

: edible

Example sentence:
Sonia is a chef at The Wild Asparagus, a top-notch restaurant whose claim to fame is that every dish on the menu features an esculent native plant.

Did you know?
One appealing thing about &quot;esculent&quot; is that this word, which comes from the Latin for food (&quot;esca&quot;), has been around for 375 years. If we give you just one more tidbit of etymology — that &quot;esca&quot; is from Latin &quot;edere,&quot; which means &quot;to eat&quot; — can you pick which of the following words is NOT related to &quot;esculent&quot;?

comestible           edacious           edible           escalade           escarole           obese

&quot;Comestible&quot; (meaning &quot;edible&quot;), &quot;edacious&quot; (meaning &quot;voracious&quot;), &quot;edible,&quot; &quot;escarole&quot; (a type of salad green), and &quot;obese&quot; are all descendants of &quot;edere.&quot; Only &quot;escalade&quot; (meaning &quot;an act of scaling walls&quot;) doesn&#39;t belong on the list. It descends from the Italian &quot;scalare,&quot; meaning &quot;to scale.&quot;

The Word of the Day for March 24 is:

macaroni \\mak-uh-ROH-nee\\ noun

1 : pasta made from semolina and shaped in the form of slender tubes
2 *a : a member of a class of traveled young Englishmen of the late 18th and early 19th centuries who affected foreign ways b : an affected young man : fop

Example sentence:
&quot;If he...talks about London and Lord March, and White&#39;s, and Almack&#39;s, with the air of a macaroni, I don&#39;t think we need like him much the less.&quot; (William Makepeace Thackeray, The Virginians)

Did you know?
As you may have suspected, the &quot;macaroni&quot; in the song &quot;Yankee Doodle&quot; is not the familiar food. The feather in Yankee Doodle&#39;s cap apparently makes him a macaroni in the now rare &quot;fop&quot; or &quot;dandy&quot; sense. The sense appears to have originated with a club established in London by a group of young, well-traveled Englishmen in the 1760s. The founders prided themselves on their appearance, sense of style, and manners, and they chose the name Macaroni Club to indicate their worldliness. Because macaroni was, at the time, a new and rather exotic food in England, the name was meant to demonstrate how stylish the club&#39;s members were. The members were themselves called &quot;macaronis,&quot; and eventually &quot;macaroni&quot; became synonymous with &quot;dandy&quot; and &quot;fop.&quot;

The Word of the Day for March 25 is:

doggerel \\DOG-uh-rul\\ adjective

: loosely styled and irregular in measure especially for burlesque or comic effect; also : marked by triviality or inferiority

Example sentence:
Murray disparaged the new poetry anthology by saying that it contained little more than doggerel verse.

Did you know?
&quot;Doggerel&quot; comes from the Middle English word &quot;dogerel&quot; of the same meaning. Beyond that, etymologists aren&#39;t certain of the word&#39;s history. They think &quot;dogerel&quot; is probably the diminutive of the Middle English word &quot;dogge&quot; (meaning &quot;dog&quot;), though the connection between man&#39;s best friend and bad poetry is unclear. &quot;Doggerel&quot; is often used as a noun, too, meaning &quot;doggerel verse.&quot; Stephen Crane uses the noun form in this excerpt from The Red Badge of Courage: &quot;As he marched he sang a bit of doggerel in a high and quavering voice: &#39;Sing a song &#39;a vic&#39;try, / A pocketful &#39;a bullets, / Five an&#39; twenty dead men / Baked in a — pie.&#39;&quot;

The Word of the Day for March 26 is:

vulcanize \\VUL-kuh-nyze\\ verb

: to treat rubber or rubberlike material chemically to give useful properties (as elasticity or strength)

Example sentence:
The American inventor Charles Goodyear is said to have discovered how to vulcanize rubber quite accidentally.

Did you know?
&quot;Vulcanize&quot; sounds like something Spock from Star Trek might do, but the explanation behind this word has more to do with ancient mythology than it does with science fiction. Vulcanization involves heating rubber in combination with sulfur. The Roman god Vulcan (whose Greek counterpart is Hephaestus) was the god of fire and of skills that used fire, such as metalworking. So when Charles Goodyear discovered that high heat would result in stronger rubber, he called the process &quot;vulcanization&quot; after the god of fire. Goodyear stumbled upon the idea in 1839 and acquired a patent for it in 1844, but the labels &quot;vulcanize&quot; and &quot;vulcanization&quot; didn&#39;t appear in print until 1846.

The Word of the Day for March 27 is:

G&#246;tterd&#228;mmerung \\gher-ter-DEM-uh-roong\\ noun

: a collapse (as of a society or regime) marked by catastrophic violence and disorder; broadly : downfall

Example sentence:
Although we all hoped for a peaceful transfer of power, we feared the conflict would instead end in a chaotic G&#246;tterd&#228;mmerung.

Did you know?
Norse mythology specified that the destruction of the world would be preceded by a cataclysmic final battle between the good and evil gods, resulting in the heroic deaths of all the &quot;good guys.&quot; The German word for this earth-shattering last battle was &quot;G&#246;tterd&#228;mmerung.&quot; Literally, &quot;G&#246;tterd&#228;mmerung&quot; means &quot;twilight of the gods.&quot; (&quot;G&#246;tter&quot; is the plural of &quot;Gott,&quot; meaning &quot;god,&quot; and &quot;D&#228;mmerung&quot; means &quot;twilight.&quot;) Figuratively, the term is extended to situations of world-altering destruction marked by extreme chaos and violence. In the 19th century, the German composer Richard Wagner brought attention to the word &quot;G&#246;tterd&#228;mmerung&quot; when he chose it as the title of the last opera of his cycle Der Ring des Nibelungen, and by the early 20th century, the word had entered English.

The Word of the Day for March 28 is:

rigmarole \\RIG-uh-muh-rol\\ noun

1 : confused or meaningless talk
*2 : a complex and sometimes ritualistic procedu

Example sentence:
Instead of going through the rigmarole of filling out deposit and withdrawal slips, waiting in line, and explaining her transactions to the bank teller, Lauren usually finds it simpler to use the ATM.

Did you know?
In the Middle Ages, the term &quot;Rageman&quot; or &quot;Ragman&quot; referred to a game in which a player randomly selected a string attached to a roll of verses and read the selected verse. The roll was called a Ragman roll after a fictional king purported to be the author of the verses. By the 16th century, &quot;ragman&quot; and &quot;ragman roll&quot; were being used figuratively to mean &quot;a list or catalog.&quot; Both terms fell out of written use, but &quot;ragman roll&quot; persisted in speech, and in the 18th century it resurfaced in writing as &quot;rigmarole,&quot; with the meaning &quot;a succession of confused, meaningless, or foolish statements.&quot; Only in the last century did &quot;rigmarole&quot; (also spelled &quot;rigamarole,&quot; reflecting its common pronunciation) acquire its most recent sense, &quot;a complex and ritualistic procedure.&quot;

The Word of the Day for March 29 is:

redux \\ree-DUKS\\ adjective

: brought back

Example sentence:
&quotolka dots, stripes and florals with ... a distinct &#39;50s and &#39;60s redux aura ... are a trend we&#39;re glad to see returning.&quot; (Jaimee Rose, The Arizona Republic, July 15, 2005)

Did you know?
In Latin, &quot;redux&quot; (from the verb &quot;reducere,&quot; meaning &quot;to lead back&quot;) could mean &quot;brought back&quot; or &quot;bringing back.&quot; The Romans used &quot;redux&quot; as an epithet for the Goddess Fortuna with its &quot;bringing back&quot; meaning; Fortuna Redux was &quot;one who brings another safely home.&quot; But it was the &quot;brought back&quot; meaning that made its way into English. &quot;Redux&quot; belongs to a small class of English adjectives that are always used postpositively — that is, they always follow the words they modify. &quot;Redux&quot; has a history of showing up in titles of English works, such as John Dryden&#39;s Astraea Redux (a poem &quot;on the happy restoration and return of his sacred majesty, Charles the Second&quot;), Anthony Trollope&#39;s Phineas Redux, and John Updike&#39;s Rabbit Redux.

The Word of the Day for March 30 is:

diadem \\DYE-uh-dem\\ noun

*1 : crown; specifically : a royal headband
2 : something that adorns like a crown

Example sentence:
The queens of ancient Greece preferred simple elegance, opting for a single diadem of gold or even leaves rather than more ornate crowns.

Did you know?
The ancient rulers of Persia wore headbands of linen, silk, or wool tied at the back as a mark of royalty. Alexander the Great and other Greek rulers took a fancy to the headgear and adopted it as a symbol of their royal authority, naming the narrow band &quot;diadēma,&quot; which comes from the Greek &quot;dia-,&quot; meaning &quot;through, across,&quot; and &quot;dein,&quot; meaning &quot;to bind.&quot; Over time, diadems became more elaborate, shifting from cloth to precious metals, adding gems and ornaments, and finally expanding from a mere headband into the flashier crowns of medieval times.
