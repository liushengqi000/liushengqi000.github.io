advertent \\ad-VER-tunt\\ adjective

: giving attention : heedful

Example sentence:
Marcia listened to everything I said with an advertent _expression on her face, then proceeded to tell me, point by point, why she disagreed with me.

Did you know?
You may be thinking that &quot;advertent&quot; should mean &quot;intentional.&quot; After all, &quot;inadvertent&quot; means &quot;unintentional.&quot; Take away the negative prefix &quot;in-&quot; and you&#39;re left with that word&#39;s opposite, right? If this is your line of thought, you&#39;re not entirely off base; the two words (which both entered English in the 17th century and derive from Latin &quot;advertere,&quot; meaning &quot;to turn the mind or attention&quot;) are in fact closely linked. But &quot;inadvertent&quot; has another, older meaning: &quot;inattentive&quot; or &quot;not focusing the mind on a matter.&quot; The established meaning of &quot;advertent&quot; falls opposite that older sense of &quot;inadvertent.&quot; Does this mean that &quot;advertent&quot; never means &quot;intentional&quot;? Not exactly. We have seen some evidence of this use, but it&#39;s not yet well enough established to be entered in our dictionaries.

gratuitous \\gruh-TOO-uh-tuss\\ adjective

1 : done or provided without recompense : free
*2 : not called for by the circumstances : unwarranted

Example sentence:
The critics decried the gratuitous violence of the new film, declaring that the movie would have been better with more plot and less savagery.

Did you know?
Like &quot;gratitude,&quot; &quot;grace,&quot; and &quot;congratulate,&quot; &quot;gratuitous&quot; is a descendant of the Latin word &quot;gratus,&quot; which means &quot;pleasing&quot; or &quot;grateful.&quot; When &quot;gratuitous&quot; was first used in the middle of the 17th century, it meant &quot;free&quot; or &quot;given without return benefit or compensation.&quot; The extended meaning &quot;done without good reason&quot; or &quot;unwarranted&quot; came about just a few decades later, perhaps from the belief held by some people that one should not give something without getting something in return. Today, that extended meaning is the more common sense, often used in such phrases as &quot;a gratuitous insult,&quot; or to describe elements of a story that are not relevant to the plot.

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

kowtow \\KOW-tow (&quot;ow&quot; as in &quot;cow&quot; in both syllables)\\ verb

*1 : to show obsequious deference : fawn
2 : to kneel and touch the forehead to the ground in token of homage, worship, or deep respect

Example sentence:
Martin kowtows to our boss every chance he gets, complimenting him on his suits and offering favors.

Did you know?
&quot;Kowtow&quot; originated as a noun referring to the act of kneeling and touching one&#39;s head to the ground as a salute or act of worship to a revered authority. In traditional China this ritual was performed by commoners making requests to the local magistrate, by the emperor to the shrine of Confucius, or by foreign representatives appearing before the emperor to establish trade relations. (In the late 18th century, some Western nations resisted performing the ritual, which acknowledged the Chinese emperor as the &quot;son of heaven.&quot;) The word &quot;kowtow&quot; derives from Chinese &quot;kòutóu,&quot; formed by combining the verb &quot;kou&quot; (&quot;to knock&quot;) with the noun &quot;tou&quot; (&quot;head&quot;). The noun had arrived in English by 1804, and the earliest evidence for the verb dates from 1826.

onomatopoeia \\ah-nuh-mah-tuh-PEE-uh\\ noun

*1 : the naming of a thing or action by a vocal imitation of the sound associated with it (as buzz, hiss)
2 : the use of words whose sound suggests the sense

Example sentence:
When Blaine said that the bacon was sizzling and the refrigerator was humming, he was using examples of onomatopoeia.

Did you know?
Onomatopoeia came into English via Late Latin, but the word &quot;onomatopoeia&quot; traces back to the Greek &quot;onoma,&quot; meaning &quot;name,&quot; and &quot;poiein,&quot; meaning &quot;to make.&quot; (&quot;Onoma&quot; can be found in such terms as &quot;onomastics,&quot; which refers to the study of proper names and their origins, while &quot;poiein&quot; gave us such words as &quot;poem&quot; and &quot;poet.&quot;) English speakers have only used the word &quot;onomatopoeia&quot; since the late 1500s, but people have been creating words from the sounds heard around them for much longer. In fact, the presence of so many imitative words in language spawned the linguistic bowwow theory, which postulates that language originated in imitation of natural sounds.

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

aficionado \\uh-fish-ee-uh-NAH-doh\\ noun

: a person who likes, knows about, and appreciates a usually fervently pursued interest or activity : devotee

Example sentence:
Dad is a passionate lover of jazz, and the father of three more jazz aficionados.

Did you know?
The affection an aficionado has for his or her favorite subject isn&#39;t merely emotional — it&#39;s also etymological. Back in the early 1800s, English borrowed &quot;aficionado&quot; from the past participle of the Spanish verb &quot;aficionar,&quot; which means &quot;to inspire affection.&quot; That verb comes from the Spanish noun &quot;afición,&quot; meaning &quot;affection.&quot; Both Spanish words trace to the Latin &quot;affectio&quot; (which is also an ancestor of the English word &quot;affection&quot;). &quot;Affectio,&quot; in turn, is from &quot;afficere&quot; (&quot;to influence&quot;), and gave English speakers the noun and verbs &quot;affect.&quot;

hegira \\hih-JYE-ruh\\ noun

: a journey especially when undertaken to escape from a dangerous or undesirable situation : exodus

Example sentence:
To escape the lowering clouds of impending war, Grandmother&#39;s family embarked on a hegira that would carry them far from their native soil.

Did you know?
&quot;A prophet is not without honor, save in his own country.&quot; By the year A.D. 622, the prophet Muhammad had learned that painful lesson. In that year, he was forced to flee his native city, Mecca, to escape persecution from those who rejected his message. Muhammad, the founder of Islam, with a number of his followers migrated to Medina, where they were guaranteed protection by local clans. This event, which traditionally marks the beginning of the Islamic era, is known in Arabic as the &quot;Hijra&quot; — literally, &quot;departure.&quot; That Arabic term passed into Medieval Latin (where it was modified to &quot;Hegira&quot;) and from there it eventually made its way into English. By the mid 18th century, English speakers were using &quot;hegira&quot; for other journeys, too — especially arduous ones.

Brobdingnagian \\brob-ding-NAG-ee-un\\ adjective

: marked by tremendous size

Example sentence:
Our little dog was frightened by the Brobdingnagian proportions of the statues in the park.

Did you know?
In Jonathan Swift&#39;s novel Gulliver&#39;s Travels, Brobdingnag is the name of a land that is populated by a race of human giants &quot;as tall as an ordinary spire steeple.&quot; In Gulliver&#39;s first close-up encounter with the giants, he is attempting to get past a stile of which every step is six feet high, when a group of field-workers approach with strides ten yards long and reaping hooks as large as six scythes. Their voices he at first mistakes for thunder. Swift&#39;s book fired the imagination of the public and within two years of the 1726 publication of the story, people had begun using &quot;Brobdingnagian&quot; to refer to anything of unusually large size. (Swift himself had used &quot;Brobdingnagian&quot; as a noun to refer to the inhabitants of Brobdingnag.)

svelte \\SVELT\\ adjective

1 *a : slender, lithe b : having even and smooth lines : sleek
2 : urbane, suave

Example sentence:
&quot;The bulky, boxy television sets we grew up with are on the way out and svelte, flat-panel displays are turning up in their place.&quot; (Oregon Business, December 1, 2004)

Did you know?
&quot;Svelte&quot; came to us, by way of French, from Italian &quot;svelto,&quot; which itself comes from the Italian verb &quot;svellere,&quot; meaning &quot;to pluck out&quot; or &quot;to pull or stretch out.&quot; In English &quot;svelte&quot; has been used since the early 19th century to describe a slender or smooth appearance; or, as one might say, an appearance plucked of all that is rough or extraneous and smoothed out. By the 20th century, people had stretched its meaning to suggest an urbane or suave nature — as poet Ezra Pound did to describe &quot;svelte Verona,&quot; a city he visited in north Italy.
