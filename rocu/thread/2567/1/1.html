US-JAPAN-S. KOREA-CHINA RELATIONS
ALL FACE RISING RISK FOR 2006...
And then there’s Sharon, Iran, etc. elsewhere


SUMMARY:  first, on the Middle East situation, we have nothing to add at this point to a developing story except to note that Prime Minister Sharon has long been seen in the “Nixon goes to China” mold...that is, as the old-time hard-liner uniquely placed to force through real compromise in domestic israeli politics, while remaining sufficiently terrifying to Palestinian political leaders and Islamic radicals that they, too, may come up with an enforceable deal. Now all that is at risk, even if Sharon’s newly-created political party can win a “sympathy vote” in upcoming elections.

On Iran, the US increasingly is in the position of having it’s views marginalized by the disconnect between what the IAEA clearly feels is accurate intel about Iran’s nuclear weapons ambitions, and the pre-war Iraq WMD fiasco.  Teheran takes advantage of this, and to have an Israeli domestic political crisis arrive in the middle of a revived Iranian nuclear crisis may be one more strain than can be successfully managed.

                                                                                                                                                                                        -0-

US Intel...as the debate rages in the US “domestic spy scandal”, we await what Sen. Arlen Specter and others in Congress come up with. In particular, will they stick to their guns, and demand a full accounting from the White House on what was done, who gave the orders, etc?  But that will be just the first step, as the real question is the political and legal judgment...has the President broken the law? What might these answers mean in the elections this November, and in 2008?

In the meantime, we note a remark by the political analyst Charlie Cook which was about the most depressing thing we read during the entire Holiday break. Charlie was quoted as saying that so long as the Democrats kept the Iraq war debate on the question of “did Bush lie about WMD?”, that was a political winner for the Dems.  But when the Democrats adopted John Murtha’s “when will we pull out?” as question number one, that has thrown the political initiative back to the White House.

Charlie was not, of course, commenting on the substance or morality of either question. He was talking pure power politics. What upset us was the realization that in purely political power terms he was...he is...entirely correct.  Think about the implications of this. Keeping the domestic political debate focused on a by now irrelevant issue (WMD “lies”) is a winner for the Dems.

But moving to the issue of greatest importance to the people of the United States, not to mention the people of Iraq and the Middle East (when will the US bring the troops home?) is a loser for the Dems...and thus, by definition, politically irresponsible.

Can this really be accurate? Maybe so. If the rise of Newt Gingrich and the New Republicans has taught nothing else, it is that in politics, Vince Lombardi was right, “winning is the only thing”. Why? Because if you are not in power, you cannot do what you think is right. Compromise leads to weakness. The other guy makes the decisions.

                                                    -0-

US-ASIA: one hates to begin the New Year with doom and gloom, and bearing in mind that most predicted disasters don’t happen, let’s start this year’s Reports with a quick look at why any Asia expert with a functioning pulse is worried...across the board...although if he or she is still in the employ of the US Government, you aren’t likely to hear this “on the record”.

And bear in mind that to the extent the Middle East situation remains the principle focus of Bush foreign policy involvement, Asia concerns will remain the purview of second and third level officials, and major inconsistencies in presidential-level thinking will likely remain unaddressed .

US-Japan: while the Foreign Ministry seems to recognize that Japan’s relations with neighbors China and South Korea are in the toilet, US observers warn that relations are likely to remain odiferous so long as Prime Minister Koizumi refuses to concede a linkage to “history” and his visits to the Yasukuni Shrine.

After trying to stay out the debate for most of last year, the Bush Administration has become so worried about the strategic implications (both for Japan, and for US relations with China, the ROK, et al) that it has conducted appropriately subtle conversations with Koizumi, and perhaps exaggerated conversations with China about those Bush/Koizumi chats. See some end-of-year, end-of-tenure quotes from the now safely-departed NSC Senior Director Mike Green, who argues that while Bush didn’t directly confront Koizumi about the Shrine visits, that the President is worried about the situation and said so, to both Koizumi, and China’s President Hu Jintao.

But there’s no sign, outside of some Gaimusho approved speeches, that Japan sees the China problem as anything but political, and mainly as a crisis of China’s choice. And there’s increasing evidence that Japanese officials, even those who ought to know better, are getting personally angry with China’s reaction to, or use of, Koizumi. That the US, despite its concerns, does not disagree with Japan’s analysis of China’s political motivations, makes real change in Tokyo even more unlikely.

Still, the movement in Washington is threatening a change of course. Bush arrived in 2001, determined to reverse what he saw as Clinton’s coddling of China, and Clinton’s bashing of Japan. Almost all of the “Japan hands” have departed, and major portions of Asia policy are largely being run out of Deputy Secretary of State Bob Zoellick’s office.  Zoellick, for all of his sophistication and intelligence, remains so virulently anti-Japan that consistently rational calculation of US-Tokyo interests may not be possible.

And now you have Bush (via Green) basically telling Japan it needs to reconsider its diplomacy, and how it handles the history issue. You don’t have to be a member of the old Chrysanthemum Club to worry that US-Japan relations in 2006 resemble a ship about to encounter some very sharp rocks.

Other issues to watch...it seems clear that Koizumi intends, as a legacy, to pursue normalized relations with N. Korea. This may prove impossible if the DPRK can’t figure out some plausible explanation for the fate, and remains, of its kidnap victims...and if Koizumi can’t come up with some face-saving formulation acceptable to domestic Japanese political interests.

But to the extent that Tokyo moves toward normalization with Pyongyang, the gap with Washington on a key issue just gets deeper.

US-Korea: if the 6 Party talks with N. Korea are not officially dead, at best they are on artificial life support, in the public view of outside observers, and in the private view of most US officials directly involved.  Don’t expect anyone to admit this, of course, since there are ramifications no one particularly wants to deal with.

Similarly, no one wants to admit, in public, that neither Seoul nor Beijing puts Pyongyang’s nuclear weapons program on a par with concerns about keeping the DPRK from catastrophic collapse. Nor do they want to admit that China, the ROK, Russia (and even Japan) basically see US policy as the biggest threat to stability...fairly, or unfairly, despite the DPRK’s nuclear threats and gamesmanship.

The S. Korean cabinet reshuffle has brought in a new Unification Minister (the real power in Korean policy toward N. Korea, and therefore, to a large degree, with the US)...a man basically seen by US experts as hopeless, since he and his Ministry support no aspect of the US hard-line. No one will say so “on the record”, of course. But everyone worries that whatever friendly things are said at joint press conferences, the practical US-ROK policy divides will continue to increase, South Korea will continue to pursue the economic development of N. Korea, and the DPRK will continue to exploit the situation.

The State Department’s Asst. Secretary for Asia, Chris Hill, remains nominally in charge of US-DPRK negotiations, but the policy...the practical policy as it works in reality...comes out of the office of Undersecretary for non-proliferation Bob Joseph, and the “real” NSC on this issue, the office of Vice President Cheney.

While Hill struggles valiantly to try to re-schedule some kind of formal or informal visitations to keep the 6 Party negotiations alive, the “real” US policy is aimed at bringing down the Kim Jong-il regime in Pyongyang. That’s what’s behind the sudden re-focus on DPRK counterfeiting, and other activities which have been notorious for more than a decade.

This US hard line will prevail so long as Secretary of State Condi Rice continues the stance she brought over from her tenure as National Security Advisor...a posture leading observers to suspect that while President Bush claims to support peaceful negotiations, Bush basically agrees with the Cheney/Joseph policy that evil must be brought down, not accommodated.

(Note the departure of Amb. Joe deTrani to do N. Asia security and terrorism for John Negroponte, the national intelligence ‘czar’. If Hill tries to replace deTrani, he risks having a Joseph hard-line clone forced on him. If Hill officially takes over a function that he has, in fact, been performing, he risks the perception that “eliminating” the deTrani slot concedes the triumph of the Joseph hard-line faction.)

It remains to be explained by President Bush why it is in US interests (or Bob Joseph’s interests) to have 6 Party stalemate, and continued production of nuclear materials by the DPRK, especially if this continues into 2007, when additional reactor capacity may come on line.

US-China: key portions of US-China relations have always been “at risk” to the extent that Beijing became a scapegoat, in Washington, for the US failure to produce a coherent Korea policy. The notion that “China can do more to deliver the DPRK at the 6 Party talks, and so if the DPRK won’t come across, it’s basically China’s fault”, was fed, if not originated, by folks on the NSC.   The idea was to help “motivate” China.  But a negative outcome is more likely to empower the “China rising” crowd, which will say, “We told you so...”

This bullet may be dodged, since China fully recognizes that it’s prime concern...stability on the Korean Peninsula...now largely rests in its hands. But there is likely to be an increasingly nasty domestic US political debate over “globalization” in which China becomes the symbol of what’s wrong. Election year 2006 in the US is likely to see China in the cross-hairs.

The “currency debate” seems certain to re-ignite in Congress, especially if Treasury runs out of excuses to not “cite” China as a manipulator; WTO compliance and IPR enforcement, despite some pockets of good news, remain genuine risk factors in the relationship.

“China rising” will continue a legitimate, and therefore serious matter of debate, regardless of how China is handled in the much-anticipated Quadrennial Defense Review. DOD Secretary Rumsfeld’s Fall visit opened up some promise of greater PRC military transparency, and a recent White Paper by China declaring it’s intentions to maintain a “peaceful rise”, are encouraging events. But it’s too soon to call them developments, since their implications remain to be measured by practical results, and staying power.

And Japan is not entirely wrong that Beijing is enjoying itself just a bit, since using the “history” fight to demonize Japan helps relieve the domestic political heat on the Taiwan reunification issue.  Don’t go too far with that analysis, just note that with the Taiwan’s domestic political crisis now threatening the DPP, and the KMT leadership’s rather cynical use of its “China card”, Beijing has some reason to feel things are moving in the Mainland’s direction.
