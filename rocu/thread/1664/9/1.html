The Word of the Day for November 01 is:

golden handcuffs \\GOAL-dun-HAND-kuffs\\ noun

: special benefits offered to an employee as an inducement to continue service

Example sentence:
Dad eventually accepted the golden handcuffs and agreed to five more years with the company.

Did you know?
Chances are you&#39;ve heard of a &quot;golden handshake,&quot; which is a particularly tempting severance agreement offered to an employee in an effort to induce the person to retire early. People started getting &quot;golden handshakes&quot; (by that name) around 1960; by 1976, English speakers had also coined the accompanying &quot;golden handcuffs&quot; to describe a situation in which someone is offered a special inducement to stay. The _expression turns up often in quasi-literal uses, such as &quot;slapped golden handcuffs on&quot; or reference to &quot;a shiny new set of golden handcuffs.&quot; (It&#39;s also common in British, as well as American, usage.)

The Word of the Day for November 02 is:

jejune \\jih-JOON\\ adjective

1 : lacking nutritive value
*2 : devoid of significance or interest : dull
3 : juvenile, puerile

Example sentence:
Professor Hazlett&#39;s jejune lectures often left students dozing in the auditorium.

Did you know?
If you are starved for excitement, you won&#39;t get it from something jejune. That term derives from the Latin &quot;jejunus,&quot; which means &quot;empty of food,&quot; &quot;meager,&quot; or &quot;hungry.&quot; Back in the 1600s, English speakers used &quot;jejune&quot; in senses very similar to those of its Latin parent, lamenting &quot;jejune appetites&quot; and &quot;jejune morsels.&quot; Something that is meager usually doesn&#39;t satisfy one&#39;s desires, and before long &quot;jejune&quot; was being used not only for meager meals or hunger, but also for things wanting in intellectual or emotional substance. The word most likely gained its &quot;juvenile&quot; or &quot;childish&quot; sense when people confused it with the look-alike French word &quot;jeune,&quot; which means &quot;young.&quot;

The Word of the Day for November 03 is:

catachresis \\kat-uh-KREE-sis\\ noun

*1 : use of the wrong word for the context
2 : use of a forced and especially paradoxical figure of speech

Example sentence:
The paper printed a correction for the previous day&#39;s catachresis: dubbing a local artist-philanthropist a &quot;socialist&quot; when they meant &quot;socialite.&quot;

Did you know?
As you might have guessed, &quot;catachresis&quot; is a word favored by grammarians. It can be employed as a fancy label of disparagement for whatever uses the grammarian finds unacceptable. Thus could Henry Fowler, in the 1920s, call &quot;mutual&quot; in &quot;our mutual friend&quot; a catachresis. (Fowler preferred &quot;common,&quot; but &quot;mutual&quot; does have an established sense which is correct in that context.) More often, &quot;catachresis&quot; is used for an unintentional misuse and is very close in meaning to &quot;malapropism,&quot; which usually refers to an unintentionally humorous misuse of a word. &quot;Catachresis&quot; has been used to describe (or decry) misuses of words since at least 1550. The word comes to us by way of Latin from the Greek noun &quot;katachrēsis,&quot; which means &quot;misuse.&quot;

The Word of the Day for November 04 is:

ab ovo \\ab-OH-voh\\ adverb

: from the beginning

Example sentence:
&quot;Would you live your life differently if you could start again ab ovo?&quot; Rachel asked.

Did you know?
&quot;Ab ovo usque ad mala.&quot; That phrase translates as &quot;from the egg to the apples,&quot; and it was penned by the Roman poet Horace. He was alluding to the Roman tradition of starting a meal with eggs and finishing it with apples. Horace also applied &quot;ab ovo&quot; in an account of the Trojan War that begins with the mythical egg of Leda from which Helen (whose beauty sparked the war) was born. In both cases, Horace used &quot;ab ovo&quot; to allude to a literal egg while figuratively suggesting the meaning &quot;from the beginning.&quot; It was this figurative meaning that found its way into English in the 16th century, when Sir Philip Sidney wrote: &quot;If [the dramatic poets] wil represent an history, they must not (as Horace saith) beginne Ab ouo: but they must come to the principall poynt of that one action.&quot;

The Word of the Day for November 05 is:

zenith \\ZEE-nith\\ noun

1 : the point of the celestial sphere that is directly opposite the nadir and vertically above the observer
2 : the highest point reached in the heavens by a celestial body
*3 : culminating point : acme

Example sentence:
&quot;Winning a gold medal at the Olympic games would be the zenith of my career,&quot; said the skier in a recent interview.

Did you know?
When you reach the zenith, you&#39;re at the top, the pinnacle, the summit, the peak. &quot;Zenith&quot; developed from Arabic terms meaning &quot;the way over one&#39;s head.&quot; As long ago as the 1300s, English speakers used &quot;zenith&quot; to name the highest point in the celestial heavens, directly overhead. By the 1600s, &quot;zenith&quot; was being used in English for other high points as well.

The Word of the Day for November 06 is:

temporize \\TEM-puh-ryze\\ verb

1 : to act to suit the time or occasion : yield to current or dominant opinion
*2 : to draw out discussions or negotiations so as to gain time

Example sentence:
The senators have been accused of temporizing while the budget crisis continues to worsen.

Did you know?
&quot;Temporize&quot; comes from the Medieval Latin verb &quot;temporizare&quot; (&quot;to pass the time&quot;), which itself comes from the Latin noun &quot;tempus,&quot; meaning &quot;time.&quot; (&quot;Tempus&quot; is also an ancestor of such words as &quot;tempo,&quot; &quot;contemporary,&quot; and &quot;temporal.&quot;) If you need to buy some time, you might resort to temporizing — but you probably won&#39;t win admiration for doing so. &quot;Temporize&quot; can have a somewhat negative connotation. For instance, a political leader faced with a difficult issue might temporize by talking vaguely about possible solutions without actually doing anything. The point of such temporizing is to avoid taking definite — and possibly unpopular — action, in hopes that the problem will somehow go away. But the effect is often just to make matters worse.

The Word of the Day for November 07 is:

detritus \\dih-TRYE-tus\\ noun

1 : loose material (as rock fragments or organic particles) that results directly from disintegration
2 a : a product of disintegration, destruction, or wearing away : debris *b : miscellaneous remnants : odds and ends

Example sentence:
&quot;The blog originated ... as a catch basin for mental detritus, for the kind of stuff not good enough for print, but too good to waste on casual conversation.&quot; (Joel Achenbach, The Washington Post, August 21, 2005)

Did you know?
In the late 18th century, Scottish geologist James Hutton borrowed the Latin word &quot;detritus&quot; (meaning &quot;rubbing away&quot;) for the process of wearing away or wearing down rock. His use of the word, however, was short-lived: one of the last appearances of this usage is in an 1802 book on his geologic theory. In that book, &quot;detritus&quot; was also used to describe the loose material that results from disintegration. It is that use, unlike Hutton&#39;s original, which has withstood the test of time and is firmly established in geology. Not surprisingly, &quot;detritus,&quot; with its erudite sound and figurative possibility, was also taken up by non-geologists, from other scientists to nonscientists.

The Word of the Day for November 08 is:

vulnerary \\VUL-nuh-rair-ee\\ adjective

: used for or useful in healing wounds

Example sentence:
Native Americans prized the herb echinacea for its vulnerary properties, using it to treat burns and snakebite as well as arrow wounds.

Did you know?
&quot;Vulnus,&quot; in Latin means &quot;wound.&quot; You might think, then, that the English adjective &quot;vulnerary&quot; would mean &quot;wounding, causing a wound.&quot; And, indeed, &quot;vulnerary&quot; has been used that way, along with two obsolete adjectives, &quot;vulnerative&quot; and &quot;vulnific.&quot; But for the lasting and current use of &quot;vulnerary,&quot; we took our cue from the Roman scholar Pliny the Elder. In his Natural History, he used the Latin adjective &quot;vulnerarius&quot; to describe a plaster, or dressing, for healing wounds. And that&#39;s fine — the suffix &quot;-ary&quot; merely indicates that there is a connection, which, in this case, is to wounds. (As you may have already suspected, &quot;vulnerable&quot; is related; it comes from the Latin verb &quot;vulnerare,&quot; which means &quot;to wound.&quot;)

The Word of the Day for November 09 is:

yahoo \\YAH-hoo\\ noun

: a boorish, crass, or stupid person

Example sentence:
The local teenagers&#39; reputation as a bunch of yahoos was belied by their courteous treatment of the stranded motorists.

Did you know?
We know exactly how old &quot;yahoo&quot; is because its debut in print also marked its entrance into the English language as a whole. &quot;Yahoo&quot; began life as a made-up word invented by Jonathan Swift in his book Gulliver&#39;s Travels, which was published in 1726. The Yahoos were a race of brutes, with the form and vices of humans, encountered by Gulliver in his fourth and final voyage. They represented Swift&#39;s view of mankind at its lowest. It is not surprising, then, that &quot;yahoo&quot; came to be applied to any actual human who was particularly unpleasant or unintelligent. Yahoos were controlled by the intelligent and virtuous Houyhnhnms, a word which apparently did not catch people&#39;s fancy as &quot;yahoo&quot; did.

The Word of the Day for November 10 is:

carceral \\KAHR-suh-rul\\ adjective

: of, relating to, or suggesting a jail or prison

Example sentence:
When James first glimpsed his new campus, he thought there was something rather carceral about the school&#39;s tall wrought-iron fence.

Did you know?
Describing a painting of John Howard visiting a prison in 1787, writer Robert Hughes reminds us that Howard was &quot;the pioneer of English carceral reform&quot; (Time Magazine, November 11, 1985). Huges might have said &quot;prison reform,&quot; but what about Vladimir Nabokov, when, in his inimitable prose, he describes a prison scene in Invitation to a Beheading: &quot;The door opened, whining, rattling and groaning in keeping with all the rules of carceral counterpoint.&quot; Here we find &quot;carceral&quot; not only practical but practically poetical. An adjective borrowed directly from Late Latin, &quot;carceral&quot; appeared shortly after &quot;incarcerate&quot; (&quot;to imprison&quot;), which first showed up in English around the mid-1500s; they&#39;re both ultimately from &quot;carcer,&quot; Latin for &quot;prison.&quot;
