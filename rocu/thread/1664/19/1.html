: a position of great prominence or advantage

&quot;In the catbird seat&quot; was among the numerous, folksy expressions with which the legendary baseball broadcaster Red Barber delighted listeners. In a 1942 short story titled &quot;The Catbird Seat,&quot; James Thurber featured a character, Mrs. Barrows, who liked to use the phrase. Another character, Joey Hart, explained that Mrs. Barrows must have picked up the expression from Red Barber. gaffer \\GAFF-er\\ Audio icon  noun
1 : an old man
2 : British a : foreman, overseer b : employer
3 : a head glassblower

Example sentence:
Before the first day of shooting, the gaffer spent several days setting up all the lights.

Did you know?
Though movie and cinema buffs associate &quot;gaffer&quot; with Hollywood, the word actually predates motion pictures by about 300 years. The first recorded use of &quot;gaffer&quot; dates from the 16th century, when it was used as a title of respect for an older gentleman. Later it was used as a generic noun for any elderly man, and then it picked up the sense &quot;foreman&quot; (still used in British English), perhaps because the foreman was the most experienced and, most likely, the oldest person in a work crew. Today &quot;gaffer&quot; is usually applied to the head lighting electrician on a movie set. The gaffer&#39;s assistant is called the &quot;best boy.&quot;

The Word of the Day for February 12 is:

walleyed \\WAWL-eyed\\ adjective

1 : having walleyes or affected with walleye
*2 : marked by a wild irrational staring of the eyes

Example sentence:
Being refused service at the restaurant left Trent so angry that he could only manage a walleyed stare.

Did you know?
The noun &quot;walleye&quot; has several meanings. It can refer to an eye with a whitish or bluish-white iris or to one with an opaque white cornea. It can also refer to a condition in which the eye turns outward away from the nose. The extended second sense of the adjective &quot;walleyed&quot; came from the appearance of eyes affected with the condition of walleye. You might guess that &quot;walleyed&quot; has an etymological connection with &quot;wall,&quot; but that?s not the case. Rather, it is derived from &quot;wawil-eghed&quot; — a Middle English translation of the Old Norse word &quot;vagl-eygr,&quot; from &quot;vagl&quot; (&quot;beam&quot;) and &quot;eygr&quot; (&quot;eyed&quot;).

The Word of the Day for February 13 is:

numen \\NOO-mun\\ Audio icon noun
: a spiritual force or influence often identified with a natural object, phenomenon, or place

Example sentence:
We were in a village that had hardly changed in a thousand years, and we felt a numen that transcended earthly religions and human histories.

Did you know?
How did &quot;numen,&quot; a Latin term meaning &quot;nod of the head,&quot; come to be associated with spiritual power? The answer lies in the fact that the ancient Romans saw divine force and power operating in the inanimate objects and nonhuman phenomena around them. They believed that the gods had the power to command events and to consent to actions, and the idea of a god nodding suggested his or her awesome abilities—divine power. Eventually, Latin speakers began using &quot;numen&quot; to describe the special divine force of any object, place, or phenomenon that inspired awe (a mystical-seeming wooded grove, for example, or the movement of the sun), and &quot;numen&quot; made the semantic leap from &quot;nod&quot; to &quot;divine will or power.&quot; English speakers adopted the word during the 1600s.

The Word of the Day for February 14 is:

eradicate  \\ih-RAD-uh-kayt\\  verb
1 : to pull up by the roots
*2 : to do away with as completely as if by pulling up by the roots

Example sentence:
Efforts to eradicate smallpox have been almost entirely successful.

Did you know?
Given that &quot;eradicate&quot; first meant &quot;to pull up by the roots,&quot; it&#39;s not surprising that the root of &quot;eradicate&quot; is, in fact, &quot;root.&quot; &quot;Eradicate,&quot; which first turned up in English in the 16th century, comes from &quot;eradicatus,&quot; the past participle of the Latin verb &quot;eradicare.&quot; &quot;Eradicare,&quot; in turn, can be traced back to the Latin word &quot;radix,&quot; meaning &quot;root&quot; or &quot;radish.&quot; Although &quot;eradicate&quot; began life as a word for literal uprooting, by the mid-17th century it had developed a metaphorical application to removing things the way one might yank an undesirable weed up by the roots. Other descendants of &quot;radix&quot; in English include &quot;radical&quot; and &quot;radish.&quot; Even the word &quot;root&quot; itself is related; it comes from the same ancient word that gave Latin &quot;radix.&quot;

We don&#39;t know if that&#39;s true, but we do know that ?zeitgeist? has been a useful addition to the English language since at least 1835.

The Word of the Day for February 16 is:

pixilated \\PIK-suh-lay-tud\\ adjective

1 : somewhat unbalanced mentally; also : bemused
*2 : whimsical

Example sentence:
In 1998, Julie Taymor won a Tony Award for her original, pixilated costume and puppet designs that gave life to Disney&#39;s The Lion King on the stage.

Did you know?
&quotixilated&quot; is an American coinage that dates back to about the mid-19th century. A pixilated person behaves as if under the spell of pixies or enchanted by them. The word has been used to describe people who are confused (as though they have seen a pixie) or intoxicated (to the point that they are seeing pixies); it can also be used for people who are like playfully mischievous pixies themselves. Today, &quot;pixilated&quot; usually means &quot;somewhat unbalanced mentally,&quot; &quot;bemused,&quot; or &quot;whimsical.&quot; Additionally, it has fairly recently begun popping up in the digital realm as a word describing an image (as on a computer or television screen) that is made up of a large number of pixels. This recent usage, which we are monitoring for entry in our dictionary, is also commonly spelled &quot;pixelated.&quot;

The Word of the Day for February 17 is:

bijou \\BEE-zhoo\\ noun

*1 : a small dainty usually ornamental piece of delicate workmanship : jewel
2 : something delicate, elegant, or highly prized

Example sentence:
Some jewelers believe that women who buy their own bijoux are the next growth market.

Did you know?
&quot;Bijou&quot; (which can be pluralized as either &quot;bijoux&quot; or &quot;bijous&quot;) has adorned English since the late 17th century. We borrowed it from French, but the word ultimately traces to Breton, a Celtic language (one closely related to Cornish and Welsh) spoken by inhabitants of the Brittany region of northwest France. Our modern English word derives from Breton &quot;bizou,&quot; which means &quot;ring.&quot; That history makes &quot;bijou&quot; a rare gem in English because, although the Breton people occupied part of England for many years before they were pushed into France by the Anglo-Saxons in the 5th and 6th centuries, very few Breton-derived words remain in our language (another Breton descendant is &quot;menhir,&quot; a term for a prehistoric monument).

hallowed \\HAL-oad\\ adjective

1 : holy, consecrated
*2 : sacred, revered

Example sentence:
During the tour of the university campus, Derek was pleased to find himself walking the same hallowed halls that his father and grandfather had walked years before.

Something that is hallowed is looked upon with great respect, often due to its high stature or important role in history. Look into the history of the word, however, and it might just give you the shivers. &quot;Hallowed&quot; is the past participle of the verb &quot;hallow,&quot; a term that derives from the Middle English &quot;halowen.&quot; That word can in turn be traced back to &quot;hālig,&quot; Old English for &quot;holy.&quot; During the Middle Ages, &quot;All Halowen Day&quot; was the name for what Christians now call All Saints&#39; Day, and the evening that preceded All Halowen Day was &quot;All Hallow Even&quot;—or, as we know it today, Halloween.

The Word of the Day for February 19 is:

repugn \\rih-PYOON\\ verb

: to contend against : oppose

Example sentence:

&quot;Repugn&quot; is a word that was relatively common in English in the 16th and 17th centuries. These days, however, English speakers are more likely to be familiar with one of its close relatives, namely, the adjective &quot;repugnant,&quot; which formerly meant &quot;hostile&quot; but today most commonly means &quot;exciting distaste or aversion.&quot; The Latin root for both of these words is &quot;pugnare,&quot; meaning &quot;to fight.&quot; Other English derivatives from this root are &quot;pugnacious,&quot; meaning &quot;belligerent,&quot; and &quot;impugn,&quot; meaning &quot;to assail with words or arguments.&quot; Even &quot;pungent&quot; is a relative of &quot;pugnare.&quot; Therefore, don&#39;t try to repugn, or impugn for that matter, the influence of &quot;pugnare&quot; on our language — lest you appear pugnacious!
