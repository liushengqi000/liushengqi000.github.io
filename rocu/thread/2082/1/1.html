October 28, 2005

Harriet Miers Exits Stage Right

President Bush finally dug himself out yesterday from the political rubble he created by choosing a Supreme Court nominee whose only known qualifications were his personal comfort with her and her personal loyalty to him. Soon we&#39;ll learn which lesson Mr. Bush drew from the spectacle of a Supreme Court nominee&#39;s being booted off the island by her own team. Has the president learned that he should nominate someone of obvious quality? Or has he decided that he has to continue to bow to the increasingly strident demands of his party&#39;s right wing?

Mr. Bush pretended to be agreeing reluctantly to Ms. Miers&#39;s desire to withdraw over high principles of executive privilege and the separation of powers. We wish that Mr. Bush had chosen a different face-saving device. The one he selected - the senators&#39; perfectly justified desire to review Ms. Miers&#39;s tenure as White House counsel - played to his worst instincts to withhold information.

But everyone knew the truth. Ms. Miers seemed such a legal lightweight that the president created the perfect political storm: pressures converged from his right flank, which did not find her a safe enough bet to rule against legalized abortion, and from the mainstream of his party and Democrats in Congress, who refused to back Ms. Miers because she was just not qualified.

The Supreme Court is the one branch of government whose members do not have to answer to the voters, ever, so they derive their authority from their credibility. Ms. Miers&#39;s career and her bumbling after being nominated suggested that she did not rise to that level. Mr. Bush did not have to listen to Edward Kennedy or Dianne Feinstein to get this message. It also came from the Senate Judiciary Committee&#39;s chairman, Arlen Specter, and from other, more conservative, Republicans who share Mr. Bush&#39;s views on the role of judges and abortion but would not rubber-stamp such a poor choice.

Mr. Bush will compound the Miers disaster if he responds to only the pressure from conservatives - if he tries to placate right-wing Republicans with someone who simply passes their increasingly restrictive litmus test on abortion and is on the fringe of mainstream judicial thinking.

A Supreme Court nominee does not have to be a judge or a constitutional scholar. Ms. Miers&#39;s problem was not that she lacked those lines on her résumé. These jobs demand candidates with a legal background, preferably a solid grounding in constitutional law, but also people who can hold their own in the court&#39;s internal debates, formulate strong and well-drawn arguments, and build majorities on court decisions. A good nominee would understand the court&#39;s role in protecting rights and have a judicial approach within the broad mainstream on issues like the balance of federal and state powers, civil liberties, and the role of government - and be capable of filling out the Senate&#39;s questionnaire without a do-over.

A Supreme Court justice should have an open mind and a capacity to grow. Far-right Republicans live in such fear of this notion that they were not soothed by the White House&#39;s inappropriate harping on Ms. Miers&#39;s evangelical Christian faith, or Mr. Bush&#39;s peculiar assurance that she would never change her mind, not even over decades.

Finally, there is the lingering issue of the candor of Supreme Court nominees during the confirmation process. Some nominees have run into serious trouble by talking too much, but the current tactic of saying nothing of substance is not the answer.

The Senate has a duty to question Supreme Court nominees, not about how they would rule in particular cases, but about their philosophy and their records. The nominees have a duty to respond. This page opposed the nomination of Chief Justice John Roberts, not because we considered him unqualified, but because he refused to clear up the many mysteries about his views. Now Mr. Bush claims that senators (read: Democrats) damaged the process and drove away Ms. Miers by saying they would ask about her job as White House counsel and request documents from the counsel&#39;s office.

Mr. Bush is not the first president to face this challenge. Richard Nixon had two mediocre nominees shot down, Harold Carswell and Clement Haynesworth, and came up with Harry Blackmun and William Rehnquist. Neither man ended up quite where he started on the court, and people differ about their legacies, but nobody doubts that they were of Supreme Court caliber.

Editorial
Van Gogh&#39;s Pen

It&#39;s almost impossible to think of Vincent van Gogh without thinking of the velocity of his career: just a short decade filled with hundreds of paintings and drawings before his suicide in 1890. The compression is so great, the pressure of his concentration so intense, that his works almost refuse to be considered apart from one another and their role in defining the dense, swirling arc of his imagination.

The remarkable exhibition of van Gogh&#39;s drawings at the Metropolitan Museum naturally insists - simply by the logic of chronology - on his swift, brutal development from a beginning artist of conventional means and nearly conventional talents into van Gogh. Looking at these drawings in order is like watching the formal written alphabet turn, in a few short years, into a series of runic marks with vastly greater power to say just what the artist sees.

But these drawings also ask us to think about van Gogh&#39;s tools, especially the pen he learned to use when he went to Provence in February 1888. It&#39;s hard to imagine a plainer instrument: a reed plucked from the southern marshes and given a penlike tip with a sharp knife. It carried only a trace of ink and was as blunt or as fine as the artist chose to make it. There is no guessing how many reed pens van Gogh made while he was in Provence. But their impact is visible from the moment he reaches Arles.

The reed begins to subvert his landscapes. A relatively traditional view of Arles in the distance is utterly upstaged by a cluster of irises in the foreground, drawn as if the pen was suggesting just how frank and simple its vocabulary could be. We have the privilege of watching van Gogh discover the geometry of a line, the elliptical power of a sky full of dots, the rhythm of the ink itself.

Many of these drawings echo - or record - the subject of van Gogh&#39;s paintings. But they also ask the kind of question that can be answered only in our own imaginations. What was it like for van Gogh to put down the reed pen, a cheap tool using cheap ink, and pick up the brush, committing himself, in expensive oils, to canvas?

The question presents itself again and again. Seeing these late drawings, you can imagine a convergence taking place in van Gogh&#39;s mind - that it was now, at last, that his eye and hand mattered, not the pen or the brush, the ink or the oil. You would recognize these strokes anywhere.

November 5, 2005
Editorial
The Time Is Wrong for Tax Cuts
Before Hurricane Katrina, Congressional Republicans seemed unstoppable in their drive to cut taxes by an additional $70 billion in the next five years. Now some of the moderates are thinking twice. They fear the cost at a time when the country is already borrowing hundreds of billions of dollars - most of it from foreigners - to pay for things like war, hurricane recovery and flu vaccine. And they fear voters&#39; disapproval if they cut taxes for wealthy taxpayers after gutting programs for the poor. (The tax cuts are scheduled to come up just after the current round of spending cuts.)

The moderates are right to be wary, especially when the tax-cut die-hards in Congress and the administration keep saying that more tax cuts are vital for continued economic growth after Katrina. That&#39;s not so.

In the short term, the economy expands when there&#39;s growing demand for all the things that money can buy. Tax cuts increase demand when consumers hold back because of, say, a recession. There&#39;s no need for that spur now, but if there were, the right tax cuts would put cash into the hands of people who would otherwise not have much to spend. Tax breaks that mainly benefit the wealthiest, like the special low rates for investment income that Congress is itching to pass, don&#39;t fit that description.

In the long run, the economy grows when money is invested in ways that increase the nation&#39;s capacity to make more goods and provide more services. The money for such investments comes from national savings, which are highly dependent on a healthy federal budget.

Today&#39;s budget is decidedly unhealthy, and more tax cuts will only make the big deficits worse. That&#39;s bound to drive up interest rates, making it more expensive for businesses to borrow money. Economists say that will lead to less investment and less growth.

Tax-cut zealots are also misleading Americans when they imply that they are offsetting reductions in revenue by slashing government spending. These lawmakers have largely spared pork-barrel spending and are instead cutting deeply into programs that foster better health, more education and a stronger infrastructure. Those are precisely the things that are crucial for widespread economic vitality.

There are many things that could provoke an economic slowdown: the cooling of the housing boom, miserable wage growth, high energy prices and so on. Dropping the tax cuts is not one of them.
