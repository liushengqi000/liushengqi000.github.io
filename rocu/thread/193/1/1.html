Issue 1: China regresses with anti-Taiwan aggression
                                                                     By Jo Kent

Taiwan is no longer simply an area of political controversy. A few weeks ago, the People’s Republic of China deemed a dispute with Taiwan a viable reason for a war, viewing it as a rogue province that needs to be reeled into shore.

On March 13, Mainland China’s 10th National People’s Congress voted unanimously for the “anti-secession” bill, which gave mainland China the legal authority to attack Taiwan if China determined Taiwan’s independence efforts were too aggressive. The legislation authorizes China’s People’s Liberation Army to use “non-peaceful means” to suppress Taiwan if Taiwan is seen as seeking “virtual independence” or engendering any “grave events” that aim to split the island from the mainland. These two conditions are so vague that Beijing could use force against Taiwan arbitrarily.

Thrown into that Molotov cocktail is the independence-oriented policy of Taiwanese President Chen Shui-Bian. Before the bill was even introduced, Chen plausibly could have proclaimed “independence” and Asia would have been embroiled in a war. The existing tension combined with this new anti-secession bill is an unproductive recipe for what would be a regrettable disaster.

So the head-scratching, fundamental question here is “Why now?” The authorization of war against Taiwan seems to be an unnecessary law at a time when leaders on the two sides of the Taiwan Strait are showing signs of reaching common ground. The direct flights between Taiwan and the mainland during the Chinese New Year holidays last year — after the flights were banned from 1949 to 2003 — are an example of the rekindled conversation.

Moreover, when the National People’s Congress opened in March, Communist leaders set out to cultivate China’s image as an emerging, viable superpower. One of President Hu Jintao’s top priorities for the NPC was to “promote a harmonious society.” So with hopes to abate threats to the party leadership, mainland China implied that 2005 is the year to give its national and international images permanent face-lifts politically, economically and ideologically. It seems to me, however, that when a country needs to talk about being more harmonious and peaceful, it is neither. Beijing has been suspicious and distrustful of Chen, and the anti-secession law largely limits Taiwan’s political maneuverability. Beijing now has the authority to unilaterally interpret cross-strait relations, and that antagonistic approach is a moronic way to welcome a powerful island back into the family.

In addition, the anti-secession law jeopardizes mainland China’s own relationships with the rest of the world. Mainland Chinese leaders know the nation will have difficulty excelling economically if the international community has to add “worry about new Chinese military threat” to its laundry list of concerns.

The country formerly known as a Paper Tiger should not be wasting its time on a war of rhetoric with Taiwan. It has bigger fish to fry, namely solving its own gaping east-west regional disparity of development and teetering relationships with North Korea and Japan. Then again, China’s threats might just be the same rhetoric it has been spewing since mainland China and Taiwan split in the Communist Revolution of 1949. While China certainly has the capabilities to pitch missiles across the strait, disrupting trade and infrastructure, would it really do it? Perhaps the furor has been much ado about nothing.

                                                          Jo Kent is a Baker College junior.

Issue 2: Leebron-Forman spring study break to include raffle, food, performances
                                                                         By Katricia Lang | Thresher staff

President David Leebron and Dean of Undergraduates Robin Forman will host a year-end study break, the “Leebron-Forman Getaway Study Break” May 1 from 9-11 p.m. at the Student Center. The study break will have a tropical theme and will feature student performances, door prizes, food and free massages.

Last semester, Leebron hosted a “Getting Centered” study break that was held on three days at three different locations — the Recreation, Student and Wellness Centers. Fewer students attended those study breaks than previous years’ study breaks held at one time in a single location. Student Center Director Boyd Beckwith said Student Center Advisory Council members suggested the low attendance may have been the result of the multiple dates and locations. “We found that students wanted one big event so they could hang out with their friends,” Beckwith said.

Residential colleges have been asked not to schedule any college events during the study break.

Beckwith said he expects about 800 students to attend this semester’s study break. College serveries and Student Center businesses will provide the food, which will include tropical wings, vegetable stir fry, fried tofu, tropical ice cream sundaes, sherbet, fruit, brownies, cookies and Starburst candy. The Coffeehouse will offer free coffee, and Smoothie King is donating 750 12-ounce smoothies.

Student bands will perform in Farnsworth Pavilion, which will also be the location of a hula-hoop contest and a limbo contest. Spontaneous Combustion, Rice’s improvisational comedy group; the Rice Philharmonics, an a cappella group; and the slam poetry team will perform in the Grand Hall. Students will also be able to pick up the 2003-’04 Campanile, and the Rice Student Volunteer Program will collect money for charity.

Beginning at noon May 1, students can sign up for 10-minute massages at the study break. Forman and Leebron will be available for students to meet at the study break, but they will not pose for pictures, unlike former President Malcolm Gillis and former Vice President for Student Affairs Zenaido Camacho. Forman said he has not yet decided whether to wear a costume. Beckwith said he is pleased Leebron is making himself available to students. Jones College sophomore Misha Teplitskiy said seeing administrators in costume was not the reason he attended the study break in the past.

“The reason I went to the study break was … to get some food, … to get out of the room and … to see friends, not to see someone dressed up or meet Camacho,” Teplitskiy said. Forman said he hopes the study break will give students a respite from their finals-week work.

“The study break gives students a chance to get together for a big social event that they can all share,” Forman said. “It’s a little fun and relaxation during the final-[exam] period.”

Issue 3: Campus hurt by Rice for Life’s playground politics
                                                                 By Evan Mintz

Every week I look forward to the new posters I’m bound to find around campus. What fun events are going on this week? Party — oh boy! Advertisements for Red Bull — sorry, I’m a Mountain Dew man. Rice for Life — I grab my forehead and count to 10.

According to recent signs, Margaret Sanger’s flawed views on eugenics reflect directly on the modern pro-choice movement. Apparently, a young woman going to Planned Parenthood to get birth control pills is actually supporting the sterilization of minorities — never mind the eulogy given by Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. at Sanger’s funeral.

Abortion is a tricky and controversial issue and civilized discourse is the only way to properly deal with it. We should all come together and talk about a way to reduce abortions while preserving a woman’s control over her own body.

Alas, Rice for Life seems unable to engage in such civilized discourse. Their arguments have sunk to the level of childish name-calling and attack politics. Every poster seems to attempt to stimulate emotion rather than attempt to make a legitimate point. By “legitimate,” I mean lines of argument that do not rely on pictures of fetuses and claims of “real feminists” not getting abortions.

Rice for Choice could just as easily sink to these levels. I see it now: “Rice for Life, why do you support killing Planned Parenthood doctors? Outraged? You should be.”

These are not actual arguments. They are name-calling and will solve nothing.

The people on both sides of this issue are not nuts, fanatics or evil. They are our fellow Rice students. I do not think students want to create a climate of hate on campus — at least I hope not. No one supports a culture of death. No one hates women. Neither group advocates something it considers wrong.

If Rice for Life wants to stop being hated on campus, it should treat others as it would like to be treated and end its current attack and protest methods.

Also, Rice for Life needs to realize that Rice for Choice should not be its enemy but rather its ally in reducing abortions. Indeed, very few people are actually “pro-abortion,” and Rice for Life needs to recognize that if it wants to be taken seriously. Joint civilized debates with Rice for Choice, a stance supporting birth control and an initiative to educate the community outside the hedges about alternatives to unsafe sex would help Rice for Life dispel its current image as a hate group.

And if Rice for Life wants to really reform, it could actually address all issues of sustaining life, like supporting a ban on executions or advocating universal health care to ensure no one dies before his or her time. Extending the message of life to people in developing nations would certainly lend legitimacy to the title “Rice for Life,” rather than the currently more appropriate “Rice Against Rice for Choice.”

Right now, Rice for Life’s posters are Ward Churchill-esque accusations of evil, tolerable only because of freedom of speech. But resorting to accusations of evil usually implies a need to hype up an otherwise poor argument.

Please, Rice for Life, if you have a legitimate argument, problem and solution then present it. Otherwise, let the grown-ups talk and leave the name-calling on the playground. Evan Mintz is a Hanszen College freshman and opinion editor.
