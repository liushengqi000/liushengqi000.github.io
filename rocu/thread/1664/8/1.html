The Word of the Day for October 22 is:

telegraphese \\tel-uh-graf-EEZ\\ noun

: language characterized by the terseness and ellipses that are common in telegrams

Example sentence:
The translator couldn&#39;t keep up with the speaker, so what we heard of the speech sounded like telegraphese.

Did you know?
E-mail&#39;s the thing nowadays, but in the 19th century the way to send a quick message to someone far away was, of course, the telegraph. The original French namers of the telegraph (&quot;télégraphe&quot; in French) took a lesson from the Greeks: Greek &quot;tēle-&quot; means &quot;distant,&quot; and &quot;-graphe&quot; traces to a Greek verb meaning &quot;to write.&quot; Later, a message sent by telegraph was dubbed in English a &quot;telegram&quot; (from Greek &quot;gramma,&quot; meaning &quot;letter&quot;). Telegrams were a great innovation, but they were expensive. You had to pay by the word, so folks created a kind of shorthand that let them keep their missives brief. By the late 1800s, &quot;telegraphese&quot; was being used for any language that was as terse as the clipped and cryptic style used in telegrams.

The Word of the Day for October 23 is:

equivocal \\ih-KWIV-uh-kul\\ adjective

1 *a : subject to two or more interpretations and usually used to mislead or confuse b : uncertain as an indication or sign
2 a : of uncertain nature or classification b : of uncertain disposition toward a person or thing : undecided c : of doubtful advantage, genuineness, or moral rectitude

Example sentence:
When asked if he would run for president, the senator gave only equivocal answers, providing little information about his plans.

Did you know?
&quot;Equivocal,&quot; &quot;vague,&quot; and &quot;ambiguous&quot; all mean &quot;not clearly understandable&quot; and are used to describe confusing speech or writing. &quot;Equivocal&quot; — which can be traced back to the Latin prefix &quot;aequi-&quot; (&quot;equi-&quot;) and the Latin word &quot;vox&quot; (&quot;voice&quot;) — applies to language left open to differing interpretations with the intention of deceiving or evading (&quot;moral precepts with equivocal phrasing&quot;). &quot;Vague&quot; implies a lack of clear formulation due to inadequate conception or consideration (&quot;I had only a vague idea of how to get there&quot;). &quot;Ambiguous,&quot; like &quot;equivocal,&quot; applies to language capable of more than one interpretation but usually does not have the negative connotations of deception or evasion (&quot;the poet&#39;s wording is intentionally ambiguous&quot;).

amphibology \\am-fuh-BAH-luh-jee\\ noun

: a sentence or phrase that can be interpreted in more than one way

Example sentence:
Not wanting to voice what I really felt, I resorted to amphibology and said, &quot;I can&#39;t say too many good things about her.&quot;

Did you know?
A venerable old word in English, &quot;amphibology&quot; is from Greek &quot;amphibolos&quot; (via Late Latin and Latin). &quot;Amphibolos,&quot; from &quot;amphi-&quot; (&quot;both&quot;) and &quot;ballein&quot; (&quot;to throw&quot;), literally means &quot;encompassing&quot; or &quot;hitting at both ends&quot;; figuratively it means &quot;ambiguous.&quot; Amphibology is an equivocator&#39;s friend. An editor who has been sent an unsolicited manuscript to critique, for example, might reply, &quot;I shall lose no time in reading your book.&quot; Or a dinner guest who feels the onset of heartburn might say something like, &quot;Ah, that was a meal I shall not soon forget!&quot; But amphibology&#39;s ambiguity can be unintended and undesirable as well, as in &quot;When Mom talked to Judy, she said she might call her back the next day.&quot; (Who said who might call whom back?)

The Word of the Day for October 25 is:

filial \\FILL-ee-ul\\ adjective

*1 : of, relating to, or befitting a son or daughter
2 : having or assuming the relation of a child or offspring

Example sentence:
Martha&#39;s acute sense of filial responsibility made her check on her mother whenever a few days went by without contact.

Did you know?
&quot;Filial&quot; is descended from the Latin &quot;filius,&quot; meaning &quot;son,&quot; and &quot;filia,&quot; meaning &quot;daughter,&quot; and in English (where it has been used since at least the 14th century) it has always applied to both sexes. At one time, the word carried the dutiful sense &quot;owed to a parent by a child,&quot; typically found in the then-common phrases &quot;filial respect&quot; and &quot;filial piety.&quot; It can now be used more generally for any emotion or behavior of a child to a parent. You might suspect that &quot;filia&quot; is also the source of the word &quot;filly,&quot; meaning &quot;a young female horse&quot; or &quot;a young girl,&quot; but it isn&#39;t. Rather, &quot;filly&quot; is from Old Norse &quot;fylja.&quot;

The Word of the Day for October 26 is:

sleuth \\SLOOTH\\ verb

*intransitive verb : to act as a detective : search for information
transitive verb : to search for and discover

Example sentence:
After several employees complained of nausea, a shrewd bit of medical sleuthing turned up the culprit: a bacterium in the drinking water.

Did you know?
&quot;They were the footprints of a gigantic hound!&quot; Those canine tracks in Arthur Conan Doyle&#39;s The Hound of the Baskervilles set the great Sherlock Holmes sleuthing on the trail of a murderer. It was a case of art imitating etymology. When Middle English speakers first borrowed &quot;sleuth&quot; from Old Norse, the term referred to &quot;the track of an animal or person.&quot; In Scotland, a &quot;sleuthhound&quot; was a bloodhound used to hunt game or track down fugitives from justice. In 19th century U.S. English, &quot;sleuthhound&quot; became an epithet for a detective and was soon shortened to &quot;sleuth.&quot; From there, it was only a short leap to turning &quot;sleuth&quot; into a verb describing what a sleuth does.

Other descendants of &quot;confidere&quot; in English include &quot;confide,&quot; &quot;confidence,&quot; and &quot;confident,&quot; as well as &quot;confidential&quot; (which was formed from &quot;confidence&quot;).

The Word of the Day for October 28 is:

valetudinarian \\val-uh-too-duh-NAIR-ee-un\\ noun

: a person of a weak or sickly constitution; especially : one whose chief concern is being or becoming a chronic invalid

Example sentence:
Will complained constantly of his aches, pains, and sniffles; he was a terrible valetudinarian.

Did you know?
It&#39;s ironic that hypochondriacs and others who are convinced that their health is fragile often outlive their heartier compatriots. It is also ironic that &quot;valetudinarian,&quot; a word for someone who is sickly (or at least thinks he or she is) comes from &quot;valēre,&quot; a Latin word that means &quot;to be strong&quot; or &quot;to be well.&quot; Most of the English offspring of &quot;valēre&quot; imply having some kind of strength or force — consider, for instance, &quot;valiant,&quot; &quot;prevail,&quot; &quot;valor,&quot; and &quot;value.&quot; But the Latin &quot;valēre&quot; also gave rise to &quot;valetudo.&quot; In Latin, &quot;valetudo&quot; refers to one&#39;s state of health (whether good or bad), but by the time that root had given rise to &quot;valetudinarian&quot; in the early 1700s, English-speaking pessimists had given it a decidedly sickly spin.

The Word of the Day for October 29 is:

goldbrick \\GOLD-brik\\ noun

1 a : a worthless brick that appears to be of gold b : something that appears to be valuable but is actually worthless
*2 : a person who shirks assigned work

Example sentence:
While the goldbricks in the office were goofing off, Leslie was rushing to finish the project on time.

Did you know?
&quot;The gold brick swindle is an old one but it crops up constantly,&quot; states an 1881 National Police Gazette article referring to the con artist&#39;s practice of passing off bricks made of base metal as gold. By the time World War I was under way, the word &quot;goldbrick&quot; was associated with another sort of trickery. The sense of the word meaning &quot;shirker&quot; originated in the slang of the United States Army, where it referred to a soldier who feigned illness or injury in order to get out of work or service. That sense has since expanded in usage to refer to any person who avoids or tries to get out of his or her assignment.

The Word of the Day for October 30 is:

antic \\AN-tik\\ adjective

*1 : characterized by clownish extravagance or absurdity
2 : whimsically gay : frolicsome

Example sentence:
As the movie progresses, the characters become involved in a series of antic misadventures, each one funnier and more absurd than the last.

Did you know?
When Renaissance Italians began exploring the ancient Roman ruins around them, they discovered fantastic mural paintings that they called &quot;pitture grottesche&quot; (which means &quot;cave paintings,&quot; from the fact that they were found in caves, or &quot;grotte&quot;). Because they were so old, the murals were also called &quot;antichi,&quot; or &quot;ancient things.&quot; English speakers began to use &quot;antics,&quot; both for depictions that are incongruous, caricatured, and ludicrous (such as gargoyles, which we now might refer to as &quot;grotesques&quot;) and for ludicrous or outlandish behavior. Within 20 years of its earliest recorded uses as a noun, &quot;antic&quot; appeared as an English adjective. Originally, it meant &quot;grotesque&quot; or &quot;bizarre&quot; (a sense now considered archaic), but today it means &quot;playful, funny, or absurd&quot; and the noun means &quot;a wildly playful or funny act.&quot;

The Word of the Day for October 31 is:

lamia \\LAY-mee-uh\\ noun

: a female demon : vampire

Example sentence:
In his latest horror flick, a seductive lamia revengefully preys upon the young men of a suburban town, who, it turns out, were responsible for her brutal death.

Did you know?
According to Greek mythology, Lamia was a queen of Libya who was beloved by Zeus. When Hera, Zeus&#39;s wife, robbed her of her children from this union, Lamia killed every child she could get into her power. Stories were also told of a fiend named Lamia who, in the form of a beautiful woman, seduced young men in order to devour them and who also sucked the blood of children. Such nightmarish legends uncannily compelled poet John Keats, and many other writers before and after him, to write their own tales of Lamia, which still haunt and terrify those souls who dare read them.
