The Word of the Day for August 20 is:


vituperate \\vy-TOO-puh-rayt\\ verb

*1 : to abuse or censure severely or abusively : berate
2 : to use harsh condemnatory language

Example sentence:
The author was vituperated by many critics in the media for lifting material from other sources without properly attributing them.

Did you know?
&quot;Vituperate&quot; first appeared in English in the mid-16th century. It derives from the past participle of the Latin verb &quot;vituperare,&quot; which was formed as a combination of two Latin words: the noun &quot;vitium,&quot; meaning &quot;fault,&quot; and the verb &quot;parare,&quot; meaning &quot;to make or prepare.&quot; (&quotarare&quot; helped give us a number of other English words, such as &quot;prepare,&quot; &quot;separate,&quot; &quot;apparatus,&quot; and even &quot;emperor.&quot;) &quot;Vituperate&quot; possesses several close synonyms, including &quot;berate&quot; and &quot;revile.&quot; &quot;Berate&quot; usually refers to scolding that is drawn out and abusive. &quot;Revile&quot; means to attack or criticize in a way prompted by anger or hatred. &quot;Vituperate&quot; adds to the meaning of &quot;revile&quot; by stressing an attack that is particularly violent in nature.

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

The Word of the Day for August 21 is:

funambulism \\fyoo-NAM-byuh-liz-um\\ noun

1 : tightrope walking
*2 : a show especially of mental agility

Example sentence:
As a game-show contestant Brian amazed us all with his funambulism, answering every question correctly to win the $10,000 first prize.

Did you know?
Back in ancient Rome, tightrope walking was a popular spectacle at public gatherings. The Latin word for &quot;tightrope walker&quot; is &quot;funambulus,&quot; from the Latin &quot;funis,&quot; meaning &quot;rope,&quot; plus &quot;ambulare,&quot; meaning &quot;to walk.&quot; It doesn&#39;t take any funambulism on our part to see how the word for an impressive act of physical skill and agility came to mean an impressive act of mental skill or agility. That extended sense of the word has been around since at least 1886, when British academic and writer Augustus Jessopp described the act of diagramming sentences as &quot;horrible lessons of ghastly grammar and dreary funambulism.&quot;

The Word of the Day for August 22 is:

microburst \\MY-kroh-burst\\ noun

: a violent short-lived localized downdraft that creates extreme wind shears at low altitudes and is usually associated with thunderstorms

Example sentence:
During thunderstorm season, microbursts as strong as 50 miles per hour can cause almost as much damage to crops and structures as a full-force hurricane.

Did you know?
Credit for the invention of the word &quot;microburst&quot; is generally given to tornado expert Tetsuya Theodore Fujita. Fujita first described these extremely intense wind patterns in 1974. He noted that microbursts are usually short-lived, lasting only 5 to 15 minutes, but that they are extremely dangerous, especially for aircraft, because they cause sudden unexpected changes in wind direction or speed. Since the mid-1970s, many airports have installed Doppler radar systems to help them detect potentially deadly microbursts.

The Word of the Day for August 24 is:


mitigate \\MIT-uh-gayt\\ verb

1 : to cause to become less harsh or hostile : mollify
*2 : to make less severe or painful : alleviate
3 : extenuate

Example sentence:
Gordon was determined to help mitigate the suffering of the people in the tornado-ravaged area.

Did you know?
Would it be correct to say, &quot;His boyish appearance mitigated against his getting an early promotion&quot;? Most usage commentators would say &quot;no.&quot; They feel such examples demonstrate a long-standing confusion between &quot;mitigate&quot; and the look-alike &quot;militate.&quot; Those two words are not closely related etymologically (&quot;mitigate&quot; descends from the Latin verb &quot;mitigare,&quot; meaning &quot;to soften,&quot; whereas &quot;militate&quot; traces to &quot;militare,&quot; another Latin verb that means &quot;to engage in warfare&quot;), nor are they particularly close in meaning (&quot;militate&quot; means &quot;to have weight or effect&quot;). The confusion between the two has existed for long enough that one commentator thinks &quot;mitigate against&quot; should be accepted as an idiomatic alternative to &quot;militate,&quot; but if you want to avoid criticism, you should keep &quot;mitigate&quot; and &quot;militate&quot; distinct.

The Word of the Day for August 25 is:

puerile \\PYUR-ul\\ adjective

1 : juvenile
*2 : childish, silly

Example sentence:
Beth&#39;s husband likes pulling puerile gags with handshake buzzers and dribble glasses.

Did you know?
&quotuerile&quot; may call to mind qualities of youth and immaturity, but the term itself is no spring chicken. On the contrary, it&#39;s been around for more than three centuries, and its predecessors in French and Latin, the adjectives &quot;puéril&quot; and &quot;puerilis,&quot; respectively, are far older. Those two terms have the same basic meaning as the English word &quot;puerile,&quot; and they both trace to the Latin noun &quot;puer,&quot; meaning &quot;boy&quot; or &quot;child.&quot; Nowadays, &quot;puerile&quot; can describe the acts or utterances of an actual child, but it more often refers (usually with marked disapproval) to occurrences of childishness where adult maturity would be expected or preferred.

The Word of the Day for August 26 is:

schlemiel \\shluh-MEEL\\ noun

: an unlucky bungler : chump

Example sentence:
&quot;What a schlemiel,&quot; sighed Evelyn, watching Frank trip his way up to the bowling lane and throw yet another ball straight into the gutter.

Did you know?
&quot;Schlemiel,&quot; from the Yiddish &quot;shlemil,&quot; has sometimes been associated with Peter Schlemihl, the hero of a story about an unlucky man who sold his shadow to the devil, by German writer Adelbert von Chamisso. While this story may have helped to popularize &quot;schlemiel,&quot; the word probably has much older roots. The Hebrew name &quot;Shelumiel&quot; is mentioned in the Bible (Numbers 1:6), and the Talmud describes Shelumiel as a man whose behavior earned derision and an unfortunate fate. A &quot;schlemiel&quot; in modern English usage is a chronic blunderer or loser, and is sometimes paired with the less common &quot;schlimazel,&quot; another Yiddish word for an unlucky or bungling person.
