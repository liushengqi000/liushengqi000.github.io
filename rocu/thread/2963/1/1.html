yourdictionary.com:

Pith (noun)

Pronunciation: [pith] Listen

Definition: (1) The central core of elongated objects, such as the center of a flower stem, feather, horn, or log. (2) The metaphorical core or center, the essence as a source of strength, as the pith of an idea (or an idea without pith).

Usage: Today&#39;s is a lovely word that is used less often than the adjective derived from it: &quot;pithy.&quot; For centuries it was featured in the popular idiom &quot;pith and marrow,&quot; as those who are the very pith and marrow of a club, which is to say, the very heart of it. Of course, when the British were traipsing about the tropics at the turn of the last century, they often wore really cool pith helmets, made of the soft pith of the solah or spongewood plant of Bengal, pressed, molded, and encased in cloth.

Suggested Usage: Botanists use today&#39;s word a lot in referring to the inner core of plant stems. If you are not a botanist, we recommend that you use &quot;pith&quot; in its metaphoric sense. &quot;Andrea, I will strive for your love with all my pith,&quot; would be a lovely sentiment to express to the object of your affection on St. Valentine&#39;s day (if you don&#39;t lisp). In the 3rd Act of Hamlet, Shakespeare writes of &quot;[e]nterprizes of great pith and moment.&quot; We can use the word in that sense, too, &quot;The pith of the matter is that today&#39;s word is much too pithy to be left out of our vocabularies.&quot;

Etymology: Today&#39;s word and &quot;pit,&quot; in the sense of a fruit pit, descend from the same source. In English the final consonant changed to [th] but in Dutch, it did not. So English, as it is wont to do, kept its own &quot;pith,&quot; borrowed &quot;pit&quot; from Dutch, and ended up with two words for the history of one.

   –Dr. Language, YourDictionary.com

When &quot;fountainhead&quot; first came into English in the late 1500s, it meant only the source of a stream. Over time, &quot;fountainhead&quot; came to mean any original or primary source, be it an idea, place, or person. Often, a fountainhead is a person who inspires a new way of thinking or of doing something. &quot;I coined this word. Could you tell me how I can get it into a dictionary?&quot; From time to time I&#39;m asked questions like this by folks wondering how to get a word to take up residence in the hallowed halls of lexiconia.

Thousands of new words do enter the dictionaries every year. So, what&#39;s the criterion for their inclusion? What does a word have to do to be worthy of being called &#39;legitimate&#39;? Who decides what&#39;s a good word and what isn&#39;t?

Usage is the single most important factor to determine if a word gains membership of that exclusive club. It has to appear extensively, in many different sources, such as newspapers, magazines, books, TV, radio, Internet, etc., over several years to show that it is gaining currency. It has to fill a need and describe a phenomenon for which no other word exists. Also, it doesn&#39;t hurt if the word is catchy and captures public imagination.

Dictionary editors read a wide variety of sources to monitor the language. They take notes -- known as citations -- on little index cards or in a computer database. Once there is enough evidence, they consider whether to include it in the next edition of their dictionary, and if the answer is yes, they work to define it precisely.

So how do you win that honor for your little baby? It&#39;s not easy. Share it with family and friends, use it, and encourage them to publish letters, articles, stories using that word. And even if it doesn&#39;t make it into the dictionary, remember that it&#39;s still a bona fide word -- nothing in the definition of the word &#39;word&#39; says that a word has to be in a dictionary to be called one.

Have fun coining words, and enjoy this week&#39;s coinages that did reach the dictionaries.
