Sentence Completion

In this type of question you are given a sentence with one or two words missing.
Below it are five choices, and you must select from among them the one with the correct word, or two words, to insert in the blank.
The correct word will be the one that best fits the sentence. There is always some good reason to consider it the best fit.
Questions of this type will begin easy and get progressively harder.
The questions get harder because the words get tougher and because the sentences get more complicated, so the meaning becomes more difficult to grasp.
We think you can do well on the sentence completions if you follow some simple advice
Our first and most important tip is: don&#39;t look at the answers.
Okay, obviously you&#39;ll have to look at the answers in order to pick the best one.
But we&#39;re serious when we say don&#39;t look right away. We mean don&#39;t look UNTIL you have done your very best to accomplish the following two goals:
1.    Understand the overall meaning of the sentence.
2.    Have a pretty good idea of what word, or at least what kind of word, should go in the blank.
Let&#39;s take these goals one at a time...
What do we mean by &quot;Understand the overall meaning of the sentence&quot;?
You might think that this is hard to do when there are words missing.
Don&#39;t worry, the missing words do not make the sentence incomprehensible, they just make it more challenging to understand. You can still get a good feeling for what the sentence is trying to say.
The trick is to pay close attention to key words in the sentence. In particular, look for logical connecting words and phrases such as and, while, because of, due to, despite or ever since, which give you clues as to what the sentence is saying.
Consider these two examples:
Because of the heavy rainfall in the spring, the tenants were ---- about flooding.
Despite the heavy rainfall, the tenants were ---- about flooding.
What kind of word fits in the blank in the top sentence? Well, if it is raining a lot, then it is natural for people to be worried about flooding.
So the word worried or concerned might fit in the blank. We know this from the opening words of the sentence which say: &quot;Because of the heavy rainfall.&quot;
Now the second sentence begins with the word despite, which is telling us that the tenants are feeling something even though there had been a lot of rain.
The meaning of the word in the blank is therefore probably one that means the opposite of what you would expect the tenants to be feeling if there was heavy rain.
So in the second sentence we should look for an answer that means unconcerned and not worried.
There will always be a clue as to the logical structure of the sentence in the words that are available.
In the previous example we saw that if you grasp the meaning or intent of the sentence, then you&#39;ll naturally have some feel for what goes in the blank.
Sometimes, as in the rainfall example, you&#39;ll be able to put in almost the exact word.
At other times, you might only be able to tell whether the word that goes in the blank has a positive or negative sense. The next page gives you an example...
Sally was popular and ----, and received an overwhelming majority of the votes for student council president.
It&#39;s not immediately clear what word should go in the blank, but you know for sure that it is a word with a positive meaning.
Possible words include: respected, admired, intelligent, talented, responsible. Any of these words could make the sentence complete.
What they have in common is that they are all strongly positive words describing traits or attributes that might make someone a good choice to be a student council president
If you approach the five possible answers with a strong intuition for the type of word that you are looking for two very good things happen.
First, there&#39;s a chance you&#39;ll see the exact word you had in mind. If this happens you&#39;re in great shape; it&#39;s almost guaranteed to be right.
Second, you&#39;ll have a lot easier time rejecting the wrong answers. You&#39;ll have a stronger sense that they don&#39;t complete the sentence in the way you think the sentence should be completed.
Let&#39;s practice this technique a couple of times. When you click for the next page, you&#39;ll see the sentence with the blank, but not the answers. Take a moment to grasp the meaning and come up with your own word. Then click for the five answer choices:
While some dancers at the ball were as smooth as professionals, others were clearly still ---- in their movements .
1.Help! I think I know what I&#39;m doing; tell me if I&#39;m on the right track. (The sentence contrasts smooth professional dancers with other dancers. So the missing word, which describes the other dancers, must mean something quite different from smooth or professional.
Some good possibilities are amateurish, clumsy, or hesitant. )
2.I&#39;ve got it! I&#39;ve got the meaning down and I&#39;ve selected my own word. I&#39;ll click to the next page to see the answers....
While some dancers at the ball were as smooth as professionals, others were clearly still ---- in their movements.
     graceful
     able
     agile
     uncertain
     despondent

While some dancers at the ball were as smooth as professionals, others were clearly still ---- in their movements.
     graceful
     able
     agile
     uncertain
     despondent
First off, with this one you need to understand that the word while is telling you to contrast the dancing of some of the couples with that of very professional couples. It&#39;s a safe bet, then, that the adjective used to describe these other dancers will be something bad - like clumsy or amateur or something like that.
When you saw the five answers, the first three were all positive. This means that none of them is appropriate, and on the real SAT you should have crossed them out definitively.
By contrast, the last two words are negative.
Uncertain means that these couples are unsure of their movements.
The word despondent has a very strong negative connotation. But it means to be depressed or to have lost hope, and is thus not an appropriate contrast with the word smooth.
Therefore, uncertain is the best choice.
Let&#39;s try one more.
Although the first eighteen miles of the Boston marathon are downhill, they are followed by a series of ----; the ---- terrain makes the event far more difficult than other races.
1.    Help! I think I know what I&#39;m doing; tell me if I&#39;m on the right track.
2.    I&#39;ve got it! I&#39;ve got the meaning down and I&#39;ve selected my own word. I&#39;ll click to the next page to see the answers....
Although the first eighteen miles of the Boston marathon are downhill, they are followed by a series of ----; the ---- terrain makes the event far more difficult than other races.
     inclines . . varied
     slopes . . contiguous
     intersections . . consecrated
     hills . . uniform
     curves . . slippery
Click on the answer choices to learn more about each choice. When you&#39;re ready, go on to the next page.
Although the first eighteen miles of the Boston marathon are downhill, they are followed by a series of ----; the ---- terrain makes the event far more difficult than other races.
     inclines . . varied
     slopes . . contiguous
     intersections . . consecrated
     hills . . uniform
     curves . . slippery
As you read the sentence, you will have recognized that it is describing the course of the Boston marathon as not very flat. The word although tells us that the first blank needs to be filled with something that contrasts with downhill. The second blank needs a word that means uneven.
If you were to look exclusively at the words for the second blank, you&#39;d notice right away that only one fits. Only the word varied describes the terrain as composed of uphills and downhills. So, the answer is (A).
If you always attempt to predict the answer, you will be able to approach these questions more confidently.
In our classes, we frequently see what happens when students don&#39;t use this technique. Instead, they read the sentence and then try to use the answer words to help them understand it. This will only lead you into trouble. You have to figure the meaning out for yourself before you turn to the answers.
Here are a few other things to keep in mind as you work through sentence completion problems...
Tip 1: Eliminate wrong answers
When an answer has a word that you know is wrong, cross it off with your pencil immediately. Even if you still aren&#39;t exactly sure which answer is right, keep eliminating answers until you can&#39;t eliminate any more. Then go ahead and guess: you&#39;ve earned yourself a better chance at getting points.
Tip 2: Solve two-blank problems one word at a time.
If you recognize that some of the answers contain words that do not fit - for either the first or second blank - then strike those out.
It doesn&#39;t matter if the other word fits: either both words fit, or the answer is wrong.
Tip 3: Don&#39;t force a word into the sentence
If the only way you can make a word fit into the sentence is by distorting a fairly straightforward understanding of what the sentence means, then you are probably choosing the wrong word.
The right word will almost always fit quite smoothly into the sentence.
Tip 4: Go back and check your answer
After you put the whole sentence together there&#39;s no guarantee that you&#39;ll know for sure it&#39;s right (as there is, say, in the math problems).
However, this is a very good way to check that you didn&#39;t accidentally make an obvious mistake.
The sentence should make sense to you when you read it with all the words.
If you keep these suggestions in mind, you should find yourself getting the hang of sentence completions fairly quickly. Like all parts of the SAT, though, the more you practice them, the more skilled you&#39;ll become at these questions.
