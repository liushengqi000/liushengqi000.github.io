One guess is that when a Venetian glassblower discovered a flaw developing in a beautiful piece he was working on, he would turn it into an ordinary bottle to avoid having to destroy the object. The Word of the Day for March 06 is:


saltation \\sal-TAY-shun\\ noun

1 *a : the action or process of leaping or jumping b : dance
2 a : the origin of a new species or a higher taxon in essentially a single evolutionary step that in some especially former theories is held to be due to a major mutation or to unknown causes b : mutation

Example sentence:
The kangaroo, the frog, and the flea all move by means of saltation, using their powerful hind legs to propel their bodies through the air.

Did you know?
&quot;Saltation&quot; comes from Latin, deriving ultimately from the verb &quot;salire,&quot; meaning &quot;to leap.&quot; Etymologists think it meant &quot;leap&quot; or &quot;jump&quot; when it was first used in English, too, but documented evidence of early use in that sense is scarce. Instead, the oldest manuscripts containing the word (which date from the 1620s) show it used as a synonym of &quot;dancing.&quot; The first recorded incidence of the &quot;leaping&quot; sense dates from 1646, when British physician and author Sir Thomas Browne used it in an entomological context: &quot;Locusts ... being ordained for saltation, their hinder legs doe far exceed the other.&quot; The word made the leap to evolutionary theory in the late 19th century.

Example sentence:
There&#39;s no need to derogate your sister&#39;s accomplishments just because you&#39;re jealous.

Did you know?
You&#39;re probably familiar with &quot;derogatory,&quot; the adjective meaning &quot;expressing a low opinion,&quot; but you may not have heard of the less common verb &quot;derogate.&quot; Both words can be traced back to the Late Latin word &quot;derogatus,&quot; the past participle of the verb &quot;derogare,&quot; meaning &quot;to detract&quot; or &quot;to annul (a law).&quot; &quot;Derogare,&quot; in turn, derives from the Latin word for &quot;ask,&quot; &quot;rogare.&quot; &quot;Derogate&quot; first appeared in English in the 15th century. &quot;Derogatory&quot; was adopted in the early 16th century, and has become much more popular than the verb. Other &quot;derogate&quot; relatives include &quot;derogative,&quot; &quot;derogation,&quot; and &quot;derogatorily.&quot;

The Word of the Day for March 08 is:

advocate \\AD-vuh-kayt\\ verb

: to plead in favor of

Example sentence:
Many parents throughout the district have long advocated full-day kindergarten.

Did you know?
Benjamin Franklin may have been a great innovator in science and politics, but on the subject of &quot;advocate,&quot; he was against change. In 1789, he wrote a letter to his compatriot Noah Webster complaining about a &quot;new word&quot;: the verb &quot;advocate.&quot; Like others of his day, Franklin knew &quot;advocate&quot; primarily as a noun meaning &quot;one who pleads the cause of another,&quot; and he urged Webster to condemn the verb&#39;s use. Actually, the verb wasn&#39;t as new as Franklin assumed (etymologists have traced it back to 1599), though it was apparently surging in popularity in his day. Webster evidently did not heed Franklin&#39;s plea. His famous 1828 dictionary, An American Dictionary of the English Language, entered both the noun and the verb senses of &quot;advocate.&quot;

The Word of the Day for March 09 is:

kitsch \\KITCH\\ noun

*1 : something that appeals to popular or lowbrow taste and is often of poor quality
2 : a tacky or lowbrow quality or condition

Example sentence:
On the route up the mountain, there is plenty of kitsch (from teepees to giant dinosaur replicas) to entice campers to visit the roadside shops.

Did you know?
&quot;The fashionable clothing label...kicked off the revival last June..., putting its models in [Carmen] Miranda-inspired swimsuits and marching them through a gantlet of 50 tons of bananas,&quot; writes Mac Margolis in Newsweek International (January 2006) of a fabulously kitschy gala commemoration for the late Brazilian singer and actress Carmen Miranda. Since we borrowed &quot;kitsch&quot; from German in the 1920s, it has been our word for things in the realm of popular culture that dangle, like car mirror dice, precariously close to tackiness. But although things that can be described with &quot;kitsch&quot; and the related adjective &quot;kitschy&quot; are clearly not fine art, they may appeal to certain tastes — some folks delight in velvet paintings, plastic flamingos, dashboard hula dancers, and Carmen Miranda revivals!

The Word of the Day for March 10 is:

fictioneer \\fik-shuh-NEER\\ noun

: one who writes fiction especially in quantity and without high standards

Example sentence:
Dwight was a fictioneer who specialized in pulp novels, producing over 300 of them in his long career.

Did you know?
In Latin, the verb &quot;fingere&quot; means &quot;to shape, fashion, or feign.&quot; Fictioneers surely do shape stories and feign the truth, so you could say that the noun &quot;fictioneer&quot; is etymologically true to its ancestor. The word &quot;fiction&quot; had been around for more than 500 years by the time &quot;fictioneer&quot; appeared in English in 1923, bearing a suffix that harks back to such words as &quot;engineer&quot; and &quot;pamphleteer.&quot; The word is used generally to refer to any writer of fiction but often specifically to one who writes with little concern for literary quality. &quot;Fictioneer&quot; and &quot;fiction&quot; aren&#39;t the only English feigners and shapers born of &quot;fingere.&quot; The words &quot;effigy,&quot; &quot;feign,&quot; and &quot;figment&quot; are among others that trace back to that Latin verb.

The Word of the Day for March 11 is:

anfractuous \\an-FRAK-chuh-wus\\ adjective

: full of windings and intricate turnings : tortuous

Example sentence:
&quot;The film is finally sunk not by its anfractuous plot but by its weight of whimsy.&quot; (Nigel Andrews, Financial Times, March 30, 1984)

Did you know?
Plots and paths can be anfractuous. They twist and turn but do not break. Never mind that our English word comes from Latin &quot;anfractus&quot; (same meaning as &quot;anfractuous&quot;), which in turn comes from the Latin verb &quot;frangere,&quot; meaning &quot;to break.&quot; (&quot;Frangere&quot; is also the source of &quot;fracture,&quot; &quot;fraction,&quot; &quot;fragment,&quot; and &quot;frail.&quot;) The prefix &quot;an-&quot; here means &quot;around&quot; (from &quot;ambi-&quot;). At first, &quot;anfractuous&quot; was all about ears and the auditory canal&#39;s anfractuosity, that is, its being curved rather than straight. Now &quot;anfractuous&quot; has been around some 400 years, without a break, giving it plenty of time to wind its way into other applications; e.g., there can be an anfractuous thought process, or an anfractuous shoreline.
