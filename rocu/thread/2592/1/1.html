As you know, I believe good Sino-Japanese relations are in the interest of people in both countries and essential to long-term peace and stability in the region.  Unfortunately the recent protests in China have inflamed opinions in both countries and made the task of preserving long-term stability in the region more difficult.



            *The protests occurred just when China was considering protesting Japan’s membership in the United Nations Security Council. Now, after Wen Jiabao said the Japanese do not deserve to be a regular member of the Security Council, Chinese leaders are taking stronger steps to curb the protests. In explaining their position on Japan’s Security Council membership, Chinese leaders can say they were responding to the strong wishes of the Chinese people in protesting Japanese regular membership in the Security Council.

            *The residence of the Japanese ambassador in Beijing was pelted for some four hours. Police did not stop the protestors and reports are that no one was arrested. Some Japanese shops in China have been damaged and police did not fully protect property owners. Bus loads were organized to carry students to protest and return them to campus. Protests could not have continued on such a scale if high level leaders had firmly opposed protests.

            *In the 1980s, China criticized textbooks and Yasukuni Shrine but Deng Xiaoping strongly and publicly supported good relations with Japan. At that time, Japan and China were united against their cold war enemy, the Soviet Union, and Japanese aid and technology assistance to China were at very high levels. Since the 1980s, there has not been enough change in how Japanese handled textbooks or Yasukuni to explain the difference in China’s handling of relations to Japan between the 1980s and now. The changes in Chinese attitudes cannot be explained without taking into consideration the role of Chinese official media, as well as the internet, in handling information about Japan.

            *If the purpose of Chinese demonstrations is to weaken the power of rightists in Japan and to win the support of Japanese people for friendship in the future, they have failed in their purpose. The attacks on Japanese have strengthened anti-Chinese sentiment in Japan and the power of the right in Japan, making it more difficult for top Japanese political leaders not to visit Yasukuni. If the purpose is to unite Chinese to dampen other possible protests within China in the cause of unified nationalism, they have perhaps succeeded as long as the anti-Japanese sentiments do not get so strong as to get out of control. If the purpose is to build a strong case against Japanese entry into a regular seat in the Security Council, they have succeeded in their purpose.

           *When Chinese talk about Yasukuni, Japanese textbooks, and Japanese politician’s statements about World War II, their comments sound more like political expressions than a simple reflection of sentiments of Chinese whose family suffered from Japanese cruelty or an objective balanced description of Japanese realities. The Chinese public generally knows only about the most extreme cases of Japanese textbooks and does not have an adequate understanding of the range of Japanese textbooks and understanding of Japanese attitudes. They know about Japanese white-washing of war guilt in the Yasukuni Shrine Museum but do not know of the more balanced handling of the World War II in museums like those in Hiroshima and Nagasaki. The Chinese incorrectly believe that when the Japanese prime minister visits Yasukuni Shrine it signifies that the Japanese are denying the horrible things they did in World War II.

            *The continued visits by Prime Minister Koizumi to the Yasukuni Shrine and the permission of the Japanese Ministry of Education to allow the use of some textbooks which have downplayed the horrors of Japanese aggression in World War II make it much easier for Chinese leaders to mobilize anti-Japanese sentiments.

            *It is too early to tell how much Japanese industrial investment in China and tourism to China will be affected by the protests, but there is likely to be some impact. The hardening of attitudes in Japan as a result of the protests is likely to strengthen Japan’s determination to engage in energy competition with China. World opinion is likely to be critical of China for not apologizing and not compensating Japanese owners for damage to Japanese property in China.  If Chinese protests against Japanese goods continue, Japanese public’s attitude toward purchasing Chinese products may also be affected and foreigners’ attitudes toward participation in the Olympics could be affected.



In my view, the chances of good relations between China and Japan would be enhanced if:

1) The Japanese prime minister would stop visiting Yasukuni Shrine, the Japanese Ministry of Education would be more vigilant in stopping textbooks that whitewash Japanese actions in World War II and if Japan would take the initiative to invite China to set up joint groups of Chinese and Japanese to carry on joint research about what happened in World War II and examine textbooks in both countries openly and freely. They would examine both Chinese and Japanese textbooks.

2) Chinese leaders would allow Japanese culture (movies, books, songs) to be widely known to the Chinese public as they began to do in the 1980s. Chinese leaders would do more to let the Chinese people know about the extent to which Japan has pursued a peaceful policy after World War II, the extent of Japanese technological and financial aid to China. They would do more to give a balanced picture of Japanese attitudes and Japanese textbooks. They would list to the Chinese public an accurate account of the times and nature of Japanese leaders’ apologies to China.

3) China would support Japan’s membership in the UN Security Council. The membership of the Security Council was determined at the end of 1945 and was based on the victors, not the vanquished. The world has changed, Japan is the second largest economy, Japan is the second largest contributor to the UN and it has played a very constructive peaceful role in the world, providing in some years more aid to other countries than any other country.

4) As constitutional revision becomes an issue in Japan, Japanese would take care to explain that the revision of their constitution, which is a natural development for any country that once had its constitution imposed by others, is not anti-Chinese and is consistent with preserving world peace and if China publicizes more the mainstream of Japanese attitudes toward constitutional revision rather than minority extremist views.

5) If other countries (the United States, European countries, and others), both in government and private organizations, would encourage Sino-Japanese cooperation and understanding.

Ezra F. Vogel, Harvard University
