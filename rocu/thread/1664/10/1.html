The Word of the Day for November 11 is:

skulk \\SKULK\\ verb

1 : to move in a stealthy or furtive manner
2 *a : to hide or conceal something (as oneself) often out of cowardice or fear or with sinister intent b chiefly British : malinger

Example sentence:
During the thunderstorm, we realized that we hadn&#39;t seen our dog in a while; when we searched, we found her skulking under the bed.

Did you know?
Here&#39;s one for the word-puzzle lovers. Can you name three things that the word &quot;skulk&quot; has in common with all of these other words: &quot;booth,&quot; &quot;brink,&quot; &quot;cog,&quot; &quot;flit,&quot; &quot;give,&quot; &quot;kid,&quot; &quot;meek,&quot; &quot;scab,&quot; &quot;seem,&quot; &quot;skull,&quot; &quot;snub,&quot; and &quot;wing&quot;? If you noticed that all of the terms on that list have just one syllable, then you&#39;ve got the first (easy) similarity, but the next two are likely to prove a little harder to guess. Give up? All of the words listed above are of Scandinavian origin and all were first recorded in English in the 13th century. As for &quot;skulk,&quot; its closest Scandinavian relative is Norwegian dialect &quot;skulka,&quot; which means &quot;to lie in wait&quot; or &quot;lurk.&quot;

The Word of the Day for November 13 is:

flippant \\FLIP-unt\\ adjective

: lacking proper respect or seriousness

Example sentence:
The young attorney reddened when she realized the reply she&#39;d just given the frowning judge bordered on flippant.

Did you know?
&quot;Flippant&quot; was probably created from the verb &quot;flip,&quot; which in turn likely originated as an imitation of the sound of something flipping. Among early senses of the adjective were &quot;nimble&quot; and &quot;limber.&quot; One could be flippant not only on one&#39;s feet, but also in speech; that is, someone &quot;flippant&quot; might have a capacity for easy, flowing speech. But people who speak freely and easily can sometimes seem too talkative, even impertinent, and from the beginning &quot;flippant&quot; referred to such overly glib speech as well. By the end of the 18th century, the flip-flop was complete — the positive sense of &quot;flippant&quot; had slipped from use, and the &quot;disrespectful&quot; sense had taken over.

The Word of the Day for November 14 is:

praxis \\PRAK-sis\\ noun

1 : exercise or practicing of an art, science, or skill
2 : customary practice or conduct
*3 : practical application of a theory

Example sentence:
Working in a law office allowed Anne to see firsthand the praxis of the ideas she learned in her classes.

Did you know?
It&#39;s one thing to have an idea or knowledge about something, and quite another to turn that knowledge into action; the word &quot;praxis&quot; is often called upon to represent the latter of these. First appearing in English in the 16th century, &quot;praxis&quot; came to our language via Medieval Latin and ultimately derives from the Greek verb &quot;prassein,&quot; meaning &quot;to do&quot; or &quot;to practice.&quot; That Greek verb also gave us such words as &quot;practical&quot; and &quot;pragmatic.&quot; Something that is done as praxis applies an abstract theory toward practical purposes, and for that reason &quot;praxis&quot; is often used in contexts that pointedly contrast it with &quot;theory.&quot;

The Word of the Day for November 15 is:

waggish \\WAG-ish\\ adjective

*1 : resembling or characteristic of a wag
2 : done or made in waggery or for sport : humorous

Example sentence:
Lisa listens to the same waggish DJ every morning, never tiring of his prank phone calls and irreverent impressions of local politicians.

Did you know?
One who is waggish acts like a wag. What, then, is a wag? Etymologists think &quot;wag&quot; probably came from &quot;waghalter,&quot; a word that was once used for a &quot;gallows bird&quot; (that is, a person who was going to be, or deserved to be, hanged). &quot;Waghalter&quot; was apparently shortened to &quot;wag&quot; and used jokingly or affectionately for mischievous pranksters or youths. Hence a wag is a joker, and waggery is merriment or practical joking. &quot;Waggish&quot; can describe the prank itself as well as the prankster type; the class clown might be said to be prone to &quot;waggish antics&quot; or even to have a &quot;waggish disposition.&quot;

The Word of the Day for November 16 is:

quietus \\kwy-EE-tus\\ noun 1 : final settlement (as of a debt)
2 : removal from activity; especially : death
*3 : something that quiets or represses

Example sentence:
&quot;The constant rain and the cold have combined to put a quietus on outdoor activities.&quot; (Glenn Tucker, quoted in the Bangor Daily News [Maine], Oct. 10, 2005)

Did you know?
In the early 1500s, English speakers adopted the Medieval Latin phrase &quot;quietus est&quot; (literally &quot;he is quit&quot;) as the name for the writ of discharge exempting a baron or knight from payment of a knight&#39;s fee to the king. The _expression was later shortened to &quot;quietus&quot; and applied to the termination of any debt. William Shakespeare was the first to use &quot;quietus&quot; as a metaphor for the termination of life in his tragedy Hamlet: &quot;For who would bear the whips and scorns of time, ... When he himself might his quietus make / With a bare bodkin?&quot; The third meaning, which is more influenced by &quot;quiet&quot; than &quot;quit,&quot; appeared in the 19th century. It often occurs in the phrase &quot;put the quietus on&quot; (as in our example sentence).

The Word of the Day for November 17 is:

alleviate \\uh-LEE-vee-ayt\\ verb

: relieve, lessen: as *a : to make (as suffering) more bearable b : to partially remove or correct

Example sentence:
Mom suggested that ibuprofen and tea would perhaps alleviate some of the misery of my cold.

Did you know?
&quot;Alleviate&quot; derives from the past participle of Late Latin &quot;alleviare&quot; (&quot;to lighten or relieve?), which in turn was formed by combining the prefix &quot;ad-&quot; and the adjective &quot;levis,&quot; a Latin word meaning &quot;light&quot; or &quot;having little weight.&quot; (&quot;Levis&quot; comes from the same ancient word that gave rise to that &quot;light&quot; in English.) We acquired &quot;alleviate&quot; in the 15th century, and for the first few centuries the word could mean either &quot;to cause (something) to have less weight&quot; or &quot;to make (something) more tolerable.&quot; The literal &quot;make lighter&quot; sense is no longer used, however, so today we have only the &quot;relieve&quot; sense. Incidentally, not only is &quot;alleviate&quot; a synonym of &quot;relieve,&quot; it&#39;s also a cousin; &quot;relieve&quot; comes from &quot;levare&quot; (&quot;to raise&quot;), which in turn comes from &quot;levis.&quot;

bumbershoot \\BUM-ber-shoot\\ noun

: umbrella

Example sentence:
As the light sprinkle gradually turned into steady rain, Uncle Winston said, &quot;I&#39;m glad I remembered to carry my bumbershoot!&quot;

Did you know?
Umbrellas have plenty of nicknames. In Britain, &quot;brolly&quot; is a popular alternative to the more staid &quot;umbrella.&quot; Sarah Gamp, a fictional nurse who toted a particularly large umbrella in Charles Dickens&#39; novel Martin Chuzzlewit, has inspired some English speakers to dub oversize versions &quot;gamps.&quot; &quot;Bumbershoot&quot; is a predominantly American nickname, one that has been recorded as a whimsical, slightly irreverent handle for umbrellas since the late 1890s. As with most slang terms, the origins of &quot;bumbershoot&quot; are a bit foggy, but it appears that the &quot;bumber&quot; is a modification of the &quot;umbr-&quot; in &quot;umbrella&quot; and the &quot;shoot&quot; is an alteration of the &quot;-chute&quot; in &quot;parachute&quot; (since an open parachute looks a little like an umbrella).

The Word of the Day for November 19 is:

utile \\YOO-til\\ adjective

: useful

Example sentence:
Shaker crafts are simple, meticulously constructed, pleasing to the eye, and eminently utile, all at the same time.

Did you know?
For over a hundred years before &quot;useful&quot; entered our language, &quot;utile&quot; served us well on its own. We borrowed &quot;utile&quot; from Middle French in the 15th century. The French derived it from Latin &quot;utilis,&quot; meaning &quot;useful,&quot; which in turn comes from &quot;uti,&quot; meaning &quot;to use.&quot; &quot;Uti&quot; (the past participle of which is &quot;usus&quot;) is also the source of our &quot;use&quot; and &quot;useful.&quot; We&#39;ve been using &quot;use&quot; since at least the 13th century, but we didn&#39;t acquire &quot;useful&quot; until the late 16th century, when William Shakespeare inserted it into King John. Needless to say, we&#39;ve come to prefer &quot;useful&quot; over &quot;utile&quot; since then, though &quot;utile&quot; functions as a very usable synonym. Other handy terms derived from &quot;uti&quot; include &quot;utilize,&quot; &quot;usury,&quot; &quot;abuse,&quot; and &quot;utensil.&quot;

The Word of the Day for November 20 is:

linchpin \\LINCH-pin\\ noun

1 : a locking pin inserted crosswise (as through the end of an axle or shaft)
*2 : one that serves to hold together parts or elements that exist or function as a unit

Example sentence:
Jane&#39;s canceled check was the linchpin of the case against her, because it proved that she did know about the sale of Aunt Jessie&#39;s vase.

Did you know?
&quot;There was the good old custom of taking the linch-pins out of the farmers&#39; and bagmens&#39; gigs at the fairs, and a cowardly blackguard custom it was.&quot; That custom, described by British writer Thomas Hughes in his 1857 novel Tom Brown&#39;s School Days, was &quot;blackguard&quot; indeed. The linchpin in question held the wheel on the carriage and removing it made it likely that the wheel would come off as the vehicle moved. Such a pin was called a &quot;lynis&quot; in Old English; Middle English speakers added &quot;pin&quot; to form &quot;lynspin.&quot; Modern English speakers modified it to &quot;linchpin&quot; and, in the mid-20th century, began using the term figuratively for anything as critical to a complex situation as a linchpin is to a wagon.

The Word of the Day for November 21 is:

élan \\ay-LAHNG (the &quot;NG&quot; is not pronounced, but the vowel is nasalized)\\ noun

: vigorous spirit or enthusiasm

Example sentence:
With all the requisite élan, our guest blessed the food, toasted the cook, carved the roast turkey, served it around, and ate multiple helpings of everything.

Did you know?
Once upon a time, English speakers did not have &quot;élan&quot; (the word, that is; that&#39;s not to say we haven&#39;t always had potential for vigourous spirit). We had, however, &quot;elance,&quot; a verb meaning &quot;to hurl&quot; that was used specifically for throwing lances and darts. &quot;Elance&quot; derived down the line from Middle French &quot;(s&#39;)eslancer,&quot; meaning &quot;to rush&quot; or &quot;dash&quot; (that is, &quot;to hurl oneself forth&quot;). With the decline of lance-throwing, we tossed out &quot;elance&quot; a century and half ago. Just about that time we found &quot;élan,&quot; a noun that traces to &quot;(s&#39;)eslancer.&quot; We copied &quot;élan&quot; in form from the French, but we dispensed with the French sense of a literal &quot;rush&quot; or &quot;dash,&quot; retaining the sense of enthusiastic animation that we sometimes characterize as &quot;dash.&quot;
