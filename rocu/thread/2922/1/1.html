Q:
What impresses you about an applicant? What would set an applicant apart from the crowd and make you recommend him/her highly?

A:
I&#39;m a straight male, so a chick with nice legs and a big rack sets herself apart. We can&#39;t help it.

Seriously though.... An outgoing personality sets someone apart from the pack. Most 18 yo&#39;s are shy/nervous, but a kid that can present him/herself well leaves a good impression.

Having something interesting to say also is impressive. 95% of HS kids do the same crap (debate, NHS, etc.), and they basically have nothing to talk about other than barfing out their resumes.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
What&#39;s an example of a club/ec that really left an lasting impression? Thanks

A:
Non-school activities are the more impressive ones. Every HS has the same clubs, and at any given school some kiss-ass is president of five of them. Perhaps... 1) someone who did something interesting in the community, or 2) outside research in say science.


Q:
ok well how does this sound? I am really really into tennis so in the summer when the ATP comes to my city I volunteer a TON of time doing stuff like escorting the players from the airport into their rides, ushering, etc. Does that sound good as a volunteering opp?

A:
If you told me you did that, I would assume you did all that because you wanted to meet Andre and Pete. The ATP isn&#39;t exactly in need of your help (they&#39;re rich and can pay the big purses). Helping some indigent with AIDS and 6 kids is more of the &#39;give back to the community&#39; that colleges have in mind.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
Do you feel like your recommendations have any impact on the admissions officers? (I&#39;m assuming this isn&#39;t your first year)

A:
Minimal impact. First, good applicant=good interview is the general trend, so it doesn&#39;t matter for most. Second, one piece of the application (other than SAT/GPA) isn&#39;t worth too much. But, a bad ref definitely raises red flags (an awesome ref only goes so far).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
ok,,how bad can a recom be? how harmful is a bland/little interactive interview?

thanks

A:
I haven&#39;t had a &quot;bad&quot; one yet, but I would say &quot;you guys have got to be complete morons to take this kid&quot; in a nice way.

A bland interview gets a bland rec. Most applicants are bland, and will also have bland recs. You shouldn&#39;t have a problem. It will most likely neither help or hurt.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
i have heard that for some countries certain ivy selects applicants to be interviewed,also before the interview the school will have already put the applicants into three catalogs: wanna admit, fair, and don&#39;t wanna admit. true?

A:
When is your interview? Is it local? If you interview in Feb, there isn&#39;t time for them to screen you out. They&#39;re probably pushing paper and making files throughout Jan. Sounds fishy to me, though.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
Do you usually ask this question &quot;what other schools are you applying to?&quot;


A1:
Yes, I&#39;m nosey. It also gives you the idea of what type of person they are (i.e. a prestige-whore who applies to all 8 ivies). That doesn&#39;t mean you have to give me an honest/complete answer. I&#39;ll never know if you BS that question. I would assume most interviewers ask that; most asked me when I applied.

A2:
As long as you can name another school or two and give good reasons. You dont want to sound arrogant (&quot;Im ONLY applying to Yale!&quot;) or completely out of it (&quot;I applied to all 8 ivies, i dont know why, but why not? my parents wanted me to.&quot;)

A3(Harvard Interviewer):

Harvard interviewers are told not to ask this.


Q:
So just random list some reach/match/safety? Thanks. And another, do I need to bring a resume? E.g. I&#39;m pretty interested in/good at designing so could I bring some kinds of samples(color prints)?

A1:
Ya, just tell them some random schools. But, make sure they make sense. Say you&#39;re from a big city and you&#39;re interviewing with Cornell/Dartmouth, don&#39;t list 10 urban schools.

I tell kids to bring one. I&#39;d bring a resume, either way. They have something to look at and ask questions about, if the conversation dies. They can read it later if some parts aren&#39;t covered. It leaves an impression. Just make sure it is professional-looking, otherwise you look like a fool.

Bring the prints. If a kid brought something like that in, I&#39;d think that 1) they give a sh*t about this, and 2) they&#39;re different than the pack. I don&#39;t see how it could hurt, unless you get some crabby old guy who finds it inappropriate.


A2:
Bring the prints. I had a friend who wrote a lot of (extremely good) poetry and had a couple poems that she brought to interviews in folder with her resume, the logic being that she wouldn&#39;t have to show them to the interviewer if she mentioned that she wrote poetry and the interviewer thought that was lame or whatever. However, for her top-choice school, she was interviewed by an English teacher, who absolutely loved the poems.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
What type of awards are considered &quot;impressive&quot;? What do you recommend potential applicants to do over the summer?

A:
Impressive awards are ones that are rare/unique. Anything national is good, state/regional may be impressive. Whether it is sports, music, or science fair doesn&#39;t matter -- the fact is that you distinguish yourself. Any high school award is bullsh*t... NatMerit/NHS are common..etc.

Which summer? Recommend, in terms of what?

Q:
I have won national awards for MUN and regional ones for Model Congress, so I was wondering how those would be viewed.

It would be the summer between my junior and senior year; I am a junior now. I absolutely LOVE political science/international relations/diplomacy/economics. I was interested in taking summer courses at colleges. I&#39;ve researched Harvard&#39;s, Stanford&#39;s, Georgetown&#39;s, BC&#39;s, and MIT&#39;s program, among others. Plus, I was considering participating in a summer Junior Statesmen of America program. It is also possible that I could have an internship with Senator Lieberman. But I would really like to study something related to politics, U.S. foreign policy, or economics because none of the course offered at my school entail those subjects. But cost is a problem.

A:
Those awards aren&#39;t going to make you an autoadmit, but they definitely add something to the package.

Taking college courses is very helpful, in terms of preparing you for freshman year. High school is totally different than college. However, lots of kids do it so it won&#39;t &quot;help&quot; you.

Those summer programs are BS to me, because you basically pay a fee to go to camp. Lots of kids do those things.

The internship with the Senator might be good experience if you get to do stuff, but would they have you stuck making photocopies of donkeys all day?

Don&#39;t worry. You&#39;ll have plenty of time to take all the courses you want at college.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
How are Brown interviewers? (Quirky | psuedo-ivy attitude | etc)

A:
You can&#39;t make any generalization about Brown&#39;s (or any other school&#39;s) interviewers. You could get an 80 year old conservative snobby WASP, or you could get a 25 year old lesbian with purple hair.

Q:
If I want to mention something to my interviewer that wouldn&#39;t have been on my application how should I do that?

A:
What do you want to mention? The best way is to work it into the conversation. The problem is that you have to let the interviewer know to write about it in the evaluation, so being subtle is sometimes hard. I don&#39;t see anything particularly tacky about, &quot;ya know, my app doesn&#39;t indicate XYZ but...&quot; Furthermore, some interviewers will ask something like &quot;is there anything you&#39;d like to tell me that I can pass on to the adcoms?&quot;

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
Do you ask basic questions about themselves and their achievements or do you ask for their opinions on politics/world events? What if they disagree with you? Also, do you encourage them to ask questions about the college? And if so, what kind of questions should/shouldn&#39;t they ask?

A:
I ask some basic questions, throughout the session. I try to make it more of a conversation than a question-firing session.

I try not to bring up politics. There is no point. The interview is about them and the school, not Usama or Saddam. I&#39;m a right-winger, so everyone would disagree with me anyway.

I don&#39;t encourage them to ask anything. The ones who give a sh*t will generally come prepared with questions and knowledge about the school. Try not to ask anything that can be found on the website, but anything else (social life, professors, environment, etc.) is good.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
How are the SATs actually weighted? Is it true that it is more important to have a better verbal score than a better math score? How are SAT IIs actually weighted? I&#39;ve took AP Chem and AP Euro last year and gots 5s on both. I took the SAT IIs in the respective subjects. Should I sumbit my SAT II chem and world history or other SAT IIs that were just as good in other subjects to show &quot;well-roundedness&quot;?

A:
I&#39;m an alum interviewer, not an adcom. So, I can&#39;t testify as to what goes on in the adcom&#39;s mind. However, I&#39;ll give my best answer.

#1 - Nothing is weighted, but you&#39;re looked as a &quot;package&quot;.

#2 - BS. They want high scores in both. A high verbal score is more rare, because of the percentiles.

#3 - See #1

#4 - If you did well on the SAT2&#39;s (you didn&#39;t say), submit them. They won&#39;t care if you&#39;re a dork and got 10 800&#39;s. Your grades can show well-roundedness. It isn&#39;t as if Chem and Euro are similar -- that is well rounded.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
May I ask which school you interview for? Additionally, are you in the bay area? If you are, we should have a right wing interview session(I&#39;ll pay for your sh*t at the coffee shop).

Do kids often come off as arrogant? Do you ever ask &quot;How would your friends describe you?&quot; (That is the worst of all)

A:
I really don&#39;t want to specify. I might lose my &quot;job&quot;, since they pay us the big bucks!

Nope, I interview on the east coast. In the bay area, you&#39;re out of luck for a right-wing interview session. Or, maybe some rich SV computer alum will be the exception to the rule.

Some kids are arrogant; you get a broad range of kids. I don&#39;t care if kids come off as arrogant, as long as they&#39;re not pricks (you see the difference, right?). I&#39;m arrogant, so I don&#39;t mind arrogant people.

I would never ask a lame-ass question like that. If I would want to say &quot;fu*k you&quot; to an interviewer who asked me something, I won&#39;t ask it to a kid. It is only a college interview, not an interview for a management position at a Fortune 500 company. I had one college interview that was full of stupid questions like that, and it sucks.

Q:
I got some guy at Yale who gave me that crap. I had the lamest answers.

&quot;uhmm, i dont know. hopefully they&#39;d say I&#39;m smart, I challenge myself, and I have a sense of humor.&quot;

then he asked me what i would change about my highschool and i gave some pc crap about how it would be great if whites and blacks and mexicans would hold hands in a circle.

A:
Your interviewer sounds like a prick. He probably gets lame answers from everyone. As long as you didn&#39;t say something moronic, you&#39;re fine.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
what should an applicant wear to an interview?

A1:
honestly, a nice business casual outfit would be good (slacks or a skirt with a blouse or nice sweater). Make sure everything is pressed and clean. If the interviewer is a lawyer or something and youre meeting him/her at the office, you might want to dress up a little more.

You might want to bring an extra copy of your resume in case, and if you are a really exceptional artist or somethng, you could bring a couple pictures of your work or something like that.


A2:
Dress for the place. Don&#39;t wear a suit/formal. It really won&#39;t be appropriate in any situation. Biz-casual is usually safe. If you feel comfortable, you might want to ask the person. Though, some might see it as tacky.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
What is the format for the write-up of an interview? Are there important traits to display?

A:
A short numeric evaluation, and then an essay/letter/paragraph/whatever you want to write. From what I&#39;ve hear, this format is common. If the write-up was a pain in the ass, alums wouldn&#39;t bother.

Important traits -- the one thing that any good kid will display is that s/he wants to attend the school. Any positive trait is good. Everyone is different, so it isn&#39;t like every kid is the same. What did you have in mind?

Q:
What are the numerical evaluation categories?

A:
Very general stuff. Overall impression... Attitude... That kind of stuff.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
I just had an interview for Harvard and I was very nervous about it. While I have great ec&#39;s, recs, grades etc I was afraid that the interviewer would take one look at my board scores and politely ask me to leave. In actuality I could tell that he was none too impressed with my middle to low SATIIs and my below-average SATS (for Harvard at least). Right after he saw them he did the normal time-to-leave speech, &quot;ok well is there anything you would like to ask me about the school.&quot; So I asked questions and we got to talking about campus life, economic situations in the world, and even related some personal stories. After I couldn&#39;t think of anything else he went back to an interview for about another 30 minutes...overall it lasted for an hour. Do you think he was just being polite to me with keeping up the interview after seeing that my scores were too low or was him going back to an interview a good sign?

A:
Hard to tell. Maybe he uses a standard format. Maybe he was being polite. Maybe he thought the conversation got interesting so he didn&#39;t mind. Maybe he just likes to ramble about himself.

Scores/grades really aren&#39;t that much of the interviewer&#39;s business. Most of us ask because we&#39;re nosey. The adcoms already have all of your scores, and it isn&#39;t an alum&#39;s job to judge your numbers. If you left him with a good impression of you, he might not be too influenced by the them.

Q:
Does Harvard ask you to bring a sheet with your SAT scores and grades? Did he ask you what your scores were?

A1:
It sounds like they were on the resume. Note, that you don&#39;t have to list them even if you bring a resume. No Ivy that I know of makes kids bring their scores to the interview. The admissions office has all the stuff. This particular interviewer could have asked for them, though.

A2(Harvard Interviewer):
I always have a copy of the transcript, teacher rec, EC list etc

Q:
Do you make the kid bring that or does the school give it to you?

A1:
I get it from the school.

A2:
No, my job as an interviewer is to evaluate the kid as a person. The admissions office doesn&#39;t need 1,000 different interviewers to judge numbers; that is their job. If I write a paragraph about a kid&#39;s numbers, I do the kid no good because the adcom doesn&#39;t gain any additional information.

A3(Harvard Interviewer):
&gt;No, my job as an interviewer is to evaluate the kid as a person. The admissions office doesn&#39;t need 1,000 different interviewers to judge numbers; that is their job.

No, that&#39;s your job as a NON-HARVARD interviewer. A Harvard interviewer is specifically instructed to look at the kid across the board. We have to give the adcom our impression of him in all three areas: academics, activities, and personal qualities. In order to help us evaluate academics, we ask for scores and rank.

Needless to say, admissions officers primarily evaluate candidates&#39; academic credentials themselves. But they apparently find it valuable to get a second opinion. The alumni are local residents, they know the schools well, and their input on the topic is valued.

Every school uses their alumni differently. I fail to see how it is &quot;retarded&quot; to follow the instructions in the interviewers&#39; handbook for your school.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
TOP LACS &gt; LOWER IVIES
what do you have to say interviewer?

A:
In terms of what? Top LACs may be better than &quot;higher&quot; Ivies in some respects (say, teaching quality). My personal opinion is that Lower Ivies &gt; Top LACs.

Q:
In terms of what?

A:
Ah, you&#39;re good  Overall reputation.


--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q
Also, you probably shouldnt dress skanky cuz a lot of interviewers make you meet them at their house, which in itself isnt a bad thing, but what if you had to have coffee with his wife and sh*t like that?

A:
Interviews at the house are tacky. When I interviewed, I felt unconfortable sitting on someone else&#39;s sofa and having family members coming in and out.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
Does it look snotty if you say that you read the Economist since you were 14?

A:
No, but it will make you look like brainwashed liberal slime. I&#39;d like you better if you said you read the National Review or New Republic.

Seriously though, it depends how you bring it up. If you relate it to your interests (i.e. &#39;the article last week on Iraq relates to my passion for international relations&#39;), you&#39;ll sound smart. If you just say &#39;one of my ECs is reading the Economist when I am on the sh*tter&#39;, they you&#39;ll sound like you&#39;re bragging.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q:
well i have had several interviews...while some interviewers said &quot;you are very impressive&quot;, some didn&#39;t express any personal opinions. my question is that if they said you were great/impressive/whatever, does that mean they really think so or this is just a kind of manner? if they don&#39;t say so, does that mean i had a mediocre/dull/crappy interview?

A:
I don&#39;t express a personal opinion like that, because it is very superficial. I&#39;m not a touchy-feeley person who can tell a moron that he was impressive, just to be polite. And, I don&#39;t want to tell a kid that he was very impressive to give him a false sense of security, since most people get rejected.

Your person may very well have been honest with you. Could you read him/her? Realize that some will tell everyone that to be polite, while others will never say anything like that even if you are amazing.
