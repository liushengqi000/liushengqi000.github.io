masterful \\MASS-ter-ful\\ adjective

1 a : inclined and usually competent to act as master *b : suggestive of a domineering nature
2 : having or reflecting the power and skill of a master

Example sentence:
&quot;He was a fellow of huge physical strength, masterful, violent, with a certain barbaric thrift and some intelligence of men and business.&quot; (Robert Louis Stevenson, In the South Seas)

Did you know?
Some commentators insist that &quot;masterful&quot; must only mean &quot;domineering,&quot; reserving the &quot;expert, skillful&quot; sense for &quot;masterly.&quot; The distinction is a modern one. In earlier times, the terms were used interchangeably, with each having both the &quot;domineering&quot; and &quot;expert&quot; senses. The &quot;domineering&quot; sense of &quot;masterly&quot; fell into disuse around the 18th century, however, and in the 20th century the famous grammarian H. W. Fowler decided that &quot;masterful&quot; should be similarly limited to a single meaning. He summarily ruled that the second definition of &quot;masterful&quot; was incorrect. Other usage writers followed his lead. The &quot;expert&quot; meaning of &quot;masterful&quot; has continued to flourish in standard prose, however, in spite of the disapproval. And, considering the sense&#39;s long history, it cannot really be called an error.

pidgin \\PIJ-in\\ noun

: a simplified speech used for communication between people with different languages

Example sentence:
Creole, which is now spoken in parts of southern Louisiana, originated as a pidgin spoken between French-speaking colonists and African slaves.

Did you know?
The history of &quot;pidgin&quot; begins in the early 19th century in the South China city of Guangzhou. Chinese merchants interacting with English speakers on the docks in this port sometimes pronounced the word &quot;business&quot; as &quot;bigeon.&quot; By the century&#39;s end, &quot;bigeon&quot; had developed into &quot;pigeon&quot; and finally &quot;pidgin,&quot; which then became the descriptor of the unique communication used by people who speak different languages. Pidgins generally consist of small vocabularies (Chinese Pidgin English has only 700 words), but some have grown to become a group&#39;s native language. Examples include Sea Island Creole (spoken in South Carolina&#39;s Sea Islands), Haitian Creole, and Louisiana Creole. The alteration of &quot;bigeon&quot; to &quot;pigeon&quot; also gave us &quot;pigeon,&quot; meaning &quot;an object of special concern&quot; or &quot;accepted business or interest.&quot;

bunkum \\BUNG-kum\\ noun

: insincere or foolish talk : nonsense

Example sentence:
Vincent thought he had given strong arguments in support of his theory, but Professor Lyons dismissed it as bunkum nonetheless.

Did you know?
Some words in our language have more colorful histories than others, but in the case of &quot;bunkum,&quot; you could almost say it was an act of Congress that brought the word into being. Back in 1820 Felix Walker, who represented Buncombe County, North Carolina, in the U.S. House of Representatives, was determined that his voice be heard on his constituents&#39; behalf, even though the matter up for debate was irrelevant to Walker&#39;s district and he had little to contribute. To the exasperation of his colleagues, Walker insisted on delivering a long and wearisome &quot;speech for Buncombe.&quot; His persistent — if insignificant — harangue made &quot;buncombe&quot; (later respelled &quot;bunkum&quot;) a synonym for meaningless political claptrap and later for any kind of nonsense.

germane \\jer-MAYN\\ adjective

: being at once relevant and appropriate : fitting

Example sentence:

Did you know?
&quot;Wert thou a Leopard, thou wert Germane to the Lion.&quot; So wrote Shakespeare in Timon of Athens (circa 1607), using an old (and now obsolete) sense of &quot;germane&quot; meaning &quot;closely akin.&quot; &quot;Germane&quot; derives from the Latin word &quot;germen,&quot; meaning &quot;bud&quot; or &quot;sprout,&quot; which is also at the root of our verb &quot;germinate,&quot; meaning &quot;to sprout&quot; or &quot;begin to develop.&quot; An early sense of &quot;germane&quot; referred specifically to children of the same parents, who were perhaps seen as being like buds on a single tree.

apposite \\AP-uh-zit\\ adjective

: highly pertinent or appropriate : apt

Example sentence:
&quot;It is a lucid work, written in simple English and amply illustrated by apposite examples....&quot; (Judith N. Shklar, Social Research, September 22, 2004)

Did you know?
&quot;Apposite&quot; and &quot;opposite&quot; sound so much alike that you would expect them to have a common ancestor — and they do. It is the Latin verb &quot;ponere,&quot; which means &quot;to put or place.&quot; Adding the prefix &quot;ad-&quot; to &quot;ponere&quot; created &quot;apponere,&quot; meaning &quot;to place near&quot; or &quot;apply to,&quot; and that branch of the &quot;ponere&quot; family tree led to &quot;apposite.&quot; The word is used to describe something that applies well to or is very appropriate for something else, a notion perhaps suggested by the close proximity of two objects. To get &quot;opposite,&quot; the prefix &quot;ob-&quot; was added to &quot;ponere&quot; to create &quot;opponere,&quot; meaning &quot;to place against or opposite.&quot; The related verb &quot;componere,&quot; meaning &quot;to put together,&quot; gave us &quot;compound&quot; and &quot;composite.&quot;

thanatology \\than-uh-TAH-luh-jee\\ noun

: the description or study of the phenomena of death and of psychological mechanisms for coping with them

Example sentence:
Those who work in the field of thanatology address not only the needs of the dying but the effects of death on the families as well.

Did you know?
In Greek mythology, Thanatos was the personification of death, the twin brother of Hypnos (Sleep). The ancient Greeks eventually came to use &quot;thanatos&quot; as a generic word for &quot;death.&quot; &quot;Thanatology&quot; is a direct linguistic heir of the Greek term and it was first documented in English in the mid-1800s. In 1935, the name &quot;Thanatos&quot; made a comeback in English, ushered in with psychoanalytic theory and describing an unconscious tendency toward self-destruction.

imbricate \\IM-bruh-kayt\\ verb

: overlap; especially : to overlap like roof tiles

Example sentence:
&quot;Fine-spun and see-through, the cotton... acts as canvas and writing paper, pierced and covered by the imbricated stitches that decorate it.&quot; (Frances Richard, Artforum International, January 1, 2005)

Did you know?
The ancient Romans knew how to keep the interior of their villas dry when it rained. They tiled their roofs with overlapping curved tiles so the &quot;imber&quot; (Latin for &quot;rain&quot;) couldn&#39;t seep in. The tiles were, in effect, &quot;rain tiles,&quot; so the Romans called them &quot;imbrices&quot; (singular &quot;imbrex&quot;). The verb for installing the tiles was &quot;imbricare.&quot; The &quot;imbr-&quot; root has never really been put to use in English rain-related words (though scientists have made use of the closely-related Greek &quot;ombros&quot;; for example, &quot;ombriphilous&quot; describes a plant that loves heavy rainfall). English speakers used the past participle of &quot;imbricare&quot; — &quot;imbricatus&quot; — to create &quot;imbricate,&quot; which was first used as adjective meaning &quot;overlapping (like roof tiles)&quot; and later became a verb meaning &quot;to overlap.&quot;

monger \\MUNG-gur\\ noun

1 : broker, dealer — usually used in combination
*2 : a person who attempts to stir up or spread something that is usually petty or discreditable — usually used in combination

Example sentence:
According to reports, the singer and actress were involved in a hot romance, but the rumormongers had it wrong — the two were just good friends.

Did you know?
Peddlers (especially fish merchants) have been called &quot;mongers&quot; for more than 1000 years. The term traces to a Latin noun meaning &quot;trader.&quot; Initially, it was an honorable term, but every profession has its bad apples, and the snake-oil salesmen of the bunch gave &quot;monger&quot; a bad reputation. By the middle of the 16th century, the term implied that a merchant was dishonorable and contemptible. Nowadays, &quot;monger&quot; can be combined with just about anything to identify one who specializes in fast trades and loose deals, whether they be rumormongers, scandalmongers, hypemongers, or even miraclemongers.

Pyrrhic \\PEER-ik\\ adjective

: achieved at excessive cost; also : costly to the point of negating or outweighing expected benefits

Example sentence:
Gretchen&#39;s unexpected win over the tournament&#39;s top player proved a Pyrrhic victory; in the effort, she reinjured her shoulder.

Did you know?
In 306 B.C., at the age of twelve, a youth named Pyrrhus took the throne of Epirus, a country in northwestern Greece. Pyrrhus grew to be an aggressive and quarrelsome king, given to warring with his neighbors. In 280 B.C., he brought 25,000 men (and a number of elephants) to southern Italy and defeated the Romans, but only after losing many of his soldiers. A year later, he again suffered heavy casualties at Roman hands in a battle at Ausculum. According to Plutarch, when he was congratulated on those victories Pyrrhus replied, &quot;Another such victory over the Romans and we are undone.&quot; The bloody battles of Pyrrhus didn&#39;t find their way into English in the phrase &quotyrrhic victory&quot; until the 1800s, but once it was established it quickly found occupation as an adjective even independent of the phrase, in such constructions as &quot;the vindication was Pyrrhic&quot; and &quot;a Pyrrhic gesture.&quot;

cocooning \\kuh-KOON-ing\\ noun

: the practice of spending leisure time at home in preference to going out

Example sentence:
&quot;The current trend toward cocooning has been very good for our business,&quot; noted the owner of Marvin&#39;s Hot Tubs at a recent home show.

Did you know?
It&#39;s safe to begin our history of &quot;cocooning&quot; with &quot;cocoon,&quot; the case protecting an insect pupa. We borrowed that noun from French over 300 years ago; the French &quot;cocon&quot; in turn comes from an Occitan word meaning &quot;shell.&quot; The verb &quot;cocoon,&quot; meaning &quot;to wrap or envelop as if in a cocoon,&quot; entered English in 1881 when Mark Twain wrote, &quot;We ... cocooned ourselves in the proper red blankets.&quot; Metaphorical extensions of the verb, to suggest either the condition of being entrapped (&quot;cocooned in restrictions&quot;) or of being in a safe and protected place (&quot;cocooned against outside forces&quot;), began to appear in the 20th century. The latter connotation led to &quot;cocooning,&quot; a 1980s coinage generally attributed to marketing consultant and trend-spotter Faith Popcorn.
