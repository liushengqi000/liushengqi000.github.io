The Word of the Day for December 22 is:

solatium \\so-LAY-shee-um\\ noun

: a compensation (as money) given as solace for suffering, loss, or injured feelings

Example sentence:
The judge awarded a substantial solatium to all of the bus passengers who were traumatized as a result of the accident.

Did you know?
In legal circles, a solatium is a payment made to a victim as compensation for injured feelings or emotional pain and suffering (such as the trauma following the wrongful death of a relative), as distinct from payment for physical injury or for damaged property. Like many legal terms, &quot;solatium,&quot; which first appeared in English in the early 19th century, is a product of Latin, where the word means &quot;solace.&quot; The Latin noun is related to the verb &quot;solari,&quot; which means &quot;to console&quot; and from which we get our words &quot;solace&quot; and &quot;console.&quot;

The Word of the Day for December 23 is:

dewy \\DOO-ee\\ adjective

1 : moist with, affected by, or suggestive of dew
*2 : innocent, unsophisticated

Example sentence:
&quot;She was cute. She had that dewy look. Ryan was unable to remain angry with pretty women....&quot; (Tom Clancy, Patriot Games)

Did you know?
&quot;And her faire deawy eies with kisses deare Shee ofte did bathe&quot; (Edmund Spenser, The Faerie Queene). &quot;I would these dewy teares were from the ground&quot; (William Shakespeare, Richard III). &quot;Till dewie sleep Oppress&#39;d them&quot; (John Milton, Paradise Lost). &quot;Strengthen me, enlighten me ... Thou dewy dawn of memory&quot; (Alfred Tennyson, &quot;Ode to Memory&quot;). Such lines exemplify how the greats have poetically extended the characteristics of dewy grass to misty or crying eyes, as well as to things, like sleep, that affect people gently like forming dew, or to things, like memory, that gradually vanish like a morning&#39;s dew. Not until the 20th century, however, did people begin to connect newly formed, undisturbed dew with freshness or purity and, in turn, with innocence and na&iuml;veté.

The Word of the Day for December 24 is:

luminaria &#8226; \\loo-muh-NAIR-ee-uh\\  &#8226; noun
: a traditional Mexican Christmas lantern originally consisting of a candle set in sand inside a paper bag

Example sentence:
More than one neighborhood in our Texas town is lit by luminarias lining the streets for the annual Christmas Stroll.

Did you know?
&quot;Luminaria&quot; is a fairly recent addition to English; the earliest known use in our language dates from 1949, about the time that the old Mexican Christmas custom was gaining popularity among Anglo-Americans. In some parts of the U.S., particularly New Mexico, these festive lanterns are also called &quot;farolitos,&quot; which means &quot;little lanterns&quot; in Spanish. We borrowed &quot;luminaria&quot; from Spanish, but the word has been around with exactly the same spelling since the days of Late Latin. The term ultimately traces to the classical Latin &quot;luminare,&quot; meaning &quot;window,&quot; and to &quot;lumen,&quot; meaning &quot;light.&quot; It is related to other light-bearing words such as &quot;luminary,&quot; &quot;illuminate,&quot; and &quot;phillumenist&quot; (a fancy name for someone who collects matchbooks).

The Word of the Day for December 25 is:

welkin &#8226; \\WEL-kun\\  &#8226; noun
1 *a : the vault of the sky : firmament b : the celestial abode of God or the gods : heaven
2 : the upper atmosphere

Example sentence:
The pink sky at sunset brought to mind a quote from Shakespeare&#39;s King John: &quot;The sun of heaven, methought, was loath to set / But stay&#39;d and made the western welkin blush.&quot;

Did you know?
When it comes to &quot;welkin,&quot; the sky&#39;s the limit. This heavenly word has been used in English to refer to the vault of the sky since at least the 12th century, and it derives from an earlier word from Old English that meant &quot;cloud.&quot; In modern English, &quot;welkin&quot; is still flying high, and it is often teamed with the verb &quot;ring&quot; to suggest a loud noise or an exuberant expression of emotion, as in &quot;the welkin rang with the sound of the orchestra&quot; or &quot;her hearty laugh made the welkin ring.&quot; These contemporary phrases echo an older use—the original words of a carol that once began &quot;Hark, how all the welkin ring,&quot; which we now know as &quot;Hark! The herald angels sing.&quot;

The Word of the Day for December 26 is:

Kwanzaa &#8226; \\KWAHN-zuh\\  &#8226; noun
: an African-American cultural festival held from December 26 to January 1

Example sentence:
A joyous family spirit pervaded the Allen family&#39;s Kwanzaa celebration as three generations came together for a delightful meal and a beautiful candle-lighting ceremony.

Did you know?
In 1966, Maulana Karenga, a Black-studies professor at California State University at Long Beach, created a new holiday patterned after traditional African harvest festivals. He called it &quot;Kwanzaa,&quot; a name he took from a Swahili term that means &quot;first fruits.&quot; The holiday, which takes place from December 26th to January 1st, was originally intended as a nonreligious celebration of family and social values. Each day of Kwanzaa is dedicated to one of seven principles: unity, self-determination, collective responsibility, cooperative economics, purpose, creativity, and faith.

The Word of the Day for December 27 is:

snivel &#8226; \\SNIV-ul\\  &#8226; verb
1 a : to run at the nose b : snuffle
2 : to cry or whine with snuffling
*3 : to speak or act in a whining, sniffling, tearful, or weakly emotional manner

Example sentence:
Mom told Jenny to stop sniveling about how mistreated she was and just do her chores.

Did you know?
There&#39;s never been anything pretty about sniveling. &quot;Snivel,&quot; which originally meant simply &quot;to have a runny nose,&quot; was probably &quot;snyflan&quot; in Old English. It&#39;s likely related to &quot;sniffle,&quot; not surprisingly, and also to an Old English word for mucus, &quot;snofl.&quot; It&#39;s even related to the Middle Dutch word for a cold, &quot;snof,&quot; and the Old Norse word for &quot;snout,&quot; which is &quot;snoppa.&quot; There&#39;s also a connection to &quot;nan,&quot; a Greek verb meaning &quot;flow.&quot; Nowadays, we mostly use &quot;snivel,&quot; as we have since the 1600s, to refer to self-pitying whining, whether or not such sniveling is accompanied by unchecked nasal flow.

The Word of the Day for December 28 is:

evergreen &#8226; \\EV-er-green\\  &#8226; adj
1 : having foliage that remains green and functional through more than one growing season
*2 : retaining freshness or interest : perennial

Example sentence:
Every Christmas season, we go to see Tchaikovsky&#39;s evergreen Christmas ballet, The Nutcracker.

Did you know?
Which adjective do you think has existed longer in English, &quot;evergreen&quot; or &quot;perennial&quot;? If you count the hyphenated form &quot;ever-green&quot; (which of course meant &quot;always green&quot;), then &quot;evergreen&quot; is older; its earliest known use dates from 1555. If you are a purist and insist on the hyphen-free form, 1671 is the earliest known adjectival use of &quot;evergreen&quot; (although the noun &quot;evergreen,&quot; meaning &quot;conifer,&quot; has been in use since at least 1644). The first English text known to use &quot;perennial&quot; as an adjective meaning &quot;remaining green all year long&quot; was published in 1644. But &quot;perennial&quot; wins in the more general &quot;long lasting&quot; sense; it has been used with that enduring meaning since the early 1700s. &quot;Evergreen&quot; did not appear in English texts in that sense until the 1800s.

The Word of the Day for December 29 is:

bonhomie &#8226; \\bah-nuh-MEE\\  &#8226; noun
: good-natured easy friendliness

Example sentence:
We look forward every year to champagne and eggs benedict, served up with plenty of bonhomie, at our friend&#39;s annual New Year&#39;s Day brunch.

Did you know?
English speakers adapted &quot;bonhomie&quot; from the French &quot;bonhomme,&quot; which means &quot;good-natured man&quot; and which is itself a composite of two other French words: &quot;bon,&quot; meaning &quot;good,&quot; and &quot;homme,&quot; meaning &quot;man.&quot; That French compound traces to two Latin terms, &quot;bonus&quot; (meaning &quot;good&quot;) and &quot;homo&quot; (meaning either &quot;man&quot; or &quot;human being&quot;). English speakers have warmly embraced the French term and its meaning, but we have also anglicized the pronunciation in a way that may make native French speakers cringe. (We hope they will be good-natured about it!) At this festive time of year, &quot;bonhomie&quot; is also sometimes used to mean &quot;an atmosphere of good cheer,&quot; so you might say a merry family party has a &quot;holiday bonhomie.&quot;

The Word of the Day for December 30 is:

dyspepsia &#8226; \\dis-PEP-shuh\\  &#8226; noun
1 : indigestion
*2 : ill humor : disgruntlement

Example sentence:
&quot;As his prestige grew,&quot; wrote historian Daniel Boorstin about Sir Isaac Newton, &quot;so did his dyspepsia, his unwillingness to give credit to others or share credit for his great discoveries.&quot;

Did you know?
When people get indigestion, they are often affected by nausea, heartburn, and gas—things that can cause the world&#39;s greatest gastronome to curse the world&#39;s most delectable dishes. So, it is no wonder that &quot;dyspepsia,&quot; a word for indigestion, has also come to mean &quot;ill humor&quot; or &quot;disgruntlement.&quot; The word itself is ultimately derived from the Greek prefix &quot;dys-&quot; (&quot;faulty&quot; or &quot;impaired&quot;) and verb &quot;pessein&quot; (&quot;to cook&quot; or &quot;to digest&quot;). To please the wordmonger&#39;s appetite, we would like to end with this tasty morsel: &quot;Dyspepsia&quot; has an opposite, &quot;eupepsia&quot;—a rarely used word meaning &quot;good digestion&quot; that can be found in Webster&#39;s Third New International Dictionary, Unabridged.

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

The Word of the Day for December 31 is:

connive &#8226; \\kuh-NYVE\\  &#8226; verb
*1 : to pretend ignorance of or fail to take action against something one ought to oppose
2 a : to be indulgent or in secret sympathy : wink b : to cooperate secretly or have a secret understanding
3 : conspire, intrigue

Example sentence:
Did the professor connive at the cheating that was going on during the exam, or was he unaware that it was happening?

Did you know?
&quot;Connive&quot; may not seem like a troublesome term, but it was to Wilson Follett, a usage critic who lamented that the word &quot;was undone during the Second World War, when restless spirits felt the need of a new synonym for plotting, bribing, spying, conspiring, engineering a coup, preparing a secret attack.&quot; Follett thought &quot;connive&quot; should only mean &quot;to wink at&quot; or &quot;to pretend ignorance.&quot; Those senses are closer to the Latin ancestor of the word (&quot;connive&quot; comes from the Latin &quot;connivēre,&quot; which means &quot;to close the eyes&quot; and which is descended from &quot;-nivēre,&quot; a form akin to the Latin verb &quot;nictare,&quot; meaning &quot;to wink&quot;). But many English speakers disagreed, and the &quot;conspire&quot; sense is now the word&#39;s most widely used meaning.

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.
