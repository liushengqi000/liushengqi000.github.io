---World War Two

The Allies fought the Germans primarily in Russia and in the Mediterranean until early 1944, when Allied forces invaded occupied France (on D-Day).

The War Production Board allowed government to oversee the mobilization of industry toward the war effort.
Rationing of almost all consumer goods was imposed.
Radar and the atomic bomb were developed.

The Labor Disputes Act of 1943 allowed government takeover of businesses deemed necessary to national security and gave the government authority to settle labor disputes.
Hollywood was enlisted to create numerous propaganda films.

The internment of Japanese Americans from 1942

---The End of the War
In early 1945 the Allied leaders met at Yalta to redraw the world map. The Allies agreed on a number of issues concerning borders and postwar alliances, agreed that once the war in Europe ended, the U.S.S.R would soon after declare war on Japan, and help create the United Nations.
The allied met again later in 1945 at Potsdam to decide how to implement the agreement of Yalta. Harry S Truman represented the U.S. Things did not go as well at Potsdam.

In August, 1945, the U.S. dropped two atomic bombs, first on Hiroshima, and three days later, on Nagasaki.

TRUMAN AND THE BEGINNING OF THE COLD WAR (1945-1952)

---Truman and Foreign Policy
The Soviet refused to recognize Poland’s conservative government-in-exile.
Within two years, pro-Soviet Communist coups had also taken place in Hungary and Czechoslovakia.

In 1947, Communist insurgents threatened to take over both Greece and Turkey.
The Truman Doctrine: containment.
The Marshall Plan sent more than $12 billion to Europe to help rebuild its cities and economy.
The United States formed the North Atlantic Treaty Organization with Western Europe in 1949.

The Soviets imposed a blockade on Berlin. Truman refused to surrender the city, and ordered airlifts.

After the Soviets detonated their first atomic bomb, fear of Soviet invasion or subterfuge also led to the creation of the National Security Council and the Central Intelligence Agency.

Two issues dominated U.S. policy in the region: the reconstruction of Japan and the Chinese Revolution.

---The Red Scare
In 1947, Truman ordered investigations of 3 million federal employees in a search for “security risks.”
In 1949 former State Department official Alger Hiss was found guilty of consorting with a Communist spy.

Joseph McCarthy rose to national fame.
In 1954 he accused the Army of harboring Communists. The Army fought back hard, and in the televised Army-McCarthy hearings, McCarthy was made to look foolish. The era of McCarthyism ended.

---Truman’s Domestic Policy and the Election of 1948
Unemployment levels rose dramatically after the war.
Prices rose, inflation rose.
Most of Truman’s proposals were rejected, and the few that were implemented had little effect.
The new conservatism brought with it a new round of anti-unionism in the country.

The coal miners’ strike (by the United Mine Workers, or UMW) cut off the energy supply to other industries. Truman followed the national mood, ordering a government seizure of the mines when a settlement could not be reached.
Later during a railroad strike, Truman threatened to draft into the military those strikers.
The Republicans took control of the Eightieth Congress in the 1947 midterm elections.
Truman convened the President’s Committee on Civil Rights.
The NAACP won some important lawsuits against segregated schools and buses; Jackie Robinson broke the color barrier in baseball.

The Taft-Hartley Act, passed over Truman’s veto, prohibited “union only” work environments—closed shops, restricted labor’s right to strike.
The same congress then rebuked Truman’s efforts to pass health care reform, promote civil rights, and increase aid to the disabled, the elderly.

---The Korean War
It began when Communist North Korea invaded U.S.-backed South Korea. China ultimately entered the war, pushing American and South Korean troops back near the original border dividing North and South Korea.
U.S. commander Douglas MacArthur recommended an all-out confrontation with China. Truman decided against MacArthur and fired him.

In the 1952 election, Republic Dwight D. Eisenhower beat Democratic challenger Adlai Stevenson easily.

THE EISENHOWER YEARS (1953-1960)
---The Fifties are often depicted as a time of conformity. A consensus of values reigned.
The fifties: an era in which the civil rights movement made its first strides and met some violent resistance; and era plagued by frequent economic recessions; and an era of spiritual unrest that manifested itself in such emerging art forms as beat poetry and novels, teen movies, and rock and roll.

---Domestic Politics in the Fifties
Eisenhower cut federal spending, and eased government regulation of business.
The government developed the Interstate Highway System.

His new polity: termination, would end federal support to Native Americans and subject them to state law.

In 1954, the Supreme Court heard the case of Brown v. Board of Education of Topeka. The Court overturned the “separate but equal” standard as it applied to education.
In 1957 the governor of Arkansas called in the state National Guard to prevent blacks from enrolling in a Little Rock high school.
The Montgomery bus boycott took place in the fifties. The arrest of Rosa Parks sparked the boycott. The boycott brought Martin Luther King, Jr. to national prominence.
In 1960 black college students in Greensboro tried just that approach, organizing a sit-in at a local Woolworth’s lunch counter designated “whites only.”

---America v. the Communists
The administration followed the policy of containment, but called it liberation.
Secretary of State John Foster Dulles coined the phrase “massive retaliation” to describe the nuclear attack that the U.S. would launch if the Soviets tried anything too daring.
Deterrence described how Soviet fear of massive retaliation would prevent their challenging the U.S. Dulles allowed confrontations with the Soviet Union to escalate toward war, and approach called brinksmanship.
The domino theory:  once Communists took over one country, the others surrounding it would fall quickly like dominoes.
Joseph Stalin died in 1953. The new Russian leader Nikita Khrushchev called for “peaceful coexistence” among nations.
Soviet advances in nuclear arms development and space flight (the U.S.S.R. launched the first satellite, motivating the U.S. to quickly create and fund the National Aeronautics and Space Administration, or NASA) further heightened anxieties.

American-allied Taiwan occupied two islands close to mainland China, Quemoy and Matsu. Eventually the Chinese bombed the two islands.

---Third World Politics
Nationalism swept through most Third World nations.
In Egypt the U.S. tried offering foreign aid, hoping to gain an ally by building the much-needed Aswan Dam. But Gamal Nasser turned to the Soviet Union for that aid.
The American government also used CIA covert operations to provide a more forceful method of increasing its influence abroad.
The CIA helped overthrow the governments of Iran and Guatemala.
CIA tried, unsuccessfully, to assassinate Cuban Communist leader Fidel Castro.

---The 1960 Presidential Election
Richard Nixon received the Republican nomination. The Democrats nominated John F. Kennedy.
Military-industrial complex

THE SIXTIES AND BEYOND (1961-THE NEAR PRESENT)
The New Frontier—Kennedy’s domestic program.
Much of the conflict centered around two issues: the Vietnam War, and blacks’ struggle to gain civil rights.

---Kennedy and Foreign Policy
The U.S.-friendly dictatorship had been overthrown by Communist insurgents led by Fidel Castro. CIA presented the Bay of Pigs invasion. The invasion
In 1962 the United States and the Soviet Union came the closest they had yet to a military confrontation: the Cuban missile crisis. Kennedy imposed a naval quarantine on Cuba to prevent any further weapons shipments from reaching the island.

Anti-Communism even motivated such ostensibly philanthropic programs abroad as the Peace Corps. The government called this process “nation building.”

---Kennedy and Domestic Policy
His greatest successes were in the space program.

He supported women’s rights, establishing a presidential commission that in 1963 recommended removing all obstacles to women’s participation in all facets of society.
Lyndon Johnson pushed the legislation – the Civil Rights Act of 1964 – through Congress on the strength of the late president’s popularity.
Martin Luther King, Jr. led the Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC)
The Congress of Racial Equality (CORE) organized the Freedom Riders movement.
The Student Non-Violent Coordinating Committee (SNCC)
In 1963 Mississippi’s NAACP director Medger Evers was shot to death by an anti-integrationist.

--Lyndon Johnson’s Social Agenda
Johnson oversaw the establishment of the Equal Employment Opportunity Commission (EEOC) to enforce the employment clause of the Civil Rights Act.
During his second term, Johnson signed the Voting Rights Act.
He won the Economic Opportunity Act, which appropriated nearly $1 billion for poverty relief.

Project Head Start prepared underprivileged children for early schooling; Upward Bound did the same for high school students. Job Corps trained the unskilled. Volunteers in Service to America (VISTA) acted as a domestic Peace Corps. Legal Services for the Poor guaranteed legal counsel to those who could not afford their own lawyer.
Johnson founded the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD).
Johnson’s social agenda: the Great Society.

---The Civil Rights Movement
Under Chief Justice Earl Warren, the Court was for a brief moment in history extremely liberal.
In Gideon v. Wainwright, the Court ruled that a defendant in a felony trial must be provided a lawyer for free if he or she could not afford one.
In the Miranda case, the Court ruled that, upon arrest, a suspect must be advised of his or her right to remain silent and the right to consult with a lawyer.

Malcolm X, a minister of the Nation of Islam, advocated a more aggressive approach.
The SNCC and CORE advocated the more separatist, radical program of Black Power.

---The New Left, Feminism, and the CounterCulture
In 1962 the Students for a Democratic Society (SDS) formed.
The Port Huron Statement
The protest in U.C.Berkeley grew into the Free Speech movement.

In 1963, Betty Friedan’s book The Feminine Mystique challenged many people’s assumptions about women’s place in society.
In 1966 the National Organization of Women (NOW) formed to fight for legislative changes, including the ill-fated Equal Rights Amendment (ERA) to the Constitution.
In 1971 case Roe v. Wade reached the Supreme Court and decriminalized abortion.

Hippies grew their hair long, wore tie-dyed shirts and ripped jeans, and advocated drug use, communal living, and “free love.”  ---counterculture

---American Involvement in Vietnam, 1950-1963
The foreign exploitation of Vietnam helped foster a nationalist Vietnamese resistance called the Vietminh, led by Ho Chi Minh.
The U.S. recognized the government of Bao Dai, the Vietnamese emperor whom the French had installed in the south.
In 1954 all the involved parties met in Geneva and agreed to Vietnamese elections in 1956.
The U.S. made an alliance with another South Vietnamese leader named Ngo Dinh Diem and helped him oust Bao Dai.
The U.S. rallied other countries to form the NATO-like SEATO (Southeast Asian Treaty Organization) to provide for South Vietnam’s defense against Communist takeover.
The South Vietnamese insurgents were called the Vietcong.
In 1963, the CIA helped the South Vietnamese military stage a coup to overthrow Diem’s government.

---American Involvement in Vietnam, 1964-1968
In 1964 the United States supported a second coup in South Vietnam.
The U.S.Army also started bombing the neighboring country of Laos.
Congress passed the Tonkin Gulf resolution.
The Americanization of the Vietnam War
In 1968 the North Vietnamese launched the Tet Offensive

---The Summer of 1968 and the 1968 Election
In June, Robert Kennedy was assassinated.
The Republicans handed their nomination to former vice-president Richard Nixon at a strife-free convention.
George Wallace ran a segregationist third-party campaign.
Hubert Humphrey – democratic

---Nixon, the End of the Vietnam War, and Détente
He ordered bombing raids and ground troops into Cambodia.
American involvement in Vietnam dragged on into 1973, when Secretary of State Henry Kissinger completed negotiations for a peace treaty with the North Vietnamese.
In 1975 Saigon fell to the North Vietnamese Army, and Vietnam was united under Communist rule.
Congress passed the War Powers Resolution in 1973.

Nixon and Kissinger formulated détente, which called for countries to respect each other’s differences and cooperate more closely.
The Nixon Doctrine announced that the U.S. would withdraw from many of its overseas troop commitments, relying instead on alliances with local governments to check the spread of Communism.

---Nixon’s Domestic Policy
During Nixon’s presidency, the economy worsened, going through stagflation.
He tried price-and-wage freeze and increased federal spending, but none produced their intended results.
National guardsmen shot and killed four protesters at Kent State University.
In 1972 Nixon won reelection, defeating liberal senator George McGovern.
However, both houses of Congress remained under Democratic control.

---Watergate and Nixon’s Resignation
In the summer of 1971, two major newspapers published the Pentagon Papers.
Nixon put together a team of investigators called the Plumbers, who undertook disgraceful projects.
During the 1972 elections, the Plumbers sabotaged the campaigns of several Democratic hopefuls and then botched a burglary of Democratic headquarters in the Watergate Hotel.
A Senate hearing into the matter began in early 1973 and dragged on.
Rather than face impeachment, Nixon resigned in August 1974. Gerald Ford took office.

---Gerald Ford
Ford had been chosen to replace Nixon’s first vice-president, Spiro Agnew.
Ford’s pardon of Nixon brought the Watergate era to a close.
An oil embargo organized by Arab nations (under the leadership of OPEC) against the U.S. increased fuel prices, which in turn caused the price of almost everything else to rise.
In 1976, he was defeated by Democrat Jimmy Carter.

---Jimmy Carter
Stagflation
Carter tried to balance the federal budget, but failed.
Carter created a new, Cabinet-level government agency, the Department of Energy.
A Pennsylvania plant at Three Mile Island failed, releasing radioactive materials into the atmosphere.
Carter personally brokered a peace agreement between Israel and Egypt.
He invited the two leaders of Israel and Arab AND PERSONALLY BROKERED AN AGREEMENT BETWEEN THE TWO NATIONS.
He also concluded an arms agreement with the Soviets.
In Nicaragua, Carter first befriended the revolutionary Sandinista government and then turned against them as they allied themselves more closely with the U.S.S.R. and Cuba.

---The Eighties (1980 to 1992)
By the late 1970’s, many Americans had grown tired of the conflicts of the previous decade.
Reagan won the 1980 election by a landslide margin in the Electoral College.

---Supply-side Economics
He coupled reduction of corporate taxes with large-scale deregulation. He also lobbied Congress for an across-the-board tax cut for all Americans.

---Military Spending and Budget Deficits
Reagan sought to eliminate the federal deficit by decreasing the size of the federal government. ---New Federalism
Reagan suggested the states take complete responsibility for welfare programs currently funded at the national level.
He convinced Congress to greatly increase military spending. He funded research into a space-based missile shield system called the Strategic Defense Initiative, or SDI.
The federal budget deficit was escalated.

---Foreign Policy under Reagan
During the Reagan administration, the U.S. military led an international invasion of Grenada to topple a new Communist government there.
The Iran-contra affair
The Reagan administration sent marines to Lebanon as part of a United Nations peacekeeping force. A suicide bomb killed 240 servicemen.
American-Soviet relations were further helped when the Soviet Union chose reformer Mikhail Gorbachev to lead the country.

---the 1988 Election
George Bush received the Republican nomination in 19988. Democrat—Michael Dukakis.

---George Bush
His major foreign policy achievement concerned Iraq, which invaded the small oil-rich Kuwait in the summer of 1990.—the Gulf War
Bush also presided during the disintegration of the Soviet Union. Bush established friendly relations with the new Russian regime, led by Boris Yeltsin.
President Bush initiated a humanitarian mission in Somalia in his final days in office.
