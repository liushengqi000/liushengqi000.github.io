SUMMARY:  a quick note on a Friday. Interesting, often distressing Taiwan conference at the Heritage Foundation yesterday outlines current domestic political disarray, and consequent solipsism of most Taiwan decision-makers, politicians and opinion leaders.

The electoral process is being reorganized on largely Japanese-single district lines, with a 50% cut in the number of seats (that is, “jobs”); the entire DPP cabinet has been reshuffled; the President has fired his Premier, in the wake of the recent electoral disaster, in which DPP corruption and inefficiency was punished by the voters; and most analysts seem to take for granted that the old KMT will be back in power via the presidential election in 2008, so nothing much will happen between China and Taiwan until that result.

And in the meantime, Taiwan business interests continue the joint US-Japan-Korea colonization of the Mainland with implications for policy-makers on both sides of the Straits, and in Washington....not the least being, who owns whom?

The net is China’s Leadership is almost complacent on the subject of Taiwan, after weathering the mini-crisis caused by its “Anti-Secession Law” of a year ago. As long as the US is committed to maintaining the “status quo”, but focusing its China policies on “larger” issues like Iran, N. Korea et al, China feels it can continue to deploy missiles along the coast, and to ignore US pleas to meet with the elected Taiwan DPP government.

China sees how the Bush Administration has gone out of its way to shut-down Taiwan independence moves, it sees the increasing business cross-straits, and with the US...and it’s very focused PLA military/missile buildup is now seen as sufficient to achieve its military goals vis a vis Taiwan IF Beijing were willing to put its economic and strategic interests at risk.

Please stress that, so long as the US maintains a credible defense umbrella...another way of saying a credible diplomatic commitment...no one thinks China would risk ALL of its other interests with adventurism in the Straits.  But if the US support appears to be wavering...or, even more risky, China mistakenly thinks so...well, that’s the constant downside of “creative ambiguity”.

Anyhow, for now, observers able to see beyond the missile-count school of threat analysis sound reasonably confident in predicting that so long as two factors remain constant, peace will be maintained: first, China remains dependent upon globalization to underwrite its rise to modernity, a process which will take at least the next generation, perhaps two; second, so long as the US is prepared to keep Taiwan from overt independence moves.

So...as long as you are not personally interested in Taiwan’s diplomatic and political place in the world, there’s nothing to worry about, right?

Not exactly.  First, you can never be entirely sure that a sudden accident or miscalculation, on either side of the Straits, might not prompt a military crisis that could get out of hand, and which would almost certainly face the US with some “difficult” decisions.  That’s the reason why most serious Asia analysts remain very worried about the cross-straits situation.

Second, strictly from the bullet-count school of threat assessment, it sounds like Taiwan may already have become un-defensible by conventional means, so long as you ARE willing to postulate that China is willing to set its economic and strategic interests at risk.

Specifically, it sounds like the 800+ Chinese missiles now deployed, with more on the way, are already sufficient to overwhelm all current Taiwan defense capabilities in short order. Worse, it sounds like nothing the US could provide, even if Taiwan politicians were willing to bite the bullet (sorry!) and pay for them, could be deployed in time to stop a full-scope missile attack (the coastal missiles, followed by MRBM’s we have no defense against now, and thousands of cruise missiles...with the PLA air force right behind).

And finally, there is no indication that Taiwanese political decision-makers have a clue that this is the current situation, nor does there seem to be any awareness in either Taipei (and perhaps some circles in Washington?) that in the unlikely event China did decide to launch an all-out attack on Taiwan...and please remember, this is NOT considered likely...then the president of the US would immediately be faced with a horrifying set of decisions:

“Do I authorize PACOM to attack the Mainland, specifically airfields, and the docks of Shanghai and other coastal cities? Do I authorize the Navy to seek and sink the PLA navy?  And since these would be an act of war against China, am I prepared to use our nuclear weapons to pre-empt nuclear retaliation against the US by China’s limited but deadly number of ICBM’s?”

In short, World War 3....the return of MAD.  Or...another set of calculations which are almost equally unattractive: “Do I abandon Taiwan to its fate, and thus put at risk the entire Asia alliance structure which we have spent the past 60 years putting in place, and which we now require to hedge against a Rising China which actively opposes our interests?”

Note that at least some PLA types have jibed...or is that threatened...that they doubt the US has the guts to “trade Los Angeles for Taipei”.  You cannot entirely discount that some future Chinese political leadership will make the same calculation...or mis-calculation. And none of us wants to find out the answer.

So that’s why the current, entirely inward-focused attention of the Taiwanese ruling elites is potentially so dangerous for all concerned.

While they know the numbers (President Chen talks about the 800 missiles) the implications of the strictly military facts of life do not seem to have been grasped by Taiwan elites, regardless of their assessment of Beijing’s economic and diplomatic agenda. It sounds like Taiwan decision makers still think that to maintain a peaceful status-quo, they don’t “really” have to buy the big US weapons package (stalled since 2001) since they still assume the US military will be nearly instantly available to defend them in the event of a full-scale Chinese attack, and that this is sufficient to deter such an event.

Well, that’s probably true, in a literal sense, since the weapons package itself has become obsolete, and was always too expensive;  and it’s also the most logical analysis, since China’s leadership, at least, has shown itself to be nothing if not ruthlessly logical...from ITS point of view.

But you don’t have to be a historian to note that much of the same analysis of interests and mutual dependencies based on globalization were just as true in August, 1914 as they are today.

                                                                    -0-

NOTE: participants were Amb. Harvey Feldman, chairing Richard Bush, of Brookings, John Tkacik of Heritage, and Bates Gill of CSIS.  Good comments came from an audience including Amb. Jim Lilley, and DPP Washington Liaison Mike Fonte.
