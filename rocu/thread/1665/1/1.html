malapert \mal-uh-PERT\ adjective

: impudently bold : saucy

Example sentence:
I had never imagined that Phyllis could be so malapert as to interrupt the professor right in the middle of his lecture.

Did you know?
&quot;Malapert&quot; debuted in English in the 14th century, was a favorite of Shakespeare, and is still used sporadically today. The prefix &quot;mal-,&quot; meaning &quot;bad&quot; or &quot;badly&quot; and deriving from the Latin &quot;malus,&quot; is found in many English words, including &quot;malevolent&quot; and &quot;malefactor.&quot; The second half of &quot;malapert&quot; comes from the Middle English &quot;apert,&quot; meaning &quot;open&quot; or &quot;frank.&quot; &quot;Apert&quot; further derives from the Latin word &quot;apertus&quot; (&quot;open&quot;), which gave us our noun &quot;aperture&quot; (meaning &quot;an opening&quot;). Putting the two halves together gives us a word that describes someone or something that is open or honest in a bad way — that is, a way that is bold or rude. The noun &quot;malapert&quot; also exists, and means &quot;a bold or impudent person.&quot;



gruntle \GRUN-tul\ verb

: to put in a good humor

Example sentence:
&quot;He spoke with a certain what-is-it in his voice, and I could see that, if not actually disgruntled, he was far from being gruntled.&quot; (P.G. Wodehouse, The Code of the Woosters)

Did you know?
&quot;Gruntle&quot; is the result of a mistaken assumption about the verb &quot;disgruntle,&quot; which means &quot;to make ill-humored or discontented.&quot; The prefix &quot;dis-&quot; often means &quot;to do the opposite of,&quot; so people naturally assumed that in order to have a &quot;disgruntle&quot; there must be a &quot;gruntle&quot; with exactly the opposite meaning. But actually, &quot;dis-&quot; doesn&#39;t always work that way — in some rare cases it functions instead as an intensifier. &quot;Disgruntle&quot; developed from this intensifying sense of &quot;dis-&quot; plus &quot;gruntle,&quot; an old word meaning &quot;to grumble.&quot; &quot;Gruntle&quot; began to mean &quot;to make happy&quot; only in the 1920s, when it was assumed to be the antonym of &quot;disgruntle.&quot; By contrast, &quot;disgruntle&quot; has been around since 1682, and the original grumbling &quot;gruntle&quot; dates back to 1589.



reprobate \REP-ruh-bayt\ noun

1 : a person foreordained to damnation
*2 : a depraved person : scoundrel

Example sentence:
&quot;He was just an old reprobate who lived poor and died broke....&quot; (Richard Peck, A Long Way from Chicago)

Did you know?
These days, calling Uncle Fred a &quot;reprobate&quot; is hardly a condemnation to hellfire and brimstone, but the original reprobates of the 16th century were hardened sinners who had fallen from God&#39;s grace. By the 19th century, &quot;reprobate&quot; had acquired the milder, but still utterly condemnatory, sense of &quot;a depraved person.&quot; Gradually, though, the criticism implied by &quot;reprobate&quot; became touched with tolerance and even a bit of humor. It is now most likely to be used as it was in this August 1995 New Yorker magazine article about the death of musician Jerry Garcia: &quot;It was suddenly obvious that Garcia had become, against all odds, an American icon: by Thursday morning, the avuncular old reprobate had smuggled his way onto the front pages of newspapers around the world.&quot;



small beer \SMAWL-BEER\ noun

1 : weak or inferior beer
*2 : something of small importance : trivia

Example sentence:
The player was fined $10,000 by the league for his comments about the opposing pitcher, but that&#39;s small beer when you consider his $15 million salary.

Did you know?
&quot;Small beer&quot; dates from Shakespeare&#39;s day. The Bard didn&#39;t coin it (he would have been just a child in 1568, the date of the first documented instance of &quot;small beer&quot;), but he did put the term to good use. In Henry VI, Part 2, for example, the rebel Jack Cade declares that, when he becomes king, he will &quot;make it felony to drink small beer.&quot; In Othello, Desdemona asks Iago to describe a &quot;deserving woman.&quot; Iago responds by listing praises for ten lines, only to conclude that such a woman would be suited &quot;to suckle fools, and chronicle small beer&quot;; in other words, to raise babies and keep track of insignificant household expenses. Desdemona quickly retorts, declaring Iago&#39;s assertion a &quot;most lame and impotent conclusion.&quot;



chivy \CHIV-ee\ verb

1 : to tease or annoy with persistent petty attacks
*2 : to move or obtain by small maneuvers

Example sentence:
As she told Brendan about her bad day, Megan chivied the last olive out of the jar and plopped it into her dry martini.

Did you know?
&quot;Chivy,&quot; which is also spelled &quot;chivvy,&quot; became established in our language in the early 20th century and at first meant &quot;to harass or chase.&quot; Early usage examples are of people chivying a chicken around to catch it and of a person chivying around food that is frying. The word itself is from the British noun of the same spelling meaning &quot;chase&quot; or &quot;hunt.&quot; The noun is believed to be derived from &quot;Chevy Chase&quot; — a term for &quot;chase&quot; or &quot;confusion&quot; that is taken from the name of a ballad describing the 1388 battle of Otterburn between the Scottish and English. (A &quot;chase&quot; in this context is an unenclosed tract of land in England that is used as a game preserve.)

plangent \\PLAN-junt\\ adjective

1 : having a loud reverberating sound
*2 : having an expressive and especially plaintive quality

Example sentence:
The plangent strains of a fiddle emanated from somewhere deep within the faceless gray stone building.

Did you know?
&quotlangent&quot; adds power to our poetry and prose: the pounding of waves, the beat of wings, the tolling of a bell, the throbbing of the human heart, a lover&#39;s knocking at the door — all have been described as plangent. The word &quot;plangent&quot; traces back to the Latin verb &quot;plangere,&quot; which has two meanings. The first of those meanings, &quot;to strike or beat,&quot; was sometimes used by Latin speakers in reference to striking one&#39;s breast in grief. This, in turn, led to the verb&#39;s second meaning: &quot;to lament.&quot; The sense division carried over to the Latin adjective &quot;plangens&quot; and then into English, giving us the two distinct meanings of &quot;plangent&quot;: &quot;pounding&quot; and &quot;expressive of melancholy.&quot;

execrate \\EK-suh-krayt\\ verb

*1 : to declare to be evil or detestable : denounce
2 : to detest utterly

Example sentence:
The new governor publicly execrated corruption in all its forms, and promised that her administration would be beyond reproach.

Did you know?
To Latinists, there&#39;s nothing cryptic about the origins of &quot;execrate&quot; — the word derives from &quot;exsecratus,&quot; the past participle of the Latin verb &quot;exsecrari,&quot; meaning &quot;to put under a curse.&quot; &quot;Exsecrari&quot; was itself created by combining the prefix &quot;ex-&quot; (&quot;not&quot;) and the word &quot;sacer&quot; (&quot;sacred&quot;). &quot;Sacer&quot; is also an ancestor of such English words as &quot;sacerdotal&quot; (&quot;relating to priests&quot;), &quot;sacral&quot; (&quot;holy, sacred&quot;), &quot;sacrifice,&quot; &quot;sacrilege,&quot; and of course &quot;sacred&quot; itself. There&#39;s also &quot;execration,&quot; which, true to its &quot;exsecrari&quot; roots, means &quot;the act of cursing&quot; or &quot;the curse so uttered.&quot;

inselberg \\IN-sul-berg\\ noun

: an isolated mountain

Example sentence:
Briana tied her hiking boots, adjusted her pack, and looked out across the distance at an inselberg rising abruptly from the flat plain surrounding it.

Did you know?
&quot;Inselberg,&quot; which first appeared in English in 1913, comes from the German words &quot;Insel,&quot; meaning &quot;island,&quot; and &quot;Berg,&quot; meaning &quot;mountain,&quot; apparently because German explorers thought isolated mountains rising from the plains of southern Africa looked like islands in the midst of the ocean. Geologically speaking, an inselberg is a hill of hard volcanic rock, such as granite, that has resisted wind and weather and remained strong and tall as the land around it eroded away. Ayers Rock and Olga Rocks in central Australia are two spectacular examples of inselbergs. The word &quot;monadnock,&quot; derived from the name of Mount Monadnock in New Hampshire, is a synonym of &quot;inselberg.&quot;

zoomorphic \\zoh-uh-MOR-fik\\ adjective

*1 : having the form of an animal
2 : of, relating to, or being a deity conceived of in animal form or with animal attributes

Example sentence:
A number of zoomorphic constellations are present in the zodiac, including Aries the Ram, Capricorn the Goat, and Taurus the Bull.

Did you know?
The combining form &quot;zo-,&quot; or &quot;zoo-,&quot; derives from the Greek word &quot;zōion,&quot; meaning &quot;animal,&quot; and &quot;-morphic&quot; descends from the Greek &quot;morphē,&quot; which means &quot;form.&quot; We&#39;re sure you know other English words that come from &quot;zōion,&quot; such as &quot;zoo,&quot; &quot;zoology,&quot; and &quot;zoological.&quot; Other descendants of &quot;morphē&quot; exist in English as well, including &quot;anthropomorphic,&quot; which means &quot;having human form,&quot; and &quot;metamorphosis,&quot; which refers to a change in form, structure, or substance caused especially by supernatural means. Combining these two forms gives us the adjective &quot;zoomorphic,&quot; which first appeared in English in 1872 and is used to describe something that resembles an animal in form.

laissez-faire \\leh-say-FAIR\\ noun

*1 : a doctrine opposing governmental interference in economic affairs beyond the minimum necessary for the maintenance of peace and property rights
2 : a philosophy or practice characterized by a usually deliberate abstention from direction or interference especially with individual freedom of choice and action

Example sentence:
Calvin Coolidge, our 30th president, was a firm believer in small government and the principles of laissez-faire.

Did you know?
The French phrase &quot;laissez-faire&quot; literally means &quot;allow to do,&quot; with the idea being &quot;let people do as they choose,&quot; or simply &quot;leave things alone.&quot; The origins of &quot;laissez-faire&quot; are associated with the Physiocrats, a group of 18th-century French economists who believed that government policy should not interfere with the operation of natural economic laws. The actual coiner of the phrase may have been French economist Vincent de Gournay, or it may have been Fran&ccedil;ois Quesnay, considered the group&#39;s founder and leader. The original phrase was &quot;laissez faire, laissez passer,&quot; with the second part meaning &quot;let (things) pass.&quot; &quot;Laissez-faire,&quot; which first showed up in an English context in 1825, is still most often a term of economics, but it is also used in broader contexts in which a &quot;hands-off&quot; or &quot;anything-goes&quot; policy or attitude is adopted.

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.




crabwise \\KRAB-wyze\\ adjective

1 : sideways
*2 : in a sidling or cautiously indirect manner

Example sentence:
Rather than asking his parents for a car directly, Noah approached the matter crabwise, stressing how inconvenient it was for them to have to drive him everywhere.

Did you know?
There&#39;s no reason to be indirect when explaining the etymology of &quot;crabwise&quot;; we&#39;ll get right to the point. As you might guess, the meaning of that word is directly related to that sidling sea creature, the crab. If you live near the shore or have visited a beach near the sea, you have probably seen crabs scuttling along, often moving sideways and certainly not taking what humans would consider the most direct route. The modern meanings of &quot;crabwise&quot; were definitely inspired by the crab&#39;s lateral or oblique approach to getting from one place to another. The word crept into English in the mid-19th century and has been sidling into our sentences ever since.

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.



vapor \\VAY-per\\ verb

1 a : to rise or pass off in vapor b : to emit vapor
*2 : to indulge in bragging, blustering, or idle talk

Example sentence:
Team members vapored all week about how they were going to steamroller the opposing team, but come crunch time they failed to produce.

Did you know?
Speakers of the English language, mindful of the lightness and unsubstantiality of floating air and gas, have put several airy words to good use over the years to describe the act of talking idly or boastfully. The earliest such word is &quot;blow&quot; (as in &quot;he kept blowing about his new job&quot;), which drifted into English sometime about 1400. &quot;Vapor&quot; wafted into the language in 1628, and a little over 200 years later &quot;windbags,&quot; later also known as &quot;gasbags,&quot; not only blew and vapored but also &quot;gassed&quot; about anything they could.

baleful \\BAIL-ful\\ adjective

1 : deadly or pernicious in influence
*2 : foreboding or threatening evil

Example sentence:
&quot;What are you doing here?&quot; I heard someone hiss, and I whirled around to see Sandra giving me a baleful glare.

Did you know?
The &quot;bale&quot; of &quot;baleful&quot; comes from the Old English &quot;bealu&quot; (&quot;evil&quot;), and the &quot;bane&quot; of the similar-looking &quot;baneful&quot; comes from the Old English &quot;bana&quot; (&quot;slayer, murderer&quot;). &quot;Baleful&quot; and &quot;baneful&quot; are alike in meaning as well as appearance, and they are sometimes used in quite similar contexts — but they usually differ in emphasis. &quot;Baleful&quot; typically describes what threatens or portends evil (e.g., &quot;a baleful look,&quot; &quot;baleful predictions&quot;). &quot;Baneful&quot; applies typically to what causes evil or destruction (e.g., &quot;a baneful secret,&quot; &quot;the baneful bite of the serpent&quot;). Both words are used to modify terms like &quot;influence,&quot; &quot;effect,&quot; and &quot;result,&quot; and in such uses there is little that distinguishes them.

judgment \\JUJ-munt\\ noun

1 a : a formal utterance of an authoritative opinion
*2 : the capacity for judging or the exercise of this capacity

Example sentence:
Theresa showed good judgment by clearing her family out of the house as soon as she smelled gas.

Did you know?
&quot;Judgment&quot; can also be spelled &quot;judgement,&quot; and usage experts have long disagreed over which spelling is the preferred one. Henry Fowler asserted, &quot;The OED [Oxford English Dictionary] prefers the older and more reasonable spelling. &#39;Judgement&#39; is therefore here recommended.&quot; William Safire held an opposite opinion, writing, &quot;My judgment is that Fowler is not to be followed.&quot; &quot;Judgement&quot; is in fact the older spelling, but it dropped from favor and for centuries &quot;judgment&quot; was the only spelling to appear in dictionaries. That changed when the OED (Fowler&#39;s source) was published showing &quot;judgement&quot; as an equal variant. Today, both spellings of the word are common. &quot;Judgment&quot; appears to be more popular in the U.S., whereas &quot;judgement&quot; is slightly more frequent in Britain.

vanilla \\vuh-NILL-uh\\ adjective

1 : flavored with vanilla
*2 : lacking distinction : plain, ordinary, conventional

Example sentence:
&quot;The Razorbacks [football team] backed off the blitz and played vanilla defense in the second half....&quot; (Scott Cain, Arkansas Democrat-Gazette, November 16, 2003)

Did you know?
For lexicographers, &quot;vanilla&quot; has more flavor than &quot;chocolate,&quot; because it adds a tasty synonym for &quot;plain&quot; to the English menu. The noun &quot;vanilla&quot; was first served up in 1662, but it took almost 200 years for its adjective use to become established for things, like ice and sugar, flavored with vanilla. By the 1970s vanilla was perceived as being the plain flavor of the ice-cream world, and people began using the word itself to describe anything plain, ordinary, or conventional.
