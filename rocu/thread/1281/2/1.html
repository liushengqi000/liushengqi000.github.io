REPAIR MISPLACED AND DANGLING MODIFIERS

REPAIR MISPLACED AND DANGLING MODIFIERS
1. put limiting modifiers in front of the words they modify.
Only, even, almost, nearly, just should be placed right in front of the word they modify.

# Lasers only destroy the target, leaving the surrounding healthy tissue intact.
REVISION: Lasers destroy only ...

2. place phrases and clauses so that readers can see at a glance what they modify
# The robber was described as a six-foot-tall man with a heavy mustache weighing 150 pounds.
REVISION: The robber was described as a 150-pound, six-foot-tall man ...

3. move awkwardly placed modifiers
Awkward when: a long adverbial element separates a subject from its verb, a verb from its object, or a helping verb from its main verb, the result is usually awkward. (exception: author want to create suspense.)
# Hong Kong, after more than 150 years of British rule, was transferred back to Chinese control in 1997.
REVISION: After more than 150 years of British rule, Hong Kong was ...

English does not allow an adverb to appear between a verb and its object.

4. avoid split infinitives when they are awkward

5.Repair Dangling Modifiers
Recognizing dangling modifiers: when a sentence opens with such a modifier, readers expect the subject of the next clause to name the actor.

Eliminate distracting shifts.

Eliminate distracting shifts.
1. Make the point of view consistent in person and number.
The I point of view, emphasize the writer, good for informal letters and writing based primarily on personal experience.
You point of view, emphasize the reader, works well for giving advice or explaining how to do something.
Third person point of view, emphasize the subject, appropriate in formal academic and professional writing.
. Eliminate distracting shifts.
1. Make the point of view consistent in person and number.
The I point of view, emphasize the writer, good for informal letters and writing based primarily on personal experience.
You point of view, emphasize the reader, works well for giving advice or explaining how to do something.
Third person point of view, emphasize the subject, appropriate in formal academic and professional writing.

2. Maintain consistent verb tenses.

3. Make verbs consistent in mood and voice.
Indicative: used for facts, opinions, and questions
Imperative: used for orders or advice
Subjunctive: used in certain contexts to express wishes or conditions contrary to fact.

# When the tickets are ready, the travel agent notifies the client. Each ticket is then listed on a daily register form, and a copy of the itinerary is filed.
REVISED: When the tickets are ready, the travel agent notifies the client, lists each ticket on a daily register form, and files a copy of the itinerary.

4. Avoid sudden shifts from indirect to direct questions or quotations.

# I wonder whether the sister knew of the theft and, if so, did she report it to the police.
I wonder whether the sister knew of the theft and, if so, whether she reported it to the police.

ADVICE:
CAUSE: You are writing about events in a literary work, and you’re not sure whether to use the present or the past tense.
CURE: Choose the present tense, since that is the literary convention.

# After the count of three, Mikah and I placed the injured woman on the scoop stretcher. Then her vital signs were taken by Mikah.
ADVICE: After the count of three, Mikah and I placed the injured woman on the scoop stretcher. Then Mikah took her vital signs.

# The question is whether ferrets bred in captivity have the instinct to prey on prairie dogs or is their ability a learned skill.
REVISED: The question is whether ferrets bred in captivity have the instinct to prey on prairie dogs or whether their ability is a learned skill.

Emphasize key ideas

Emphasize key ideas
1. coordinate equal ideas; subordinate minor ideas.
Coordination
(1) To coordinate independent clauses, join them with a comma and a coordinating conjunction or with a semicolon: and, but, or, nor, for, so, yet.
(2) The semicolon is often accompanied by a conjunctive adverb such as moreover, furthermore, therefore, however or by a transitional phrase such as for example, in other words, or as a matter of fact.
