Wordy sentences

Wordy sentences
Tighten wordy sentences
1.       Eliminate redundancies
Eg. Daniel is now employed at a private rehabilitation复职 center working as a registered physical therapist临床医学家.
Daniel works at a private rehabilitation center as a registered physical therapist.

2.       avoid unnecessary repetition of words
eg. The best teachers help each student to become a better student both academically and emotionally.
The best teachers help each student to grow both academically and emotionally.

3.       cut empty or inflated phrases
such as: in my opinion, I think that, it seems that, one must admit that

4.       simplify the structure
eg. The secretary is responsible for monitoring and balancing the budgets for travel and personnel.
The secretary monitors and balances the budgets for travel and personnel.

Eg. There is another module that tells the story of Charles Darwin.
Another modul tells the story of Charles.

Eg. It is imperative that all police officers follow strict procedures.
All police officers must follow strict procedures.

Also, avoid passive voice.

5.       reduce clauses to phrases, phrases to single words
eg. We gave Jess a stylish vest, made of silk.
We gave Jess a stylish silk vest.

Choose appropriate language

Choose appropriate language
1.       stay away from jargon
eg. For years the indigenous body politic of South Africa attempted to negotiate legal enfranchisement without result.
For years the indigenous people of South Africa negotiated in vain for the right to vote.

2.       avoid pretentious language, most euphemisms, and “doublespeak”
eg. When our progenitors（祖先） reach their silver-haired and golden years, we frequently ensepulcher them in homes for senescent（开始衰老的） beings as if they were already among the deceased.
When our parents become old, we frequently entomb（埋葬） them in old-age homes as if they were already among the dead.

Some euphemisms are proper.
Death: her sister passed on.
Excretion: I have to go to the bathroom.
Sexual intercourse: they did not sleep together until they were married.

But most euphermisms are needlessly evasive or even deceitful.

Avoid using utilize, finalize, facilitate, and effectuate.

3.       avoid obsolete and invented words(neologism)
neologism: palimony, technobabble
not neologism: printout, flextime

4.       in most contexts, avoid slang, regional expressions, and nonstandard English
slang: cool, groovy, neat, awesome, phat, money.

5.       choose an appropriate level of formality

6.       avoid sexist language
sexist language: call a woman as a broad, lady lawyer
It’s sexist to call a woman a girl or a doctor a woman doctor.

7. revise language that may offend groups of people.
Not to use polack, redneck.

Find the exact words

Find the exact words.
a. Select words with appropriate connatiations.
In addition to their strict dictionary meanings (denotations), words have connotations.

b. Prefer specific, concrete nouns.
No general nouns, abstract nouns.
Specific, concrete nouns express meaning more vividly than general or abstract ones.
Nouns such as thing, area, aspect, factor, and individual are especially dull and imprecise.

c. Do not misuse words.
*Mrs. Johnson tried to fight but to no prevail.
Mrs. Johnson tried to fight but to no avail.

d. Use standard idioms.
Abide by
According to
Agree with
Angry with
Capable of
Comply with
Desirous of
Different from
Intend to do
Plan to do
Off
Preferable to
Prior to
Superior to
Sure to
Try to
Type of

e. Do not rely heavily on cliches.
Cool as a cucumber
Beat around the bush
Blind as a bat
Busy as a bee/beaver
Crystal clear
Dead as a doornail
Out of the frying pan and into the fire
Light as a feather
Like a bull in a china shop
Playing with fire
Nutty as a fruitcake
Selling like hotcakes
Starting out at the bottom of the ladder
Water under the bridge
White as a sheet/ghost
Avoid cliches like the plague

f. Use figures of speech with care
In a simile, the writer makes the comparison explicitly.
In a metaphor, the like or as is omitted, and the comparison is implied.
Be careful of mixed metaphor

Be Alert to Special Problems with Verbs.

Be Alert to Special Problems with Verbs.
a. Match helping verbs and main verbs appropiately.
1)       review of helping verbs and main verbs
each verb has 5 forms: base form, past tense, past participle, present participle, -s form
Modals: can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would, ought to

2)       modal + base form
3)       do, does, or did + base form
4)       have, has or had + past participle
5)       form of be + present participle
6)       form of be + past participle (passive voice)
no passive form: occur, happen, sleep, die, fall.

b. in conditional sentences, choose verbs with care.
1)       Factual
2)       Predictive
If/unless sb. do, sth. will/can/may/should/might.
3)       Speculative

c. become familiar with verbs that may be followed by gerunds or infinitives.
1)       verb + gerund or infinitive
begin, continue, like, start, can’t stand, hate, love.

2)       verb + gerund
admit, appreciate, avoid, deny, discuss, enjoy, escape, finish, imagine, miss, postpone, practice, put off, quit, recall, resist, risk, suggest, tolerate.

3)       verb + infinitive
agree, ask, beg, claim, decide, expect, have, hope, manage, mean, offer, plan, pretend, promise, refuse, wait, want, wish.

4)       verb + noun or pronoun + infinitive
advise, allow, cause, command, convince, encourage, have, instruct, order, persuade, remind, require, tell, urge, warn.

5)       verb + noun or pronoun + unmarked infinitive
have (meaning cause), let, make

d. become familiar with commonly used two-word verbs.

e. do not omit needed verbs.

Use the Articles a, an, and the Appropriately

Use the Articles a, an, and the Appropriately.
a. use a/an with singular count nouns whose specific identity is not known to the reader.

b. Do not use a/an with noncount nouns
1)       food and drink
bacon, beef, bread, broccoli, cabbage, candy, cauliflower, celery, cereal, cheese, chicken, corn, fish, fruit, lettuce, meat, pasta, rice, salt, spinach, sugar, wine, yogurt.

2)       nonfood substances
cement, coal, dirt, gasoline, gold, petroleum, plastic, silver, soap, steel, wood, wool

3)       abstract nouns
anger, beauty, courage, employment, knowledge, love, poverty, truth

4)       other
areas of study (eg. Biology), clothing, equipment, jewelry, luggage, lumber, machinery, mail, news, poetry, pollution, research, scenery, traffic, transportation, violence, weather

5)       unit
an ear of corn, a head of cabbage/lettuce.

c. use the with most nouns whose specific identity is known to the reader.
The sun.

d. do not use the with plural or noncount nouns meaning “all” or “in general”; do not use the with most singular prper nouns.
*The fountains …
Fountains …
* The rice …
Rice …

Not used with most singular proper nouns, eg. Judge Hennessey, Spring Street, Lake Huron.
Used with plural proper nouns, eg. The United Nations, the Bahamas, the Finger Lakes.
1)       when to omit the:
streets, squares, parks
cities, states, counties, most countries, continents
bays, single lakes
single mountains, islands

2)       when to use the:
united countries
large regions, deserts
oceans, seas, gulfs, canals, rivers
mountain ranges
goups of islands, peninsulas

Other Trouble Spots.

Other Trouble Spots.
a. Do not omit subjects or the expletive there or it.

b. Do not repeat the subject of a sentence.

c. Do not repeat an object or an adverb in an adjective clause.

d. Place adjectives and adverbs with care.
1)       placement of adjectives
use no more than two or three of adjectives before the noun modified.
Order: article or other noun marker
           Evaluative word: attractive, dedicated, delicious, ugly
           Size
           Length or shape
           Age: new, old, yuoung, antique
           Color
           Nationality
           Religion
           Material
           Noun/adjective
           The noun modified

2)       placement of adverbs
An adverb may not be placed between a verb and its direct object.

e. Distinguish between present participles and past participles used as adjectives.

f. Become familiar with common prepositions that show time and place.
1)       time: at a specific time
               on a specific day or date
               in a part of a day, in a year or month, in a period of time
               by a specific time or date
2)       place: at a meeting place or location, at the edge of something, at the corner of something (at the intersection), at a target
                on a surface, on an electronic medium (on tv, on the Internet)
                in an enclosed space, in a geographic location, in a print medium (in a book)
                by a landmark (by the fence, by the flagpole)
3)       exception
ride in a car, on a bus, fly on a plane
