The Word of the Day for December 14 is:

lagniappe \\LAN-yap\\ noun

: a small gift given a customer by a merchant at the time of a purchase; broadly : something given or obtained gratuitously or by way of good measure

Example sentence:
The Garcia family&#39;s store always has the best holiday-themed lagniappes; this year with a $20 purchase you receive a hand-painted snowman figurine.

Did you know?
&quot;We picked up one excellent word,&quot; wrote Mark Twain in Life on the Mississippi (1883), &quot;a word worth traveling to New Orleans to get; a nice limber, expressive, handy word — &#39;lagniappe&#39;.... It is Spanish — so they said.&quot; Twain encapsulates the history of &quot;lagniappe&quot; quite nicely. English speakers learned the word from French-speaking Louisianians, but they in turn had adapted it from the American Spanish word &quot;la &ntilde;apa.&quot; Twain went on to describe how New Orleanians completed shop transactions by saying &quot;Give me something for lagniappe,&quot; to which the shopkeeper would respond with &quot;a bit of liquorice-root, ... a cheap cigar or a spool of thread.&quot; It took a while for &quot;lagniappe&quot; to catch on throughout the country, but by the mid-20th century, New Yorkers and New Orleanians alike were familiar with this &quot;excellent word.&quot;

The Word of the Day for December 15 is:

forfend \\for-FEND\\ verb

*1 : to ward off : prevent
2 : protect, preserve

Example sentence:
My roommate claims that the best way to forfend a nasty cold is to chew garlic.

Did you know?
&quot;Heaven forfend if you don&#39;t treat the restaurant critic well—she&#39;ll cost you points if she leaves unhappy,&quot; wrote Peter Cohen in an October 2005 issue of Macworld, using an old meaning of &quot;forfend&quot; in the process. English speakers have been using &quot;forfend&quot; with the meanings &quot;to forbid&quot; and &quot;to prevent&quot; since the late 14th century (and the meaning &quot;to protect&quot; since the late 16th century). These days, however, the &quot;forbid&quot; sense is considered archaic; we only use it, as Cohen did, in the phrase &quot;heaven forfend,&quot; which harks back to the days of yore. &quot;Forfend&quot; comes from &quot;for-&quot; (an old prefix meaning &quot;so as to involve prohibition, exclusion, omission, failure, neglect, or refusal&quot;) and Middle English &quot;fenden&quot; (a shorter variant of &quot;defenden,&quot; meaning &quot;to defend&quot;).

*Indicates the sense illustrated in the example sentence.

ailurophile \\eye-LOOR-uh-fyle\\ noun

: a cat fancier : a lover of cats

Example sentence:
Bill, the office ailurophile, could often be heard talking about his cats, Smokey, Twinkle, and Lucy, as if they were his children.

Did you know?
Although the word &quot;ailurophile&quot; has only been documented in English since the 1920s, ailurophiles have been around for thousands of years. The ancient Egyptians were perhaps history&#39;s greatest cat lovers, pampering and adorning felines, honoring them in art, even treating them as gods. But the English word &quot;ailurophile&quot; does not descend from Egyptian; rather, it comes from a combination of the Greek word &quot;ailouros,&quot; which means &quot;cat,&quot; and the suffix &quot;-phile,&quot; meaning &quot;lover.&quot; If Egyptian cat-loving sentiments leave you cold and you&#39;re more sympathetic to medieval Europeans, who regarded cats as wicked agents of evil, you might prefer the word &quot;ailurophobe&quot; (from &quot;ailouros&quot; plus &quot;-phobe,&quot; meaning &quot;fearing or averse to&quot;).

The Word of the Day for December 17 is:

seasonal affective disorder \\SEE-zun-ul-a-FEK-tiv-dis-OR-der\\ noun

: depression that tends to recur as the days grow shorter during the fall and winter

Example sentence:
Call it seasonal affective disorder,
Call it the winter blues —
But what ever you call it,
Don&#39;t let it get the better of you.

Did you know?
&quot;Seasonal affective disorder&quot; hasn&#39;t been recognized as a medical condition for very long, and the term has only become part of the general English vocabulary during the past two decades or so (its earliest documented appearance in print dates from 1983). &quot;Seasonal affective disorder&quot; (abbreviated SAD) is also sometimes called &quot;Winter Depression,&quot; and some researchers describe it as a &quot;hibernation reaction&quot; in which sensitive individuals react to the decreasing amounts of light and the colder temperatures of fall and winter. The term &quot;seasonal affective disorder&quot; is sometimes used casually of the mild blahs that so many of us experience when the days grow short, but true SAD actually goes beyond the poetic &quot;winter blues&quot; — it is a diagnosable form of depression that can be quite debilitating.

The Word of the Day for December 19 is:

circumscribe \\SER-kum-skrybe\\ verb

1 a : to constrict the range or activity of definitely and clearly b : to define or mark off carefully
2 a : to draw a line around *b : to surround by or as if by a boundary

Example sentence:
Horses grazed in a paddock circumscribed by a lovely white picket fence.

Did you know?
&quot;Circumscribe&quot; has a lot of relatives in English. Its Latin predecessor &quot;circumscribere&quot; (which roughly translates as &quot;to draw a circle around&quot;) derives from &quot;circum-,&quot; meaning &quot;circle,&quot; and &quot;scribere,&quot; meaning &quot;to write or draw.&quot; Among the many descendents of &quot;circum-&quot; are &quot;circuit,&quot; &quot;circumcise,&quot; &quot;circumference,&quot; &quot;circumnavigate,&quot; &quot;circumspect,&quot; &quot;circumstance,&quot; and &quot;circumvent.&quot; &quot;Scribere&quot; gave us such words as &quot;scribe&quot; and &quot;scribble,&quot; as well as &quot;ascribe,&quot; &quot;describe,&quot; and &quot;transcribe,&quot; among others. &quot;Circumscribe&quot; first appeared in print in the 14th century; it was originally spelled &quot;circumscrive,&quot; but the &quot;circumscribe&quot; spelling had also appeared by the end of the century.

The Word of the Day for December 20 is:

sequacious \\sih-KWAY-shus\\ adjective

: intellectually servile

Example sentence:
Ronald was disappointed that his students presented only sequacious arguments in their term papers and that few offered any original ideas.

Did you know?
&quot;Sequacious&quot; is formed from the Latin &quot;sequac-,&quot; or &quot;sequax,&quot; (which means &quot;inclined to follow&quot; and comes from &quot;sequi,&quot; &quot;to follow&quot;) and the English &quot;-ious.&quot; The original and now archaic meaning of &quot;sequacious&quot; was &quot;inclined to follow&quot; or &quot;subservient, tractable.&quot; Although that meaning might as easily describe someone who willingly dropped into line behind a war leader, or who was unusually compliant or obedient in any sense, the concept gradually narrowed into the image of someone who blindly adopts another&#39;s ideas without much thought. Labeling a person &quot;sequacious&quot; is not very complimentary, and implies a slavish willingness to adopt a thought or opinion. It is also possible to accuse someone of &quot;sequacity,&quot; but that would be equally unkind.

The Word of the Day for December 21 is:

hyperborean \\hye-per-BOR-ee-un\\ adjective

*1 : of or relating to an extreme northern region : frozen
2 : of or relating to any of the arctic peoples

Example sentence:
Pauline&#39;s hyperborean relatives (she still has cousins in Canada) don&#39;t know what to make of her preference for the tropics.

Did you know?
In ancient Greek mythology, the &quot;Hyperboreoi&quot; were a people who lived in a northern paradise of perpetual sunshine beyond the reaches of the god of the north wind. Their name combines the prefix &quot;hyper-,&quot; meaning &quot;above,&quot; and &quot;Boreas,&quot; the Greek name for the north wind. When &quot;hyperborean&quot; first appeared in the English language in the 15th century, it was a noun naming those legendary folk. Later, the noun was extended to actual inhabitants of northern climates, and the adjective came into use for anything related to the far north or the people who live there.
